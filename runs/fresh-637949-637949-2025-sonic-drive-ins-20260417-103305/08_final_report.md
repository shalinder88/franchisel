## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Franchisor**: Sonic Franchising LLC, a Delaware limited liability company formed March 23, 2011, headquartered at Three Glenlake Parkway NE, Atlanta, Georgia 30328. The Sonic brand began in the early 1950s in Oklahoma, and franchising commenced in 1974 through predecessors. The current franchisor entity was created as part of a 2011 securitization restructuring.

2. **Parent and Ownership**: Sonic Franchising LLC is ultimately owned by Inspire Brands, Inc., which is controlled by private equity funds managed by Roark Capital Management, LLC. The chain of ownership runs: Sonic Franchising LLC → Sonic Capital LLC → Sonic LLC → Sonic Holding Company → IRB Holding Corp. → Inspire Brands, Inc. Inspire is a global multi-brand restaurant company operating Arby's, Buffalo Wild Wings, Dunkin', Baskin-Robbins, Jimmy John's, and (since April 2024) Subway.

3. **System Size**: As of December 29, 2024, there were 3,461 Sonic Drive-Ins in the United States — 3,144 franchised and 317 company-owned. The system is the largest drive-in restaurant chain in the country.

4. **Growth Trajectory**: The system is in a sustained contraction phase. In 2024, the system shrank by 60 units (50 franchised, 10 company-owned). In 2023, it lost 25 units; in 2022, it lost 6. Franchised openings in 2024 were only 30 vs. 80 units that exited the system (12 terminations + 68 ceased operations). The ratio of closures to openings is approximately 2.7:1, indicating meaningful system-level attrition.

5. **Investment Range**: Traditional free-standing: $1,676,000–$3,140,900; C-Store: $669,200–$1,360,900; Drive-Thru Only: $1,609,200–$2,965,900. All figures assume leased premises and exclude real estate acquisition costs.

6. **Fee Burden**: Standard royalty 5% of Gross Sales; Sonic Brand Fund (SBF) 0.90%; System Marketing Fund (SMF) 1.625%–5% (market-dependent); Brand Technology Fund (BTF) 0.25%. Total ongoing fees range from approximately 6.4% to 11.15% of Gross Sales depending on market type and incentive program participation.

7. **Item 19 AUV**: $1,587,024 system-wide average (3,086 qualifying restaurants). Median $1,500,713. First-quartile average $935,705 — at these volumes, a franchisee paying 5% royalty plus ~5% marketing/technology fees would remit approximately $93,000 annually in franchise-level fees, before any operating expenses, debt service, or owner compensation.

8. **Financial Health**: The franchisor entity (Sonic Franchising LLC) is a thin entity with $36.2M in total assets, $26.4M member's equity, and $6.0M net income. It guarantees $1,417.3M in securitization notes. The operating entity (SIS) has $2.3B in assets including $822.6M goodwill and $1.1B in indefinite-lived trademark value, supported by $233.3M in operating income.

9. **Territory**: Protected Area is a population-density-based radius (0.75–3 miles), but it is explicitly "not exclusive" due to numerous carve-outs including previously existing restaurants, non-traditional locations, and the franchisor's broad reserved rights to operate under other brands or through alternative channels.

10. **Renewal Risk**: The franchise agreement is for 10 years with 2 additional 10-year renewal terms. Renewal requires signing the then-current franchise agreement, which "may contain materially different terms than in the Franchise Agreement, including different fees, different Protected Area, and/or Franchisee's rights in a protected area."

11. **Dispute Resolution**: Litigation only (no arbitration or mediation available) in the county of the franchisor's headquarters (currently Atlanta, Georgia). Georgia law governs. Several state addenda override this for franchisees in Maryland, Minnesota, and North Dakota.

12. **Gag Clause**: Present. "During the last 3 years, some franchisees have signed confidentiality agreements with us. In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with the Sonic Restaurant network."

13. **Special Risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution in Atlanta, Georgia, which may force less favorable settlements and higher litigation costs for non-Georgia franchisees.

14. **Transfer Spike**: 334 franchised restaurant transfers occurred in 2024, up dramatically from 75 in 2023 and 132 in 2022. Texas alone had 110 transfers. This 4.5× increase may signal large-scale portfolio consolidation, distress-driven sales, or both.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Sonic Franchising LLC operates through a management arrangement with Sonic Industries Services LLC (SIS), which actually performs franchisor obligations. The franchisor entity itself is a special-purpose entity within a securitization structure. ADIBP (America's Drive-In Brand Properties LLC) owns all Sonic intellectual property and licenses it to the franchisor for 99 years. The royalty flow is: franchisees pay royalty to Sonic Franchising LLC, which pays approximately 84% of its revenue ($49.4M of $59.0M) upstream as "royalty fees to Affiliates" (the ADIBP trademark license).

Three franchise formats are offered: Traditional Drive-In (the classic stall-and-canopy format), Drive-Thru Only (no stalls), and Non-Drive-In/C-Store (within existing convenience stores or travel plazas). Non-Traditional locations (airports, stadiums, etc.) are also possible under a Non-Traditional Rider. Multi-Brand locations combining Sonic with other Inspire brands (Arby's, BWW, Dunkin', Baskin-Robbins, Jimmy John's) require both franchise agreements and a Multi-Brand Addendum.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees**:
- Initial franchise fee: $15,000 (Traditional/Non-Drive-In); $11,250 (Non-Traditional); $5,000/$12,500 (reopened closed restaurant)
- Development fee: $10,000 per restaurant under Development Agreement (credited toward FA fee)
- Design fees: $1,200 PSL / $1,200 PKL (new) / $750 PKL (remodel) — optional
- Training fee: $200 per attendee (GM Leadership Class)

**Ongoing Fees**:
The ongoing fee structure for a Traditional Drive-In in a core market runs approximately:
- Royalty: 5.0% of Gross Sales (with NRO incentive: 1%→5% over 4 years)
- SBF: 0.90%
- SMF (via cooperative): 2.75%–4.5% of Gross Sales allocated to SMF + 0.5% to coop
- BTF: 0.25%
- Total: approximately 9.4%–10.65% of Gross Sales in core markets

In developing or new markets, the total can reach 11.15% when SMF/coop contributions are at 5%.

Additional event-driven fees include: transfer fee ($1,000–$3,000), renewal fee ($15,000), late charge (1.75%/month), audit fee and 10% surcharge (if ≥3% understatement), management fee (3% if franchisor manages pending option to purchase), insurance (actual cost if franchisee fails to maintain), and indemnification (as incurred).

**Incentive Programs**: The FDD describes six named incentive programs that can substantially reduce the fee burden for new restaurants:
1. **NRO (New Restaurant Opening) Incentive**: Royalty ramp from 1% to 5% over 4 years, plus 50% SMF discount for 5 years. Extended relief if Gross Sales <$1.4M at year 3.
2. **Relocation Incentive**: Royalty ramp from 1% to 5% over 3 years.
3. **Early Opening Incentive**: 0% royalty from opening until required opening date (up to 12 months).
4. **VetFran Program**: $10,000 royalty credit per restaurant, up to $100,000 for veterans.
5. **Pioneer Incentive**: $50,000 royalty credit for first 2 restaurants in DC, ME, NH, or VT.
6. **DTO, End Cap & Inline Incentive**: $50,000 cash payment per qualifying drive-thru-only restaurant.

### Initial Investment

**Traditional Free-Standing (Leased)**:
The total estimated initial investment ranges from $1,676,000 to $3,140,900, excluding real estate acquisition costs. The largest components are building costs ($800K–$1.1M) and site work ($450K–$1M), which together represent 75%–67% of the total. Restaurant equipment adds $175K–$260K, POS/POPS technology $105K–$160K, and external signage $50K–$150K. Pre-opening labor costs (training travel, certification team, payroll) range from $50K–$231K. The lot size required is 34,000–43,560 square feet for a 1,250–1,500 square foot building with canopies for 6–12 stalls, drive-thru lane, and patio.

**C-Store (Non-Drive-In, Leased)**:
$669,200–$1,360,900. Building costs drop to $250K–$500K and site work to $50K–$100K. Some franchisees own the C-Store building and pay no rent or security deposit.

**Drive-Thru Only (Leased)**:
$1,609,200–$2,965,900. Similar to Traditional except no stall infrastructure; lot size 24,000–32,670 square feet.

Neither the franchisor nor its affiliates offer financing for any part of the initial investment.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions and Approved Supplier Process
Franchisees must purchase food, beverages, signs, paper products, uniforms, POS, POPS, equipment, and technology from designated or approved suppliers. The franchisor controls supplier approval with "no formal procedure or written criteria" — it retains exclusive right to approve all suppliers and distributors. Approval typically takes 30 days. No fee is charged for supplier approval. Revocation is by notification to the supplier.

Of total establishment purchases, 16%–25% must come from designated/approved suppliers. Of total operating purchases, 34%–50% must come from designated/approved suppliers. Neither Sonic nor its affiliates received direct revenue from selling products to franchisees in FY2024.

**Supplier Contributions**: Over $76.75 million was contributed by suppliers to the Sonic system in FY2024, allocated as: BTF $42.5M, SBF $16.7M, SMF $13.8M, convention $3.7M. Notably, $16.7M of BTF rebates were redirected to the SBF with FAC executive committee approval, effectively cross-subsidizing brand programs from technology-earmarked supplier contributions.

### Training Program Detail
The total pre-opening training commitment is substantial: 336 hours of on-the-job training (no classroom) covering General Manager (72 hrs), Shift Manager (96 hrs split), Assistant Manager (72 hrs), and Team Member (96 hrs) programs, all at certified Sonic restaurants selected by the franchisor. Additionally, the GM Leadership Class requires 24 hours of classroom instruction (Oklahoma City, various US locations, or virtual) at $200/attendee. ServSafe certification (8 hrs) is also required for all Management Personnel.

For the first 3 restaurants, a 5-week certification training program for a Franchisee Certified Training Team (1 GM + 1–3 AMs + 1–3 SMs) at another certified Sonic restaurant is required, with all travel, lodging, and salary costs borne by the franchisee.

The Operations Manual comprises 575 pages covering 19 topic areas, with the largest being Equipment (128 pages), Food Safety (71 pages), Front Swamp Station (54 pages), and Dresser Station (53 pages).

### Technology Stack and Costs
The restaurant must have a Windows-based PC, high-speed internet (50/20 Mbps), POS system from pre-approved providers, POPS digital marketing platform, mobile ordering and payment technology, and back-office system (BOS). System costs range from $72K (Non-Drive-In/no stalls) to $182K (Traditional with 30 stalls). Annual mandatory POS maintenance is $4K–$8K; optional field services $1K–$5K.

The franchisor has "unlimited, independent access" to POS and internet-connected technology data. There is no contractual limit on the frequency or cost of technology upgrade requirements. Franchisees are solely responsible for PCI compliance.

### Marketing Fund Spending Breakdown and Audit Status
All three funds (SBF, SMF, BTF) are audited annually by an independent CPA. None are assets of the franchisor, and their earnings do not inure to the franchisor's benefit.

**SBF (FY2024)**: Advertising Production 34%, Product Cost Equalization 27%, Marketing Research 12%, Administrative 7%, Customer Relations 6%, Sonic Games 5%, Convention 4%, Mystery Shop 1%, Limeades for Learning 3%. Franchisor receives 15% admin fee capped at actual costs.

**SMF (FY2024)**: Internet/Digital 53%, TV 27%, Agency Fees 11%, Customer Research 5%, Radio 2%, Other 2%.

**BTF (FY2024)**: Technology Software/Services 55%, Satellite/Internet 16%, Support 15%, Cybersecurity 13%, Other 1%.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

The Protected Area is a radius from the front door of the restaurant, determined by population density — ranging from 0.75 miles in densely populated areas (>125,000 people/sq mi) to 3 miles in rural areas (MSA ≤25,000 or outside MSA). However, this protection is significantly limited by carve-outs: all previously existing restaurant protected radii, previously protected development areas, and all non-drive-in and non-traditional locations are excluded.

The FDD explicitly states: "You will not receive an exclusive territory. You may face competition from other franchisees, from outlets that we own, or from other channels of distribution or competitive brands that we control."

The franchisor reserves five broad rights that further dilute territorial protection: (1) operate other brands inside the PA, (2) sell same/similar products through dissimilar channels (including internet) inside the PA without compensation, (3) operate dissimilar businesses under Sonic marks inside the PA, (4) operate Sonic restaurants outside the PA regardless of proximity, and (5) be acquired by a competitor even if it operates in the PA.

Franchisees may solicit/accept orders from outside the PA (subject to delivery standards) but may not use internet, catalog, telemarketing, or other direct marketing channels.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
The initial franchise term is 10 years for Traditional and Non-Drive-In locations. Non-Traditional locations receive the lesser of 10 years or the facility contract term. Two additional 10-year renewal terms are available, but renewal requires: (1) written notice 6–12 months before expiration, (2) payment of all amounts due, (3) $15,000 renewal fee, (4) signing the then-current form of franchise agreement — which **may contain materially different terms including different fees, different Protected Area, and different franchisee rights** — (5) restaurant remodel, (6) signing a general release, and (7) completing re-training programs.

This "materially different terms" language is a significant risk: after investing $1.7M+ and operating for 10 years, a franchisee may face a substantially altered economic deal at renewal with no practical alternative.

### Transfer Provisions
All transfers require franchisor approval. Conditions include: all obligations satisfied, good standing, transferee qualifies, training complete, transfer fee paid ($1,000 non-control / $3,000 other), restaurant upgrade, general release signed by transferor, and new franchise agreement signed by transferee. The franchisor has a right of first refusal to purchase on the same terms as any third-party offer. Within 30 days of expiration/termination, the franchisor also has an option to purchase restaurant assets.

### Termination and Death/Disability
The franchisee has no right to terminate the franchise agreement. The franchisor may not terminate without cause, but the list of non-curable defaults is extensive and includes: 3 or more defaults within 12 months even if all were cured, termination of any other Sonic agreement, and failure to operate the restaurant. Curable defaults get 10 or 30 days' notice.

Upon death or disability, the franchise must be assigned to an approved heir or buyer within the greater of 120 days or completion of probate.

### Non-Competition
During the term: no involvement in any competing business. Post-term: 18 months, no competing business (and no being a landlord of a competing business) within 3 miles of the former Sonic restaurant or within 3 miles of any other Sonic restaurant. The FA provides that the franchisee consents to ex parte injunctions to enforce this provision. In North Dakota, non-competition covenants are generally considered unenforceable; the franchisor will "seek to enforce them to the extent enforceable."

### Dispute Resolution
Litigation only — no arbitration or mediation is available. Venue is the county of the franchisor's headquarters (currently Atlanta, Georgia). Georgia law governs. State overrides exist for Maryland (may bring action in MD for MD franchise law claims), Minnesota (cannot require out-of-state litigation, cannot require jury trial waiver), and North Dakota (may bring action in ND for ND franchise investment law claims).

### Litigation History
Two resolved data breach class actions (consumer: $4.3M paid by cyber insurance; financial institutions: up to $5.7M). One pending fee collection suit against a franchisee. Affiliated program disclosures include no-poach settlements for Arby's (11 states) and Dunkin' (13 states) — no monetary penalties — and a Dunkin' credential-stuffing settlement in New York ($650K).

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

The FDD provides historical AUV data for 3,086 of 3,144 franchised Sonic Restaurants (excluding 30 that opened during 2024, 26 with extended closures, 2 non-traditional, and 1 multi-brand). Company-owned restaurants (317) and the 80 that closed during 2024 are also excluded.

**Overall**:
- AUV: $1,587,024
- Median: $1,500,713
- Range: $282,129 to $4,838,846
- 43% of restaurants exceeded the average

**Quartile Distribution**:
- 1st quartile (772 restaurants): AUV $935,705 / Median $969,886
- 2nd quartile (772): AUV $1,320,788 / Median $1,320,171
- 3rd quartile (771): AUV $1,693,845 / Median $1,690,128
- 4th quartile (771): AUV $2,398,950 / Median $2,270,659

The spread is wide — the top-performing unit ($4,838,846) generates 17× the revenue of the bottom unit ($282,129). The 4th quartile average is 2.6× the 1st quartile average, indicating substantial performance dispersion.

**Regional Performance**: The West ($1,703,222 AUV) leads all regions, though with only 393 restaurants. The South dominates the system with 2,152 restaurants and a $1,559,050 AUV. The Northeast has only 67 restaurants with 31% above average — the lowest above-average percentage of any region.

**Critical Caveats**: No cost data, operating expenses, EBITDA, or profitability information is disclosed. The AUV represents top-line revenue only. The FDD states: "These financial performance representations do not reflect the costs of sales, operating expenses or other costs or expenses that must be deducted from the AUV figures to obtain your net income or profit." With ongoing franchise fees of 6.4%–11.15% of Gross Sales, plus food costs, labor, rent, utilities, insurance, and maintenance, the profit margin available to franchisees is not determinable from the FDD.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### System Trajectory
The Sonic system has been in sustained contraction. Over the three years reported:

| Year | Total Start | Total End | Net Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2022 | 3,552 | 3,546 | -6 |
| 2023 | 3,546 | 3,521 | -25 |
| 2024 | 3,521 | 3,461 | -60 |

The rate of decline is accelerating. In 2024, franchised restaurants lost 50 net units (30 opened vs. 12 terminated + 68 ceased operations). Company-owned lost 10 (1 opened, 11 closed). The ratio of total exits (80 franchised + 11 company-owned = 91) to total openings (30 + 1 = 31) is approximately 3:1.

### Transfer Trends
Transfer activity spiked dramatically in 2024 to 334 units (up from 75 in 2023 and 132 in 2022). Texas alone accounted for 110 transfers. Other states with sharp increases include South Carolina (30 vs. 0 prior year), Kentucky (29 vs. 1), Arkansas (25 vs. 1), and North Carolina (12 vs. 0). This volume represents approximately 10.6% of the franchised system changing hands in a single year, which is unusually high and may indicate large portfolio sales, distress-driven dispositions, or corporate-initiated ownership restructuring.

### State-Level Highlights
Georgia experienced the most dramatic contraction: from 77 to 52 franchised units (-25) in 2024, with 4 terminations and 21 "ceased operations" — the single largest "ceased operations" count for any state. No explanation is provided in the FDD. Company-owned Georgia locations also shrank from 22 to 19 (4 closed).

### Projected Openings
As of December 29, 2024, there were 20 franchise agreements signed but restaurants not yet opened, and 23 projected new franchise drive-ins in the next fiscal year. No projected company-owned openings. The pipeline is modest relative to the system's 3,461 total — less than 1% growth from new openings.

### Gag Clause
The FDD explicitly discloses: "During the last 3 years, some franchisees have signed confidentiality agreements with us. In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with the Sonic Restaurant network. You may wish to speak with current and former franchisees, but be aware that not all such franchisees will be able to communicate with you."

This is a material disclosure that limits a prospective franchisee's ability to conduct due diligence through direct franchisee contact.

## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

Two sets of KPMG-audited financial statements are provided: one for Sonic Franchising LLC (the franchisor entity) and one for Sonic Industries Services LLC and Subsidiaries (the management/operating entity).

### Sonic Franchising LLC

The franchisor entity is a special-purpose entity with minimal independent operations. Its sole purpose is to hold franchise agreements and collect royalties, which it largely passes through to affiliates.

**Balance Sheet (12/29/2024)**: Total assets of $36.2M consist primarily of intangible assets (franchise agreements) of $30.9M and accounts receivable of $4.7M. There is only $593K of restricted cash — no unrestricted cash. Member's equity is $26.4M, down from $28.5M a year prior due to distributions exceeding net income.

**Income Statement (FY2024)**: Revenue of $59.0M (franchise fees and royalties) — of which $8.0M was from affiliates. Against this, $49.4M was paid out as "royalty fees to Affiliates" (84% of revenue), leaving only $9.6M for G&A ($834K), amortization ($2.8M), and net income ($6.0M). Net income declined from $8.1M (2023) to $6.0M (2024).

**Distributions**: $8.1M distributed to parent in FY2024, $11.3M in FY2023, $17.7M in FY2022. Distributions consistently exceed net income, eroding member's equity — from $42.2M at the start of 2022 to $26.4M at the end of 2024.

**Securitization Guarantee**: The franchisor entity guarantees approximately $1,417.3M in Sonic Securitization Notes issued by Sonic Co-Issuers, with legal final maturities extending to 2050/2051. The franchisor has pledged substantially all its assets to secure these obligations.

### Sonic Industries Services LLC and Subsidiaries

This is the substantive operating entity that manages the Sonic franchise system and owns real estate.

**Balance Sheet (12/29/2024)**: Total assets of $2,335.0M. Goodwill is $822.6M (unchanged from 2023 — no impairment). Intangible assets include a $1.1B indefinite-lived trademark and $164M in definite-lived intangibles (franchise agreements $160.8M, computer software $3.3M). Property and equipment is $149.1M (land $124.8M, buildings $61.6M, equipment $11.7M). Total debt is $1,407.2M (net of issuance costs).

**Income Statement (FY2024)**: Total revenue of $295.9M comprising franchise fees/royalties ($233.2M), rental income ($18.0M), and other revenues ($44.6M). G&A expenses were $41.4M and depreciation/amortization $21.5M, yielding operating income of $233.3M. Interest expense was $51.4M. Net income was $183.3M. The 2023 net income of $439.8M was inflated by a $282.6M income tax benefit from the LLC conversion; normalized net income was approximately $157M.

**Cash Flow**: Operating cash flow of $171.7M. Debt repayments of $27.2M (annual). Distributions to parent of $137.8M.

**Debt Structure**: Five tranches of securitization notes:
- 2020-1 Class A-2 I: $621.8M at 3.85%, anticipated repayment Jan 2027
- 2020-1 Class A-2 II: $239.2M at 4.34%, anticipated repayment Jan 2030
- 2021-1 Class A-2-I: $241.9M at 2.19%, anticipated repayment Aug 2028
- 2021-1 Class A-2-II: $314.4M at 2.64%, anticipated repayment Aug 2031

**Key Financial Observations**:
- Leverage is very high: $1,417M debt against $856.7M equity (debt/equity ~1.65×)
- Distributions consistently exceed free cash flow requirements
- The $1.1B trademark is the single largest asset — impairment testing has shown no impairment to date
- No goodwill impairment despite system shrinkage
- Gift card liability of $36.6M represents a significant consumer float
- All debt covenants were in compliance as of 12/29/2024
- The 2027 maturity of $621.8M will require refinancing

## I. State Addenda Summary

Six states have addenda or riders in Exhibit G:

**California**: Fast Food Act (Cal. Lab. Code §§ 1474-1475) compliance is the franchisee's sole responsibility. No franchisor training or fee assistance related to Fast Food Act. Item 7 additional funds estimate accounts for increased wages. No waiver of state franchise law claims.

**Hawaii**: Standard franchise investment law registration notice.

**Maryland**: General release for renewal/transfer does not apply to claims under MD Franchise Registration and Disclosure Law. Termination upon insolvency may not be enforceable under federal bankruptcy law. Franchisee may bring actions in Maryland. 3-year statute of limitations. Anti-waiver provision.

**Minnesota**: Franchisor must indemnify MN franchisees against third-party trademark infringement claims. Requires 90 days' notice of termination (60 days to cure) and 180 days' notice of non-renewal. Cannot require litigation outside Minnesota, jury trial waiver, consent to injunctive relief/liquidated damages/termination penalties. General release excludes MN franchise law claims.

**North Dakota**: General release excludes ND Franchise Investment Law claims. Non-competition covenants "generally considered unenforceable" but franchisor will seek enforcement. Franchisee may bring action in ND. ND law applies to extent required.

**Washington**: Assurance of Discontinuance with WA AG — removed no-poach/employee solicitation restriction from form FA, will not enforce in existing agreements, no fines assessed.

## J. Unresolveds

1. **BTF continuation post-3/2026** (Medium): The technology fee (0.25% of Gross Sales) continues after March 2026 only upon 67% franchisee vote. The status of this vote is unknown, but it represents approximately $12M+ annually in system technology funding. If not approved, the technology infrastructure funding model would need restructuring.

2. **Supplier contribution cross-subsidy** (Low): $16.7M of BTF supplier rebates were redirected to the SBF with FAC executive committee approval. While within the FAC's advisory authority, this effectively repurposes technology-designated funds for brand marketing — a potential governance concern.

3. **Securitization exposure** (High): The franchisor entity guarantees $1.4B+ in securitization notes and has pledged substantially all its assets. If securitization covenants are breached or refinancing fails (particularly the $621.8M tranche due 2027), the franchisor's ability to fulfill its franchise obligations could be compromised.

4. **Georgia franchise collapse** (Medium): Georgia went from 77 to 52 franchised restaurants in 2024 (-25), with 4 terminations and 21 "ceased operations." This is the most dramatic single-state contraction in the system, and no explanation is provided. It may relate to specific market conditions, ownership group issues, or a broader refranchising strategy.

5. **Transfer spike** (Medium): The 4.5× increase in transfers (334 in 2024 vs. 75 in 2023) may indicate portfolio-level ownership consolidation (consistent with private-equity-driven refranchising), distress sales, or opportunistic acquisitions. The concentration in Texas (110 transfers, or 12.4% of the state's franchised base) suggests a major ownership change.
