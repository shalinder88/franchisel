## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System identity**: Pizza Hut, LLC is a Delaware limited liability company that franchises traditional Pizza Hut restaurants offering pizza, pasta, Italian-style food items, and WingStreet chicken products. The franchisor operates from 7100 Corporate Drive, Plano, Texas. Pizza Hut has been in continuous operation since 1958 and has offered franchises since 1959.

2. **Entity structure**: PHLLC → Pizza Hut Guarantor, LLC (direct parent) → Pizza Hut Holdings, LLC (indirect parent) → YUM! Brands, Inc. (ultimate parent, Louisville, KY). The guarantor entity exists specifically to guarantee PHLLC's franchise obligations and must maintain minimum net worth of $15 million.

3. **Strategic uncertainty**: In November 2025, YUM announced it is exploring strategic options for the Pizza Hut brand, potentially including a full or partial sale or separation. This creates material uncertainty about the future franchise relationship, brand direction, and technology investment continuity.

4. **System size**: As of December 29, 2025, the system comprised 5,031 restaurants (4,956 franchised, 75 company-owned). The system has been contracting: net decline of 206 units in FY2025, 70 in FY2024, and 16 in FY2023. An additional 127 franchised restaurants ceased operations between December 30, 2025 and March 6, 2026.

5. **Investment range**: Total investment ranges from $670,000 (Inline/Endcap Delco) to $2,130,000 (RBD/DBR and FCD), excluding land. The initial franchise fee is $25,000.

6. **Fee burden**: The ongoing fee structure includes a 6.0% royalty (6.5% in certain circumstances), 4.75% advertising fund contribution, Digital Innovation Fee ($0.41/digital transaction), Restaurant Technology Fee ($2,950/year), and Dragontail KMX fees ($240 install + $75/month for DaaS restaurants). Total ongoing burden is approximately 11.75%+ of gross sales before technology fees.

7. **Item 19 (FPR)**: Average gross sales for all 4,767 mature franchised restaurants in FY2025 were $972,864 (median $929,232). Only 43.4% of restaurants exceeded the average. This represents a 5.1% decline from FY2024's average of $1,024,898. No cost, EBITDA, or net income data is disclosed.

8. **Bottom-quartile risk**: The wide range of gross sales ($233,001 to $3,050,083) indicates significant variability. With less than half of franchisees exceeding the average in both years, and average sales declining, bottom-quartile restaurants face substantial viability risk given the ~11.75% minimum fee burden.

9. **Financial health**: Pizza Hut Guarantor reported net income of $195.2M in FY2025 (down from $226.8M in FY2024 and $231.8M in FY2023). Virtually all operating cash ($193.4M) was swept to YUM. Franchise receivable write-offs surged to $18.4M in FY2025 from essentially zero in FY2024, signaling deteriorating franchisee credit quality.

10. **Growth trajectory**: The system is shrinking. FY2025 saw 55 new openings against 259 cessations and 54 reacquisitions by the franchisor. Only 33 new franchised outlets are projected for FY2026. The Hut Forward Program permits qualified franchisees to close up to 10% of existing restaurants.

11. **Territory**: No exclusive territory. Protected radius of only 500 yards. Extensive franchisor reservation of rights including alternative distribution channels, non-traditional locations, and National/Regional/Institutional Accounts within the protected radius.

12. **Renewal**: Two 5-year renewal terms available after 10-year initial term, but the renewal franchise agreement may be "materially different" from the current form.

13. **Disputes/litigation**: One pending mass arbitration (80 claimants alleging Meta Pixel wiretapping violations). Dispute resolution requires mediation in Plano, TX and litigation in Collin County, TX. Texas governing law applies.

14. **Special risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution and no exclusive territory are called out on the Special Risks page.

15. **Gag clause**: Present. Current and former franchisees may have signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Pizza Hut, LLC is the franchisor, successor to Pizza Hut, Inc. which merged into PHLLC on May 20, 2016. PHLLC holds all domestic Pizza Hut franchise, license, and development agreements and substantially all intellectual property. The entity has no employees — all personnel are provided by Pizza Hut of America, LLC (PHALLC), an affiliate that also operates company-owned restaurants and leases real estate to franchisees.

The franchise is offered in four concept types: RBD (Restaurant-Based Delivery — traditional dine-in with delivery), Delco (Delivery/Carryout only), DBR (Delivery Based Restaurant — large-format >50 seats), and FCD (Fast Casual Delco — small-format <50 seats). All new restaurants must incorporate the WingStreet product line. Red Roof restaurants are no longer generally offered.

The Hut Forward Program, executed January 5, 2026, represents a comprehensive restructuring effort between YUM, PHLLC, and IPHFHA. It includes YUM marketing investment, delivery strategy development, a new technology framework, and Brand Standards revisions. The vast majority of existing franchisees entered into adoption agreements permitting closure of up to 10% of their restaurants.

### Fee Stack

The ongoing fee burden for a Pizza Hut franchisee is substantial and multi-layered:

**Royalty**: 6.0% of Gross Sales (6.5% in certain circumstances). This rate is not uniformly imposed. Gross Sales means all revenues from any source, less good-faith customer credits and properly remitted sales taxes. In states that prohibit royalties on alcoholic beverage sales, PHLLC estimates excluded revenues and sets an effective royalty rate.

**System Advertising Fund**: 4.75% of Gross Sales. This is credited against IPHFHA dues of the same amount, so the effective rate is 4.75% (not additive). The Advertising Committee has sole control over creative concepts, media, and placement. In its most recent fiscal year, it spent 75% on media placement, 18% on agency compensation/PR/research/brand marketing, 0% on administration, with 7% carried forward. The Advertising Committee is audited annually.

**Technology fees**: These represent a growing cost layer. The Digital Innovation Fee is $0.41 per transaction through digital or automated channels. The Restaurant Technology Fee is $2,950 per year per restaurant, payable in monthly installments. PHLLC can increase the tech fee up to 12% per year, or more with Technology Governance Committee approval. The Help Desk High-Use Fee kicks in at $12/call and $7/other event beyond 52 events per year. Restaurants using Delivery-as-a-Service must also pay the Dragontail KMX fee ($240 installation + $75/month), though this was suspended for Q1 2026 under the Hut Forward Agreement. PHLLC anticipates DaaS will become required for all restaurants in the future.

**Additional Third-Party Technology Services** (telecom, internet, credit card processing) cost $5,500–$8,000 per year. Computer System maintenance runs $1,000–$3,500 per year.

**Event-driven fees**: Transfer fee of $2,500 plus $250 per restaurant transferred, plus outside counsel fees. Renewal fee of $12,500 per restaurant (may increase to $25,000). Unauthorized closure penalty of 24 times the average monthly royalty. Late charges of 1.5% per month. On termination for default, the franchisee must pay all losses including lost profits, lost opportunities, and attorneys' fees.

For a restaurant generating the FY2025 median gross sales of $929,232, the minimum ongoing fee burden would be approximately:
- Royalty (6%): $55,754
- Ad Fund (4.75%): $44,138
- Restaurant Technology Fee: $2,950
- Computer maintenance: ~$2,250
- Third-party tech services: ~$6,750
- Digital Innovation Fee: varies by digital transaction volume
- **Estimated minimum: ~$112,000/year before Dragontail, Dinova, or variable fees**

### Initial Investment

The total initial investment varies significantly by concept type:

| Concept | Total Range | Key Driver |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| RBD/DBR (Dine-In/Delivery/Carryout) | $846,000 – $2,130,000 | Building/site: $619K–$1.65M |
| FCD (Fast Casual Delco) | $818,000 – $2,130,000 | Building/site: $619K–$1.75M |
| Freestanding Delco | $816,000 – $1,900,000 | Building/site: $619K–$1.49M |

Building and site improvements dominate the investment at 72–82% of the total. Equipment ranges from $125,000–$300,000 depending on concept. The computer system costs $15,000–$30,000. None of the expenditures are refundable, and PHLLC offers no financing.

Typical square footage requirements: 2,000–4,000 (RBD), 1,600–2,900 (DBR/FCD), 1,200–1,600 (Delco). Vehicle costs for delivery are excluded as vehicles are typically personally owned.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Franchisees face extensive supplier restrictions. The Computer System must be acquired exclusively from Pizza Hut Connect, National Systems, and Dragontail Systems — all PHLLC affiliates. Proprietary products must come from PHLLC, RSCS, or designated Approved Distributors. Non-proprietary products must come from Approved Distributors or suppliers approved by PHLLC.

McLane Foodservice is the principal system distributor, managed by RSCS (Restaurant Supply Chain Solutions). Franchisees must join the Pizza Hut National Purchasing Coop ($10 membership stock + $400 per restaurant for Store Common Stock) and purchase virtually all goods through RSCS/Co-op programs. The Co-op is governed by an 8-member board (6 franchisee-elected, 2 PHLLC-appointed) plus the RSCS President as non-voting ex officio.

Required purchases constitute approximately 30–45% of establishment costs and 30–50% of operating costs.

### Approved Supplier Process

To propose an unapproved supplier, franchisees must submit a written request per Brand Standards. PHLLC will try to respond within 45 days. Requirements may include sample submission, cost-bearing for testing/inspection, distribution chain details, and facility inspection/audit. PHLLC can revoke approval at any time upon the supplier's failure to meet criteria.

### Affiliate Revenue from Franchisee Purchases

In FY2025, PHLLC affiliates derived substantial revenue from franchisee purchases:
- Pizza Hut Connect: $68.0M (of which $31.1M to Digico under Digital Investment Committee direction)
- Dragontail: $3.6M
- PHALLC real estate leases: $3.9M
- Aggregator royalties to PHLLC: $12.1M (from Grubhub, Uber Eats, DoorDash)
- Mastercard rebates to YRSG: $64K
- Visa rebates to YRSG: $491K

### Training Program

The Initial Training Program ("First Slice") spans 6–12 weeks and includes:
- Restaurant Training (BCM/LEAD On!): 15–37 hours online + 8 hours/day OJT for 6–12 weeks at a qualified restaurant location
- Franchisee Onboarding: 8 hours/day for 3 days with designated PHLLC functional leaders (in-person in Plano or virtual)
- Pizza Prep 1: ~6 hours/day classroom for 2 days in Plano, TX
- Pizza Prep 2: ~8 hours/day classroom for 2 days in Plano, TX

Training is conducted approximately 4 times per year. Instructors have minimum 2 years field experience and 2 years operational experience. The first Qualified Operator's training is included in the franchise fee; additional trainees cost $500/person/week. The franchisee bears all living and transportation expenses.

All restaurant employees must complete mandatory BCM training within 30 days of hire. Management-level employees must complete PHLLC-approved management training. LEAD On! is optional but recommended.

### Technology Stack and Costs

The Restaurant Technology Suite is comprehensive:
- POS System (SUS) — point-of-sale order processing
- BOH System — sales forecasting, labor needs, employee hours
- KM System — kitchen management, order tracking
- HutBot System — AI-powered platform for operation enhancement and job execution

Three mandatory software agreements with affiliates: SUS/FMS License (Appendix K to FA), QuikOrder Online Order (Appendix L to FA), and HutBot Digital Program (Exhibit L). DaaS restaurants must also sign Dragontail KMX (Exhibit K).

PHLLC has unlimited access to all data in the Computer System with no contractual limitation. Franchisees must grant access to all components and may not restrict PHLLC's retrieval of information.

### Marketing Fund Spending

The Advertising Committee spent its most recent fiscal year's contributions as follows:
- 75% on media placement
- 18% on other (agency compensation, PR, research/consulting, brand marketing)
- 0% on administrative expenses
- 7% carried forward unspent

The Committee is audited annually with statements distributed to franchisees. PHLLC and affiliates contribute proportionally based on company-owned restaurant count. Fund expenditures are not required to be proportionate or directly beneficial to any particular franchisee.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory

Franchisees receive NO exclusive territory. The only protection is a 500-yard "Protected Radius" surrounding each restaurant Location. Within this radius, PHLLC will not develop or allow another franchisee/licensee to develop the SAME type of System Restaurant Concept. However, PHLLC explicitly reserves broad rights within the Protected Radius itself:

- Any OTHER type of System Restaurant Concept within the Protected Radius
- Acquisition/merger targets operating within the Protected Radius under any marks
- Alternative distribution channels (wholesale, retail, or otherwise) within the Protected Radius
- Non-traditional locations (sports arenas, airports, malls, universities, hospitals, military bases, etc.) within the Protected Radius
- National, Regional, and Institutional Account fulfillment within the Protected Radius

Franchisees are required to waive and release all claims related to PHLLC's competitive activities and promise never to join any legal action contesting these rights.

### Delivery Area

Each restaurant receives a specified Delivery Area, but this protection is contingent on providing "Adequate Delivery Service" per Brand Standards. If PHLLC determines the franchisee is not providing Adequate Delivery Service: written notice → 30-day cure → if uncured, PHLLC may redefine delivery area → on second failure, may terminate the franchise. During cure periods, PHLLC or other franchisees may furnish delivery within the affected area.

### Channel Restrictions

Franchisees may only sell from the approved Location and may deliver only within their Delivery Area. No wholesale distribution, no alternative channels without written consent. PHLLC alone controls National/Regional/Institutional Accounts and may require franchisees to fulfill orders at PHLLC-negotiated prices.

### YUM Sibling Competition

KFC, Taco Bell, and Habit Burger Grill (all YUM brands) may operate within the Protected Radius. A Taco Bell or KFC operator may even be granted a non-traditional Pizza Hut kiosk license within their restaurant, subject to the same-concept limitation.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial franchise term is 10 years from each restaurant's opening date. Two consecutive 5-year renewal terms are available if the franchisee meets all conditions: timely notice, full compliance history, current on all payments (to PHLLC, affiliates, landlord, suppliers), current on maintenance and asset upgrades, possession of Location, satisfy qualification/training, sign general release, pay renewal fee ($12,500/restaurant, may increase to $25,000), and sign the then-current franchise agreement.

The renewal agreement "may be materially different" from the current form. Only three protections: no initial franchise fee, no change in Protected Radius, and royalty rate not greater than what is imposed on similarly situated renewing franchisees. This "materially different" risk is significant — a franchisee with 20 years of investment may be forced to accept substantially different terms to continue operating.

### Transfer Provisions

Any transfer (including change of control, assignment, sale, pledge, or encumbrance) requires PHLLC's prior written consent with 30 days advance notice. Permitted transfers without full consent include transfers to existing owners, employee benefit vehicles, or wholly-owned entities.

The transferee must meet all qualifications, complete the Initial Training Program, sign the then-current franchise agreement (which may include rebuild/relocation/remodel obligations), sign a guaranty, and sign a general release. The transferor must cure all defaults, satisfy all monetary obligations, sign a termination agreement and general release, furnish transfer documents, and pay the transfer fee ($2,500 + $250/restaurant + outside counsel fees).

PHLLC has a right of first refusal to match any offer for the interest being transferred. PHLLC must also determine that the total transfer price will not jeopardize economic viability.

### Termination Triggers

PHLLC may terminate only for cause, with an extensive list of both curable and non-curable defaults:

**Curable defaults** (generally 30-day cure; 10 days for repeated payment defaults; 24 hours for trademark misuse):
- Failure to comply with FA or Brand Standards
- Failure to pay amounts owed
- Sale of unauthorized products
- Failure to provide Adequate Delivery Service
- Failure to construct/open as required

**Non-curable defaults** (immediate termination on notice):
- Failure to begin construction
- Unauthorized closure >15 consecutive days
- Felony/fraud/moral turpitude conviction
- Unauthorized transfer
- Non-compete or confidentiality violation
- Revenue concealment or false records
- Repeated curable defaults
- Bankruptcy/insolvency
- Cross-default under other agreements with PHLLC or affiliates

On termination for default, the franchisee must pay all losses including lost profits, lost opportunities, attorneys' fees, and all damages — essentially unlimited termination damages.

### Non-Competition

**During term**: You and Related Persons may not be involved in any business producing or selling pizza, pasta, or food items similar to Approved Products (including WingStreet), or be a Competitor. A "Competitor" is defined as deriving >10% of revenue from similar products, granting franchises for similar products, or offering confusingly similar products. You may not sell or lease premises to a Competitor.

**Post-term (18 months)**: You may not produce/sell similar products or assist a Competitor within (a) 25-mile radius of ANY Pizza Hut Restaurant, (b) any county where Pizza Hut Restaurants are situated, or (c) within 10 miles of any U.S. System Restaurant. For 2 years, you may not sell or lease premises to a Competitor.

State overrides: California's addendum notes the post-term non-compete "may not be enforceable." North Dakota subjects it to ND Century Code 9-08-06.

### Dispute Resolution

Disputes (excluding trademark, confidential information, and money owed) go first to mediation under the National Franchise Mediation Program (CPR). Mediation is only for disputes specific to the franchisee, not system-wide issues. Mediation venue is Plano, Texas; litigation venue is Collin County, Texas. Texas governing law applies. Loser pays attorneys' fees.

State overrides: Maryland allows lawsuits in MD for MD franchise law claims. Minnesota requires 90-day termination notice with 60-day cure and 180-day non-renewal notice. North Dakota prohibits out-of-state jurisdiction and requires arbitration/mediation in Fargo, ND if parties cannot agree.

### Litigation History

One pending matter: a mass arbitration filed December 3, 2025 (AAA Case 01-25-0009-3768) by 80 individual claimants in California alleging violations of California and federal wiretapping laws, statutory larceny, invasion of privacy, and intrusion upon seclusion related to the Meta Pixel on the Pizza Hut website. The matter is in early stages. PHLLC intends to vigorously defend.

### Transfer Activity

Transfers surged in FY2025 to 189 (from 37 in FY2024 and 147 in FY2023). Notable 2025 transfers: Georgia (50), Illinois (29), North Carolina (24), Wisconsin (23), Alabama (18), Tennessee (11). The surge in transfers combined with reacquisitions suggests portfolio restructuring and possible franchisee distress.

PHLLC dramatically increased company-owned units from 7 (end 2023) to 75 (end 2025). The FY2025 reacquisitions were concentrated in Louisiana (18), North Carolina (30), and South Carolina (6). In 2024, 16 units were reacquired in Utah. This reacquisition pattern may indicate franchisee failures in these markets or strategic corporate repositioning.

### State-Level Highlights

States with largest net franchised unit declines in FY2025: California (-28), Florida (-25), Louisiana (-25 including 18 reacquired), Illinois (-20), Georgia (-18), Texas (-9), Minnesota (-10), Wisconsin (-9), Pennsylvania (-7). Only New York showed meaningful growth (85 → 86).

### Projected Openings

Only 33 new franchised outlets are projected for FY2026 across 17 states. Zero franchise agreements are signed but not yet opened. Zero company-owned openings projected. The 33 projected openings against the recent closure pace suggest continued net contraction.

### Gag Clause

The FDD explicitly states: "In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with us." Prospective franchisees should be aware that some franchisees may not be able to provide candid feedback about their experience.

### Franchisee Organization

Located at 10111 E 21st St N, Ste. 310, Wichita, KS 67206 (316-685-1208, www.iphfha.com). ## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Overview

The financial statements are for Pizza Hut Guarantor, LLC and its subsidiary (PHLLC), audited by KPMG LLP (Louisville, KY). KPMG issued an unqualified (clean) opinion. The statements cover three fiscal years ending December 29, 2025.

### Balance Sheet Analysis

Total assets declined modestly from $149.6M (2024) to $146.1M (2025). The asset composition is dominated by accounts receivable ($93.4M net, up from $81.7M) and franchise incentives ($32.8M total current + long-term). Cash is minimal at $1.1M — essentially a pass-through entity with daily cash sweeps to YUM.

The most notable balance sheet development is the dramatic decline in restricted cash from $16.0M to less than $0.1M. Restricted cash represented advertising cooperative funds — the drawdown suggests either heavy ad spending or reduced franchisee contributions consistent with the shrinking system.

Accounts receivable increased despite the system contracting, and the allowance for doubtful accounts showed contrasting signals: the allowance balance decreased from $22.9M to $13.1M, but actual write-offs surged to $18.4M. This suggests PHLLC took large write-offs on previously reserved amounts while continuing to extend credit to remaining franchisees.

Total liabilities declined from $82.3M to $77.0M, primarily driven by lower AP/other current liabilities ($67.0M vs $73.8M). Short-term borrowings of $2.0M appeared for the first time — drawn on AdCom's $30M revolving line of credit.

Member's equity increased slightly to $69.1M from $67.3M, comfortably above the $15M minimum net worth requirement for the large franchisor exemption.

### Income Statement Analysis

Total revenues declined 9.4% from $529.6M to $479.7M. Both revenue streams contracted:
- Franchise and license revenues: $261.2M (down 9.6% from $288.8M)
- Franchise advertising contributions: $218.4M (down 9.3% from $240.8M)

The franchise revenue decline directly reflects the system's contraction — fewer restaurants paying royalties on declining same-store sales.

Total costs and expenses fell to $296.6M from $315.4M, but franchise and license expenses surged to $23.1M from $17.1M — a 34.7% increase driven by bad debt ($10.7M), discretionary marketing ($3.5M), and food safety ($3.7M). The bad debt spike is concerning and consistent with franchisee financial stress.

Other income was $12.2M (roughly stable). Net income fell to $195.2M, a 13.9% decline from $226.8M.

### Cash Flow Analysis

Operating cash flow of $176.1M was down from $219.7M, tracking net income decline plus working capital changes. The most significant financing activity was $193.4M swept to YUM (down from $226.9M). This cash sweep exceeds operating cash flow, with the gap funded by short-term borrowings and restricted cash releases.

Investing activities are negligible ($20K in software/computer equipment), consistent with an asset-light franchise model.

### Key Financial Observations

1. **Revenue erosion**: Franchise revenues declining at nearly 10% annually is significant. With system contraction accelerating and same-store sales declining, this trend is likely to continue.

2. **Bad debt surge**: Total bad debt across franchise and advertising receivables was $17.9M in FY2025. AR write-offs of $18.4M (vs <$0.1M in 2024) indicate a step-change in franchisee credit risk.

3. **Cash sweep**: PHLLC functions as a cash generation machine for YUM. Essentially 100% of operating cash flow is upstreamed, leaving minimal cash reserves at the operating level.

4. **No employees**: The entity has zero employees — all resources are provided by PHALLC. Total personnel cost allocated: $39.8M (2025).

5. **Disregarded entity**: Neither PH Guarantor nor PHLLC is subject to income taxes — all income flows to YUM's tax filings.

6. **53rd week impact**: FY2024 included a 53rd week adding approximately $5.5M to revenue and $4.0M to net income. Adjusting for this, the FY2024 to FY2025 decline is approximately 12.6% in revenue.

## M. Technology and Digital Transformation

### Current Technology Requirements

The Pizza Hut franchise technology stack is extensive and growing. The Restaurant Technology Suite encompasses four mandatory components: POS (point-of-sale processing through the SUS system), BOH (back-of-house forecasting, labor management, employee tracking), KM (kitchen management and order progress tracking), and HutBot (an AI-powered global platform for operational enhancement and job execution streamlining).

Franchisees must sign three mandatory software agreements with PHLLC affiliates: the SUS/FMS License and Support Agreement with Pizza Hut Connect for POS, BOH, and help desk services; the QuikOrder Online Order Agreement with National Systems/QuikOrder for internet ordering; and the HutBot Digital Program Agreement with Pizza Hut Connect for AI-powered operations. DaaS-participating restaurants must additionally sign the Dragontail KMX adoption agreement for kitchen management services.

### Technology Cost Layers

The technology cost structure is multi-layered and subject to escalation:
- Restaurant Technology Fee: $2,950/year (12%/year max increase, higher with TechGov approval)
- Digital Innovation Fee: $0.41 per digital/automated channel transaction
- Help Desk High-Use Fee: $12/call, $7/other event beyond 52/year
- Dragontail KMX: $240 install + $75/month (suspended Q1 2026, may resume)
- Additional Third-Party Tech: $5,500-$8,000/year
- Hardware maintenance: $1,000-$3,500/year
- Computer System initial: $15,000-$30,000

The Technology Framework Agreement (December 27, 2022) established a holistic funding strategy for 2023-2026. The Hut Forward Program (January 5, 2026) is developing a new technology framework agreement that will require franchisees to sign new technology agreements. The full scope and cost impact of these future technology obligations remain unresolved.

### Data Ownership and Access

PHLLC retains broad data rights. PHLLC can access all information stored in the Computer System with no contractual limitation. Under the HutBot Agreement, Pizza Hut owns all derived data from franchise operations. All customer information belongs to PHLLC. These provisions give the franchisor complete visibility into and ownership of franchisee operational data.

## O. Competitive Landscape and Brand Position

Pizza Hut occupies a historically dominant but increasingly challenged position in the U.S. pizza market. The system's contraction from 5,323 total units at the start of FY2023 to 5,031 at the end of FY2025 (a loss of 292 units, or 5.5%) reflects competitive pressures from both traditional pizza competitors and emerging delivery-focused brands.

The simultaneous decline in same-store sales (average gross sales down 5.1% YoY) and unit count represents a compounding challenge — the revenue base is shrinking on two dimensions simultaneously. The Hut Forward Program's explicit provision allowing franchisees to close up to 10% of restaurants suggests the franchisor anticipates further rationalization.

The strategic review announced by YUM in November 2025 adds a layer of uncertainty that is unusual for a franchise system of this scale. A change in ownership could affect brand direction, technology investment commitments, advertising strategy, and the franchise relationship itself.
