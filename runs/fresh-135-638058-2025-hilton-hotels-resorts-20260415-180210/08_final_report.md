## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size & position:** 187 franchised Hilton/Hilton Suites hotels in the US at year-end 2024 (down from 189). Zero company-owned units. Upper upscale full-service segment. Part of Hilton Worldwide Holdings (NYSE: HLT), the world's largest hospitality company by room count across 20+ brands.

2. **Entity structure:** Hilton Franchise Holding LLC (DE LLC, formed 2007) → Hilton Domestic Operating Company Inc. → Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. The franchisor is a pass-through entity for Hilton's franchise revenue, distributing $1.5B to parent in 2024. All assets pledged as collateral for Hilton's corporate debt.

3. **Ownership history:** Brand traces to 1946 (Hilton Hotels Corporation). Current franchisor entity since 2015. Predecessor entities: Hilton Franchise LLC (2007–2015), Hilton Inns, Inc. (1962–2007). Park Hotels & Resorts spin-off in 2017 divested company-owned hotels.

4. **Investment range:** $49.7M to $204.3M for a 300-room new-development hotel, excluding land. This is among the highest capital commitments in the franchise industry. The wide range reflects variations in construction costs, geography, and hotel amenities/configuration.

5. **Fee burden:** Approximately 16.75–17.05% of gross rooms revenue in core ongoing fees (5% royalty + 4% program + 0.45–0.75% OnQ + 4.3% Hilton Honors + 3% F&B), before any distribution/sales program fees, quality assurance penalties, or training costs. Royalty was modified in 11 instances in 2024, suggesting some negotiability for experienced operators.

6. **Item 19 highlights:** Average room rate $209.86 (2024); only 24.8% of franchisee-managed hotels met or exceeded this average. RevPAR Index of 104.7 indicates the brand captures above fair share vs. competitive sets. No cost, EBITDA, or profitability data disclosed — a significant limitation for investor underwriting.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk:** 75.2% of franchisee-managed hotels fall below the system average room rate. The $112–$645 room rate range and 38.4%–98.4% occupancy range suggest highly variable unit economics depending on location, market, and hotel age.

8. **Financial health:** Franchisor revenue $1.502B (2024), net income $1.488B. Extremely high-margin pass-through entity with minimal operating expenses ($11.4M total). Clean audit opinion from Cherry Bekaert. No going concern. However, entity is fully dependent on Hilton parent, and all assets are pledged as debt collateral.

9. **Growth trajectory:** Net -2 units in 2024 (4 openings, 3 terminations, 3 net ceased). 11 franchise agreements signed but not yet opened; 3 projected new openings in next fiscal year. Mature, stable system with minimal net growth at the brand level.

10. **Territory:** No exclusive or protected territory by default. Negotiable "Restricted Area Provision" available but with extensive carve-outs (existing hotels, other Hilton brands, gaming, timeshare, acquisitions of 4+ hotel chains) and a Restrictive Period shorter than the franchise term.

11. **Renewal:** NO renewal right. The franchise agreement is non-renewable. Re-licensing is at the franchisor's sole discretion and may require materially different terms. Given the $50M–$204M investment, this is a structural risk that requires careful legal and financial analysis.

12. **Disputes:** Forum in Virginia/New York (away from most franchisees). New York governing law. No arbitration (except Puerto Rico). Five pending antitrust class actions related to hotel industry revenue management software.

13. **Special risks:** Out-of-state dispute resolution (Virginia primary, New York fallback). Gag clause on some former franchisees. All assets pledged for parent debt.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Hilton Franchise Holding LLC is a single-purpose franchise entity that licenses intellectual property from its affiliate Hilton International Holding LLC on a royalty-free basis, then sublicenses it to franchisees. The entity has virtually no operating expenses of its own ($1.35M in 2024) — all services are provided by Hilton employees acting on the franchisor's behalf.

Key affiliates providing services to franchisees:
- **Hilton Reservations Worldwide** — national/international reservation services
- **Hilton Supply Management (HSM)** — procurement; $19.6M rebates + $38.7M admin fees + $8.8M goods/services revenue in 2024
- **Hilton Honors Worldwide** — guest loyalty program (mandatory participation)
- **Hilton Systems Solutions (HSS)** — OnQ computer systems, HITS Agreement

### Fee Stack

**Core Ongoing Fees (as % of GRR unless noted):**
- Monthly Royalty: 5%
- Monthly Program Fee: 4% (cap: current + 1% over term)
- OnQ FBPP: 0.45–0.75% (non-REIT); 0.35–0.45% (REIT)
- Hilton Honors: 4.3% of eligible guest folio
- Food & Beverage Fee: 3% of GFBR
- Spa Royalty (if applicable): 2% of GSR

**Event-Driven Fees:**
- Guest Assistance: $200–$300/transaction
- Brand Non-Compliance: $65–$145/room (max $50K/6mo)
- Stay Score Failure: up to $300/room or 0.5% GRR annually
- Service Quality Improvement: $20K–$50K/6mo
- Plans & Designs Late: $8,000/90 days
- PIP/Renovation Failure: $16,000/6 months
- Brand Conference: $2,500/attendee annually
- Unauthorized Opening: $5,000/day

**Distribution/Sales Fees (selected):**
- AAA/CAA: $0.30/room + 10% commission
- Hilton Advance: 1.35% Digital Direct Revenue (max $30/stay)
- Group Preferred Partnership: up to $1.80/night + commission
- Third-Party Reservation: up to $5.93/stay
- FastPay: up to $1.40/transaction + commission
- Consortia: up to $3.00/night + 10% commission
- ResMax: 5.4–5.9% consumed revenue

**Transfer/Remedies:**
- COO Application Fee: $150,000
- Permitted Transfer Processing: $5,500
- Lender Comfort Letter: $3,500
- Liquidated Damages: up to 60× average monthly royalty
- Late Payment: 1.5%/month

### Initial Investment (300-Room New Development)

Total: **$49,685,442 to $204,323,235** (excluding land, market study, insurance, interest)

The investment is dominated by construction ($25.4M–$145.2M) and FF&E ($15M–$22.5M). Technology costs are relatively modest ($310K–$593K across all systems). The franchisee must also budget $1.6M–$2.2M in additional funds for 3 months of post-opening operations, plus $2.3M–$13.2M in contingencies.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory
- **No exclusive territory** by default
- **Restricted Area Provision** negotiable for New Development/Conversion (not typically for COO/re-licensing)
- Boundaries vary (radius, political subdivisions, streets/highways)
- **Restrictive Period** shorter than franchise term, usually tied to years from approval date
- May reduce in geographic scope after agreed period

### Carve-Outs from Restricted Area
Even if granted, the Restricted Area does NOT protect against:
- Pre-existing hotels (open, under construction, or approved)
- Other Hilton Worldwide brands (including Hilton Garden Inn, DoubleTree, etc.)
- Gaming-oriented facilities
- Vacation ownership/timeshare properties
- Acquisitions of chains with 4+ hotels
- Signia by Hilton (explicitly excluded)
- Strategic Partnerships (third-party distribution/loyalty relationships)

### Channel Restrictions
- Must use Reservation Service exclusively for outgoing referrals
- No relocation permitted
- No options or first refusal for additional franchises
- "No mechanism for resolving any conflicts" between franchisee and Network Hotels

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- New Construction: **23 years** from Effective Date
- Conversion: **10–20 years** from Opening Date
- COO: Remaining term or as approved
- **NO RENEWAL RIGHT.** Re-licensing at franchisor's sole discretion; may require materially different terms, new FA, PIP compliance. This is a "materially different" risk — you may invest $50M+ and have no right to continue operating after the term.

### Transfer Provisions
- **Permitted Transfers** (no change of control): public equity, <50% private equity, institutional fund transfers
  - Some without notice/consent; others with 60-day notice + $5,500 fee
- **COO Transfer:** 60-day notice, $150,000 fee, current form FA, PIP compliance, no default, transferee qualifications
- **Right of first offer** (30 days) on unsolicited bona fide offers for hotel or controlling interest
- May not transfer to Competitor or Sanctioned Person
- Mortgage/pledge: allowed without consent if sole borrower + hotel-only collateral; otherwise consent required

### Termination and Death/Disability
- **Curable defaults:** Payment (10 days), construction deadlines (10 days), insurance (10 days), other FA/Standard failures (30 days)
- **Non-curable (immediate termination):** 16 triggers including repeated breach, 3 defaults in 12 months, bankruptcy, loss of possession, 5+ days non-operation, contesting marks, adverse conduct, fraud, unauthorized transfer, Sanctioned Person, Competitor status, guarantor breach, public health threat, cross-default
- **Interim remedies:** During cure period, franchisor may suspend from Reservation Service, remove from directories, disable software, revoke financial accommodations
- **Death:** Transfer to immediate family member within 1 year, meeting current qualifications

### Noncompete Restrictions
- **During term:** May not own/be licensor or franchisor of competing brand. May own minority interest in competitor, be franchisee of competitor, or manage competitor property.
- **Post-term:** NONE. No post-term noncompete covenant.

### Dispute Resolution
- **No arbitration** (except Puerto Rico — AAA or JAMS, venue Fairfax County VA, seat NY)
- **Forum:** US District Court, Eastern District of Virginia (Alexandria). If no federal jurisdiction: Fairfax County VA or New York NY. Franchisor may elect to sue where hotel is located.
- **Governing law:** New York (without conflicts principles)
- **State overrides:** Multiple states override forum/governing law provisions (see State Addenda)

### Litigation History
**Pending (5 actions):**
1. AAAA Property Partners v. Hilton (M.D. Fla.) — breach of contract, termination of HGI franchise
2. In re Extended Stay Hotel Antitrust Litigation (N.D. Cal.) — class action, IDeaS software price-fixing
3. Hanson Dai v. SAS Institute (N.D. Cal.) — class action, IDeaS/SAS price-fixing
4. Ryan Segal v. Amadeus (N.D. Ill.) — class action, Amadeus data exchange, higher room rates
5. Portillo v. CoStar Group (W.D. Wash.) — class action, STR data exchange, higher room rates

**Concluded (4 actions):** Texas AG ($2.1M, guest fees), Nebraska AG ($300K, guest fees), Portland Hotel Ownership (mutual releases), San Pedro Inn (franchise terminated, no damages)

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### Overview
FPR is provided based on **199 Comparable Hotels** out of 242 total system hotels (46 company-managed, 153 franchisee-managed). Data covers 2023 and 2024.

### Room Rate Performance (2024)
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average Room Rate | $209.86 |
| Median Room Rate | $188.04 |
| Range | $112.15 – $645.25 |
| Franchisee-managed at/above average | 38 of 153 (24.8%) |
| Franchisee-managed at/above median | 64 of 153 (41.8%) |
| Company-managed at/above average | 27 of 47 (57.4%) |

**Key observation:** The large gap between company-managed (57.4% above average) and franchisee-managed (24.8% above average) performance on room rate suggests that company-managed properties (typically larger, better-located, resort/convention hotels) significantly outperform and skew the average upward.

### Occupancy Performance (2024)
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average Occupancy | 69.5% |
| Median Occupancy | 69.5% |
| Range | 38.4% – 98.4% |
| Franchisee-managed at/above average | 78 of 153 (51.0%) |

Occupancy is more evenly distributed than room rate, with roughly half of franchisee-managed hotels meeting the average.

### Index Performance (2024)
| Metric | Average | Median | Range |
|--------|---------|--------|-------|
| Occupancy Index | 104.1 | 105.8 | 56.6 – 155.6 |
| RevPAR Index | 104.7 | 107.9 | 52.1 – 282.1 |

Index values above 100 indicate the brand captures above its fair share of demand and revenue relative to competitive sets. The median RevPAR Index of 107.9 suggests that a typical Hilton hotel earns nearly 8% more RevPAR than its direct competitors.

### Critical Caveats
- **No cost data disclosed.** Room rate and occupancy are top-line metrics only. Without cost structure, EBITDA margins, or NOI data, investors cannot assess profitability.
- **Sample exclusions:** 43 non-comparable hotels excluded (brand/ownership changes, substantial damage, room count changes).
- **Company-managed skew:** Company-managed hotels (which include managed-for-owners properties with Hilton management agreements) tend to be larger and higher-performing, pulling averages up.
- **STR data dependency:** Occupancy Index and RevPAR Index rely on competitive set data from STR, which Hilton has not independently verified.
- **Substantiation** available on reasonable request but no obligation to disclose specific hotel data.

### Key Financial Observations
1. **Asset-light model:** The entity has virtually no physical assets. Its primary assets are accounts receivable from franchisees ($136M), intercompany receivables from Hilton affiliates ($704M), and franchise contract intangibles ($393M).
2. **Near-100% profit margin:** Operating income of $1.49B on $1.50B revenue (99.2% operating margin). Expenses are almost entirely credit loss provisions and amortization.

6. **Graduate acquisition:** $85M franchise contract intangibles acquired in May 2024 (15-year amortization), funded by intercompany note settled February 2025.
7. **Tax status:** Disregarded entity for US income tax; all taxable income flows to Hilton.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda to the FDD exist in Exhibit J-1 for **11 states**: California, Hawaii, Illinois, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington.

### Material Overrides by State

**California:** Liquidated damages may be unenforceable (Civil Code §1671). Termination/non-renewal subject to Business & Professions Code §§20000–20043. NY choice of law and VA venue may not be enforceable. General release excludes Franchise Investment Law claims.

**Hawaii:** General release does not relieve franchisor from liability under Hawaii franchise law.

**Illinois:** Governing law, jurisdiction, venue, and jury waiver provisions do not waive rights under Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act. IL FDD Act governs for IL licensees.

**Maryland:** MD law supersedes FA on termination/renewal. May sue in MD for MD Franchise Law claims (3-year statute). General release excludes MD Franchise Law.

**Michigan:** Standard MFIL protections: good cause termination, fair compensation on non-renewal (for terms <5 years), no out-of-state litigation, transfer only refused for good cause, cannot require release of statutory rights.

**Minnesota:** 90-day notice of termination (60 days to cure), 180-day non-renewal notice. General release excludes MN Franchise Investment Law. Liquidated damages prohibited. Out-of-state litigation/jury waiver prohibited.

**New York:** Franchisee may terminate on any grounds available by law. NY Franchises Law governs NY causes of action. General release on renewal/transfer may not be enforceable. Injunctive relief requires proper proofs.

**North Dakota:** Liquidated damages deleted. ND law supersedes NY law. Venue outside ND deleted. Jury waiver deleted. General release excludes ND Franchise Disclosure Act.

**Rhode Island:** Bankruptcy termination may not be enforceable. General release excludes RI Franchise Investment Act. Venue/choice of law restrictions void for RI Act claims.

**Virginia:** Termination without reasonable cause is unlawful (VA Retail Franchising Act §13.1-564).

**Washington:** WA Franchise Investment Protection Act supersedes FA. General release excludes WA Act rights (except negotiated settlements with independent counsel). Arbitration/mediation must be in WA or mutually agreed. Noncompete restrictions subject to WA earnings thresholds ($100K employee, $250K contractor). No-hire clauses between franchisees void.
