## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Brand-new concept with zero operating history.** LivSmart Studios by Hilton is an extended-stay midscale hotel brand launched in May 2023 (originally as "Project H3 by Hilton") with no open hotels as of December 31, 2024. The brand was rebranded to its current name on January 9, 2024.

2. **Franchisor entity structure.** Hilton Franchise Holding LLC is a Delaware LLC formed September 2007, wholly owned by Hilton Domestic Operating Company Inc., which is ultimately controlled by Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. (NYSE: HLT). The franchisor entity serves as the US franchisor for all Hilton-family brands.

3. **Ownership history.** Hilton's corporate genealogy traces back to 1946. Park Hotels & Resorts, Inc. (formerly HHC) was the original parent until a January 2017 spin-off. The current parent structure has been stable since 2017.

4. **Initial investment.** $15,424,593 to $21,497,662 for a 121-guestroom newly constructed extended-stay hotel, excluding real property, market studies, insurance, and interest. Construction alone accounts for $11M-$15M.

5. **Fee burden.** The ongoing royalty fee is 6% of Gross Rooms Revenue, plus a 2.0%-2.5% program fee, plus 2% Hilton Honors contribution, plus numerous distribution, technology, and quality assurance fees. The all-in fee load on GRR (before distribution fees) is approximately 10%-10.5%.

6. **No financial performance representations.** Item 19 provides no FPR. With zero operating hotels and no comparable brand-specific data, franchisees have no franchisor-sourced economic basis for investment decisions.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk — trademark.** The primary brand marks ("LivSmart Studios by Hilton" and "LivSmart") are NOT federally registered. Four applications are pending. This is a disclosed Special Risk with potential rebranding exposure.

8. **Financial health.** The franchisor entity generated $1.502 billion in revenue and $1.488 billion in net income in 2024. However, $1.5 billion was distributed to the Hilton parent, leaving only $317K in cash. All assets are pledged as collateral for Hilton's debt (maturing 2025-2033). The entity is a pass-through royalty collection vehicle.

9. **Growth trajectory.** 73 franchise agreements signed (all pre-opening) across 23 states. Two projected to open in 2025 (Indiana and Tennessee). Texas has the most signed agreements (16). The pipeline suggests significant developer interest backed by the Hilton brand umbrella.

10. **Territory.** Non-exclusive. No protected territory in the standard agreement. Restricted Area Provision available by negotiation for New Development/Conversion but is time-limited and extensively carved out.

11. **No renewal.** The franchise is explicitly non-renewable. Re-licensing requires a new application, new agreement (potentially materially different), and is entirely at the franchisor's discretion.

12. **Dispute resolution.** Venue is Virginia (federal or state); governing law is New York. Jury trial and punitive damages waived. General release required on transfer and re-licensing.

13. **Special risks.** Two disclosed: (1) out-of-state dispute resolution in Virginia/New York, and (2) unregistered trademark.

14. **System size.** Zero open hotels. Zero terminated, transferred, or non-renewed. No current or former franchisees exist. No gag clause signed.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Franchisees must comply with comprehensive Brand Standards covering all aspects of design, construction, furnishing, equipping, and operating the hotel. Key restrictions:

- **Brand Packages**: Must purchase FF&E from pre-approved Brand Package procurement agencies. Cannot source Brand Package items elsewhere. This is a significant lock-in.
- **Hilton Supply Management (HSM)**: Optional but promoted procurement agency. Charges 4-10% procurement fee plus freight, tax, and other costs. Late payment interest at 18.5%/year compounded daily. In 2024, HSM earned $8.76M from sales, $19.6M in rebates/allowances, and $38.7M in administrative fees from franchisees of all Hilton brands.
- **Signage**: Must purchase exterior signage from licensed vendors.
- **OnQ Software**: Must license proprietary HPMS software exclusively from HSS.

### Training Program

14 training programs covering brand operations, sales, technology, compliance, and ownership orientation. Key programs:
- GM/Commercial Leader Training: 18 hours virtual, up to $1,200/attendee
- Hilton Core Sales Skills: 24 hours virtual (2-6 weeks), up to $600/attendee
- Brand Conference: annual, up to $2,500/attendee (required for GM and DoS)
- Owner Orientation: 16 hours virtual, no fee (required for first-time franchisees)
- New to Hospitality: 20 hours virtual, up to $2,500 (for owners without hospitality experience)

### Technology Stack and Costs

Eight required technology systems create a substantial ongoing cost burden:
1. **OnQ** (property management, reservations, revenue management): $38K-$94K initial + $1,495-$2,989/month ongoing
2. **Guest Internet Access (StayConnected)**: $58K-$85K initial + $1,433-$2,633/month ongoing
3. **GRO (IDeaS revenue optimization)**: ~$3,400 initial, no ongoing fee
4. **Connected Room**: $29K-$35K initial + $529-$815/month ongoing
5. **Delphi (sales/events)**: Covered by Program Fee; optional licenses $858/user/year
6. **Digital Floor Plan**: $1,500 one-time
7. **Digital Key**: Included in OnQ
8. **Digital Payment (Adyen)**: $750-$1,500 initial + up to $175/month to Adyen

OnQ must be refreshed every 3 years; Guest Internet Access every 4 years. Refresh costs anticipated at or below original installation.

### Marketing Fund and Advertising

The Monthly Program Fee (2.0%-2.5% of GRR) funds various system-wide programs including reservation systems, directories, internet sites, advertising, marketing, quality assurance, and technology. Key points:
- **Not a trust fund** — franchisor has sole discretion on spending
- No obligation to make expenditures proportionate to individual franchisee payments
- Aggregate fees do not constitute a fiduciary relationship
- No advertising advisory council currently exists
- Spending breakdown not itemized in the FDD
- Quality assurance fee revenues in excess of actual costs supplement the Program Fee

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory

There is **no protected territory** in the standard franchise agreement. The franchise is a non-exclusive license to operate at a specified location. The franchisor retains unrestricted rights to:
- Operate, franchise, license, or manage any business under any brand at any location
- Modify the System
- License the System to others
- Use System facilities/personnel in Other Businesses

### Restricted Area Provision (Optional)

For New Development or Conversion (not typically for COO or Re-licensing), the franchisor may agree to a Restricted Area Provision that limits it from authorizing another LivSmart Studios by Hilton hotel within a defined area for a defined period. Key limitations:
- The Restrictive Period is shorter than the franchise term
- Exclusions include: pre-existing hotels, other Hilton brands, gaming hotels, timeshares, multi-property acquisitions (4+), and hotels under other brand names
- The "Brand" in the RAP specifically excludes other brands containing "LivSmart" or "Studios" and all other Hilton brands
- Strategic Partnerships (SLH, Autocamp, Evermore, Field Station) may operate in the Restricted Area

### Channel Restrictions

- Must use Reservation Service for all outgoing referrals
- Cannot register domain names containing the Marks
- All advertising must be pre-approved
- Cannot cross-market with non-System/Network hotels without consent

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

- **New Construction**: 22 years from Effective Date
- **Conversion**: 10-20 years from Opening Date
- **No renewal right.** The franchise agreement is explicitly non-renewable. Franchisees who wish to continue operating must apply for "re-licensing," which is entirely at the franchisor's sole discretion and may involve materially different terms, a new PIP, and payment of a $100,000 re-licensing application fee. This represents a significant "materially different" risk.

### Transfer Provisions

Two categories of transfers:
1. **Permitted Transfers** (no change of control): Publicly traded equity, minority private interests, institutional fund transfers — varying levels of consent and fees ($5,500 processing fee where consent required)
2. **Change of Ownership Transfers**: Require 60 days notice, $100,000 fee, new franchise agreement, PIP compliance, transferee must meet all current qualifications

No right of first refusal, but must notify franchisor of intent to market equity interests.

### Termination and Death/Disability

Franchisee cannot terminate. Unilateral termination is a material breach triggering liquidated damages. Franchisor can terminate for cause (with cure periods of 10-30 days for curable defaults) or immediately for non-curable defaults (16 categories).

On death of an owner, transfer to immediate family is permitted without consent if the family member meets all requirements within one year.

### Noncompete Restrictions

- **During term**: Cannot own, license, or franchise a Competing Brand. Narrow exceptions for being a franchisee, non-exclusive manager, or minority owner of a competitor.
- **Post-term**: None. This is franchisee-favorable.

### Dispute Resolution

- **Venue**: Eastern District of Virginia (Alexandria) or Fairfax County, VA state court. Franchisor may sue where hotel is located. Puerto Rico: mandatory arbitration.
- **Governing law**: New York
- **Jury trial waiver**: Yes
- **Punitive damages waiver**: Yes
- **General release**: Required on transfer and re-licensing (subject to state law limitations — see state addenda)

### Litigation History

Five pending actions, all involving Hilton Worldwide (not the LivSmart Studios brand specifically):
- Three class actions alleging antitrust violations through revenue management software (IDeaS, Amadeus) and data sharing (STR/CoStar)
- One breach of contract action (HGI franchise termination)
- Texas AG settlement ($2.1M) regarding mandatory guest fee disclosure
- Nebraska AG settlement ($300K) on same issue
- 1970 antitrust consent decree (Portland hotel supplier case) — historical

### Key Financial Observations

1. **Pass-through entity**: The franchisor entity is essentially a royalty collection vehicle. Revenue grew 9.1% in 2024 (to $1.502B), but virtually all income is distributed to the Hilton parent. The entity retains minimal cash ($317K at year-end 2024).

2. **Extremely high margins**: Operating expenses are negligible ($11.4M in 2024 on $1.5B revenue). The entity's operating margin is approximately 99.2%. This is because all operational services are provided by Hilton and its affiliates.

3. **Related party exposure**: $703.9M due from Hilton affiliates (55% of total assets). These intercompany receivables are settled through periodic distributions. The entity has no independent cash management — Hilton's centralized cash management system controls liquidity.

4. **Debt guarantee risk**: The entity unconditionally and irrevocably guarantees certain Hilton debt obligations maturing 2025-2033. All assets and franchise contracts are pledged as collateral. While Hilton Worldwide is investment-grade, this creates structural subordination risk for franchisees.

5. **Graduate acquisition**: In May 2024, the entity recorded ~$85M in franchise contract intangible assets from the Graduate brand acquisition.

6. **Deferred revenue growth**: $558.9M in deferred revenues (up from $524.2M) represents upfront fees being recognized over franchise terms, indicating continued new agreement activity across all Hilton brands.

7. **No provision for US income taxes**: Entity is treated as a disregarded entity for tax purposes; all taxable items flow through to Hilton.

### Material Overrides by State

**California**: Liquidated damages may be unenforceable per Civil Code §1671. Post-term noncompete unenforceable. Forum selection and choice of law provisions may be unenforceable. Release must exclude California Franchise Investment Law and Relations Act claims.

**Illinois**: Choice of law/venue provisions do not waive Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act rights. Jury trial waiver does not waive Act rights. Act voids any provision purporting to waive compliance.

**Maryland**: General release does not relieve liability under Maryland Franchise Registration and Disclosure Law. Maryland law may supersede termination/renewal provisions. May sue in Maryland for claims under Maryland law. 3-year statute of limitations.

**Michigan**: Comprehensive state notice prohibiting: restricting franchisee association, requiring waivers, termination without good cause (30 days cure), refusing renewal without fair compensation, requiring out-of-state litigation, refusing transfer without good cause.

**Minnesota**: 90 days notice of termination (60 days cure), 180 days for non-renewal. Liquidated damages provisions deleted entirely. Out-of-state litigation prohibited. Jury trial waiver deleted. General release does not relieve Minnesota franchise law liability.

**New York**: Release language does not relieve liability under NY franchise law. General Business Law Article 33 may supersede FA provisions. Injunctive relief requires proper judicial proofs.

**North Dakota**: Liquidated damages provisions deleted entirely. Out-of-state venue/jurisdiction deleted. Jury trial waiver deleted. Punitive damages waiver deleted. North Dakota law supersedes.

**Puerto Rico**: All disputes resolved by mandatory arbitration (AAA/JAMS). Venue: Fairfax County, VA. Seat: New York, NY. Federal Arbitration Act governs.

**Rhode Island**: Release does not apply to RI Franchise Investment Act claims. Out-of-state venue and choice of law void for RI Act claims.

**Virginia**: Franchisor cannot cancel without reasonable cause under Virginia Retail Franchising Act.

**Washington**: RCW 19.100.180 may supersede termination/renewal provisions. Release excludes WA Act claims unless negotiated settlement with independent counsel. Arbitration/mediation must be in Washington or mutually agreed. Noncompete covenant void unless employee earns >$100K (adjusted for inflation). No-hire restrictions between franchisees prohibited.
