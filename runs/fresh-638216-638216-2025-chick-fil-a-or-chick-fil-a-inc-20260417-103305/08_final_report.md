## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size and scale.** As of December 31, 2024, Chick-fil-A operates one of the largest quick-service restaurant systems in the United States with 3,109 domestic locations: 2,629 franchised Operator restaurants, 55 company-owned restaurants, and 425 Licensed Units. System-wide sales from franchised and company restaurants alone approximated $22.75 billion in 2024, making Chick-fil-A among the highest-grossing QSR brands per unit in the nation.

2. **Entity structure and IP ownership.** Chick-fil-A, Inc. is the franchisor, but all intellectual property — trademarks, patents, copyrights, and trade secrets — is owned by CFA Properties, Inc., an affiliate, and licensed to CFA for sublicensing. This two-entity IP structure creates a layer of separation between the operating franchisor and the IP holding entity. CFA Supply (distribution), Bay Center (lemon juice production), and CFA Servco (gift cards) are key operational affiliates. In October 2024, CFA acquired Truett's Group, LLC (18 legacy restaurants) from related party STC Brands for $67.6 million.

3. **Ownership and governance.** The Cathy family maintains control. Dan T. Cathy serves as Chair of the Board (director since 1979). Andrew T. Cathy serves as CEO (since November 2021). Susannah W. Frost was appointed President in October 2024. Donald "Bubba" Cathy serves as EVP and director. The board includes notable external directors: Cheryl Bachelder (former Popeyes CEO), Craig Menear (former Home Depot CEO/Chairman), Kirk Perry (former Google President), and Milford McGuirt (former KPMG Managing Partner).

4. **Investment range.** Total initial investment: $585,500 to $3,437,000. Notably, Chick-fil-A charges zero initial license fee — one of the only major QSR brands to do so. The wide investment range reflects the diversity of non-traditional locations (from small campus kiosks to large airport installations).

5. **Fee burden.** The license fee is 10% of gross receipts (7% for transportation venues). There is no advertising fund contribution for Licensees. No ongoing technology fees are specified (though CFA reserves the right to impose them). The fee structure is simpler and lower than the Operator program, which charges 12–15% base plus 50% of net profit plus up to 6% rent.

6. **Item 19 financial performance.** Two segments are disclosed. College/university units (312 units): median $897,932, average $1,395,745, ranging from $192,830 to $4,906,963. Hospital/business/airport units (97 units): median $3,152,764, average $3,451,788, ranging from $150,498 to $11,573,107. No cost data or EBITDA is provided, making profitability assessment impossible from the FDD alone.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk.** Approximately 10% of college/university units generated less than $500,000 in annual sales. At a 10% license fee, a $500,000 unit generates only $50,000 in license fees for CFA — suggesting very thin margins for the Licensee at these volumes, particularly given build-out costs that can exceed $1 million.

8. **Financial health.** CFA is extraordinarily strong financially. Total assets: $16.59 billion. Net earnings: $1.04 billion. Cash position: $1.41 billion. Audited by PricewaterhouseCoopers with a clean unqualified opinion. The company has significant debt ($2.83 billion long-term notes) but generates substantial cash flow ($2.39 billion from operations in 2024).

9. **Growth trajectory.** Licensed Units grew from 393 (start 2022) to 425 (end 2024), a net gain of 32 units over three years with 25 more projected for the next fiscal year. The Operator system grew much faster — from 2,230 to 2,629 franchised restaurants, with 183 projected new openings.

10. **Territory.** No exclusive or protected territory. CFA can open competing locations anywhere, including Operator restaurants, company-owned restaurants, Licensed Units, and potentially competitive concepts under different brands (Little Blue Menu delivery kitchen is in testing).

11. **Renewal.** No contractual right to renew. This is a critical structural risk — CFA can simply allow the license to expire and is under no obligation to offer renewal terms. If it does offer renewal, it may impose entirely new terms, fees, and conditions.

12. **Disputes and litigation.** All disputes must be litigated in Atlanta, Georgia under Georgia law (subject to state addenda modifications). The only material litigation disclosed was a delivery pricing class action settled for $4.4 million in 2024.

13. **Special risks.** Out-of-state dispute resolution is the only risk flagged on the Special Risks page.

14. **Gag clause.** Licensees have NOT signed speech-restricting provisions. However, former Operators HAVE signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly, which may limit a prospective Licensee's ability to gather due diligence intelligence from the broader CFA system.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

This FDD covers the Licensee program only — a distinct offering from the traditional Operator franchise program. Licensees operate Chick-fil-A branded food service at non-traditional "captive audience" locations including universities (the largest segment at 312 of 409 qualifying units), hospitals, airports, workplaces, and similar venues. The program has two variants: Self-Operators (who own or lease the Licensed Site directly) and Food Service Providers (who operate through a contractual arrangement with the site owner). Each variant has its own License Agreement (Exhibits B-1 and B-2).

CFA was founded by S. Truett Cathy, who opened the original Dwarf Grill in Hapeville, Georgia in 1946. The first franchised restaurant opened in a mall in 1967. The Licensee program commenced in 1992. CFA has never offered franchises in any other line of business.

### Fee Stack

The fee structure for Licensees is notably simple compared to many franchise systems:

**License Fee:** 10% of gross receipts, payable monthly by the 25th. Transportation venue units pay a reduced rate of 7%. "Gross receipts" includes all cash and charge sales except refunds and employee meals. This is a straightforward percentage-of-revenue fee with no variable components tied to profitability.

**Training Fees:** The first 2 management employees per new Licensed Site are trained at no charge. Additional employees cost $250 (2-day In-Person Experience only) or $400 (full 2-day + 5-day program). Training-related travel, lodging, wages, and workers' comp are the Licensee's responsibility.

**Equipment Handling Fee:** 8% of the purchase price for equipment or signage purchased through CFA, covering administrative costs of CFA's equipment vendor.

**Audit:** If an audit reveals an understatement of gross receipts, the Licensee must pay the underpayment plus 12% per annum interest. If the understatement exceeds 5%, the Licensee must also reimburse CFA's full audit costs.

**Indemnification:** Variable — Licensee must reimburse CFA for any claims arising from Licensed Unit operations.

**Late Payment:** 12% per annum on amounts 30+ days overdue.

There is NO advertising fund contribution, NO national marketing fee, and NO technology fee currently imposed on Licensees. CFA reserves the right to add technology requirements in the future.

### Initial Investment

The total estimated initial investment ranges from $585,500 to $3,437,000, broken down as follows:

|-----------|-----|------|-------|
| Opening Inventory/Paper | $11,500 | $65,000 | From CFA Supply or other approved distributors |
| Utensils/Smallwares | $10,000 | $35,000 | From CFA or approved suppliers |
| Equipment/Signage | $100,000 | $400,000 | May be purchased through CFA |
| Build-out | $300,000 | $1,886,000 | Highly variable by location |
| Architectural Drawings | $15,000 | $100,000 | Third-party costs |

The wide range reflects the diversity of Licensed Sites. A small campus counter may cost under $700,000 while a full airport installation could approach $3.5 million. Security deposits, insurance, and lease payments are excluded as they vary substantially by site.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

CFA imposes heavy supplier restrictions. Licensees must purchase virtually all food products, drinks, ingredients, packaging, marketing materials, and trademark-bearing items from CFA-approved suppliers. CFA has an interest in proprietary seasoning ($0.06/lb received from poultry suppliers) and proprietary seasoned coater (variable markup). Affiliate Bay Center produces proprietary lemon juice and may be the sole approved supplier in its service area.

CFA Supply, LLC (affiliate) is an approved distributor that may be the only primary distributor in its geographic area. CFA Supply served 1,107 restaurant locations in 2024, up from just 410 in 2022 — a rapid expansion of the company's vertical integration into distribution. CFA Supply's pricing includes a distribution fee and fuel/freight allowance, but the specific markup is not disclosed.

Total 2024 affiliate revenues from Operator/Licensee purchases: CFA received $1.78 billion from proprietary items and leases (19.63% of total revenue), CFA Supply received $1.99 billion, and Bay Center received $186.6 million. The national advertising fund also receives contributions from designated suppliers of up to 33% of Operator purchases.

Licensees should expect 50–80% of operating purchases and 30–67% of establishment purchases to come from approved or designated suppliers.

### Approved Supplier Process

CFA has not adopted specific criteria to evaluate alternative suppliers. Requests are handled case-by-case. CFA will provide specifications for non-proprietary items and consider alternatives, but reserves the right to designate sole suppliers for any item. CFA can add, change, or remove approved suppliers at any time. Using non-approved suppliers or failing to stop using disapproved suppliers can result in termination.

### Training Program

The initial training program has two components:

1. **In-Person Cultural Experience** (2 days at CFA Support Center in Atlanta): 15 hours classroom instruction covering CFA brand history, culture, values, customer strategies, SERVE leadership model, and a menu tasting experience. Plus 5 hours of on-the-job training.

2. **Virtual Licensee Training** (5 days via Zoom): 15 hours classroom and approximately 10 hours of on-the-job training covering quality assessments, back-of-house and front-of-house operations, critical success factors, non-compliance processes, product assembly, and action planning.

Certification is valid for 5 years with mandatory recertification. Training is offered approximately 8 times per year (2 per quarter), directed by Bill Dunphy with 30+ years of CFA experience.

### Technology Stack

CFA does not currently specify or require particular POS equipment for Licensees but reserves the right to do so. Licensees choose their own POS system and management PC. For reference, franchised Operator Restaurants typically use POS systems with annual support costs of $9,500–$20,000 for high-speed Internet, equipment support, and software upgrades.

CFA does not currently have independent access to Licensee POS data but reserves the right to require data transmission in the future.

### Reporting Burden

Licensees must submit: Weekly Product Sales Reports, Daily Product Sales Reports, and Calendar Month Sales Summary Reports. These feed into the license fee calculation based on gross receipts.

### Marketing Fund

Licensees are NOT required to contribute to any advertising cooperative or advertising fund. However, they must honor CFA-issued coupons (with potential food/paper/distribution cost reimbursement from CFA). All marketing materials must be CFA-approved.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory

**There is no protected territory.** The FDD states plainly: "You will not receive an exclusive or protected territory, express or implied." The License Agreement contains no exclusive grant, exclusive area, exclusive territorial rights, protected territory, or right to exclude, disapprove, control, or impose conditions on other CFA locations.

The only site-level protection is that CFA will not operate or grant others the right to operate at the "exact same Licensed Site" (or exact same retail area within a multi-tenant site like an airport) during the license term. This is a narrow protection — for example, at an airport, CFA could open a second Licensed Unit in a different terminal.

### Competition Risk

The Licensee faces competition from multiple CFA-controlled sources:
- Other Licensed Units in the geographic area
- Franchised Operator restaurants (the dominant CFA format, with 2,629 locations)
- Company-operated restaurants
- Truett's Group legacy restaurants (18 Dwarf House/Truett's Grill locations)
- Little Blue Menu (delivery kitchen concept with virtual restaurants, currently in testing)
- Any future competitive brand CFA may operate or license

CFA also reserves the right to use Internet, catalog sales, telemarketing, and other direct marketing channels without restriction.

### Channel Restrictions

Licensees may solicit and accept food/beverage orders from outside their geographic area (subject to compliance with CFA standards) but may not use unapproved distribution channels. This means Licensees can serve walk-in customers from any area but cannot independently establish delivery, catering, or e-commerce channels without CFA approval.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The license term is **not standardized.** It is "determined by Chick-fil-A prior to the execution of your License Agreement based on a variety of factors" including CFA's assessment of the opportunity, the nature of the site, the Licensee's contractual right to operate at the site, and CFA's past experience at similar locations. This creates significant uncertainty — a Licensee does not know at the outset what term they will receive.

**There is no contractual right to renew or extend.** CFA "may decide, but is under no contractual obligation, to offer you a new license agreement." If CFA does offer renewal, it may be on CFA's then-current terms (which may include new fees, conditions, and requirements) and may be conditioned on refurbishment/remodeling to current standards.

This is one of the most significant structural risks in this FDD. A Licensee who invests $1–3 million in build-out has no guarantee of operating beyond the initial term. The lack of renewal rights means the Licensee has limited leverage at term end.

### Termination

CFA can terminate with cause for both curable and non-curable defaults. Curable defaults include breach of material duties, damage to goodwill, hours violations, loss of site, regulatory non-compliance, and standards violations. Non-curable defaults include material misrepresentation, felony conviction, embezzlement, securities fraud, repeated gross receipts understatement (5%+ on two or more occasions), insolvency, and notably — operation frustrated by "the existence or declaration of a pandemic or epidemic." This last provision is unusual and reflects lessons from COVID-19, giving CFA broad discretion to terminate during public health emergencies.

Post-termination, the Licensee must immediately convert the site to a different use, pay all sums due, cease all CFA trademark/patent/IP use, return manuals, and comply with noncompete provisions.

### Transfer

No transfer is permitted without CFA's prior written approval. CFA has the right but not the obligation to approve any transfer. There is no right of first refusal or option to purchase. This means CFA has total discretion over whether the Licensee can sell or assign their interest.

### Noncompete

During the term, the Licensee cannot sell nationally or regionally branded chicken products or substantially similar products at the Licensed Site. This is a narrower noncompete than many franchise systems — it is limited to the Licensed Site only, not a geographic radius.

Post-termination, the noncompete extends 12 months at the former Licensed Site. The geographic limitation is again narrow (former site only). However, trade secret protection is permanent, and confidential information protection lasts 2 years (3 years for managers/supervisors).

Several states limit noncompete enforcement: California (likely unenforceable per Bus. & Prof. Code §16600), Minnesota (limited), and North Dakota (limited per state law).

### Dispute Resolution

All litigation must occur in the Atlanta, Georgia area under Georgia law. No arbitration or mediation is required. Several states override this: California and New York may prevent out-of-state litigation for resident franchisees asserting state franchise law claims. North Dakota requires ND law for ND claims.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

CFA provides Item 19 data for 409 domestic Licensed Units that were open for at least one full calendar year as of December 31, 2024 (out of 425 total domestic Licensed Units). The data covers only gross sales — no costs, expenses, or profitability metrics are disclosed.

### College/University Campus Units (312 units)

The median annual sales volume was $897,932 and the average was $1,395,745. The significant gap between median and average ($498K) indicates a right-skewed distribution pulled up by high-performing units. Only 40% of units met or exceeded the average.

The highest-performing unit generated $4,906,963 and the lowest generated $192,830 — a 25:1 ratio that underscores the extreme variability in this segment. Distribution:
- Below $500K: ~10% (approximately 31 units)
- $500K–$750K: ~13% (approximately 41 units)
- $750K–$1M: ~19% (approximately 59 units)
- $1M–$2M: ~35% (approximately 109 units)
- Above $2M: ~23% (approximately 72 units)

A prospective Licensee targeting a college/university campus should note that nearly a quarter of units generate less than $750,000 in annual sales, which at a 10% license fee and estimated operating costs could be marginal.

### Hospital/Business/Industry/Airport Units (97 units)

The median annual sales volume was $3,152,764 and the average was $3,451,788. This segment performs significantly better, with 46% of units meeting or exceeding the average. The highest-performing unit generated $11,573,107 and the lowest $150,498. Distribution:
- Below $1M: ~11% (approximately 11 units)
- $1M–$2M: ~33% (approximately 32 units)
- $2M–$4M: ~30% (approximately 29 units)
- Above $4M: ~26% (approximately 25 units)

Airport and hospital locations clearly drive higher volume, likely due to captive traffic patterns and extended operating hours.

### Critical Caveats

The FPR does NOT include cost data. The best source of cost and expense data is current and former Licensees. Individual results will vary based on location, geographic area, operational skill, management methods, and menu pricing. No assurance of any particular performance level is given.

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The Licensed Unit program shows steady but modest growth — +2 (2022), +17 (2023), +13 (2024). The Operator program is growing much faster — +108, +163, +135 over the same period. Company-owned outlets continue to decline as CFA transitions them to franchised Operators.

### Licensed Unit Activity (2024)

- Opened: 18
- Terminations: 5
- Non-Renewals: 0
- Net Change: +13

The termination rate is modest (5 of 412 = 1.2%). States with the most Licensed Units at end of 2024: Texas (63), Florida (31), Georgia (27), North Carolina (23), Virginia (22), South Carolina (20), Alabama (17).

### Franchised Restaurant Activity (2024)

- Opened: 210
- Terminations: 1
- Reacquired by CFA: 58
- Ceased Operations: 16
- Net Change: +135

The reacquisition rate is notable — 58 restaurants returned to CFA control in 2024. However, CFA also sold 86 company-owned restaurants to franchisees. Many reacquisitions are voluntary — Operators leaving one location to receive a commitment for a different restaurant.

### Transfers

Zero transfers in all three years (2022–2024) for both Licensed Units and Franchised Restaurants. This is consistent with CFA's business model where the franchisor owns/leases the real estate for Operator restaurants and controls all assignment decisions for Licensed Units.

### Projected Openings

CFA projects 25 new Licensed Units in the next fiscal year across 18 states, with 3 signed but not yet opened. For Franchised Restaurants, CFA projects 183 new openings with 38 signed but not yet opened.

### Gag Clause

CFA discloses that during the last three years, it has NOT signed confidentiality clauses with Licensees restricting their ability to speak openly. However, it explicitly states that "in some instances during the last three fiscal years, former Chick-fil-A Restaurant Operators have signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experiences." This distinction is important — while a prospective Licensee can freely speak with current and former Licensees, Operators (who make up the vast majority of the CFA system) may be restricted.

## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Auditor and Opinion

PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP (Atlanta, Georgia) issued an unqualified (clean) opinion dated March 21, 2025, covering the consolidated financial statements for fiscal years 2024, 2023, and 2022.

### Balance Sheet Highlights (December 31, 2024)

CFA's consolidated balance sheet reveals a massive, asset-heavy business. Total assets grew from $14.49 billion (2023) to $16.59 billion (2024), driven primarily by property and equipment additions of nearly $2 billion in 2024 alone.

**Assets:** Cash and equivalents: $1.41 billion. Short-term investments: $193.8 million. Total current assets: $1.81 billion. Property and equipment (net): $14.14 billion — the dominant asset, reflecting CFA's model of owning/leasing restaurant real estate. Long-term investments: $463.1 million.

**Liabilities:** Total current liabilities: $1.94 billion. Long-term notes payable: $2.83 billion. Long-term operating lease liabilities: $2.59 billion. Long-term finance lease liabilities: $1.75 billion. Total liabilities: $10.74 billion.

**Equity:** Retained earnings: $5.57 billion. Total stockholders' equity: $5.84 billion. The Company paid dividends of $37.6 million in 2024 and repurchased $162.0 million of treasury stock.

### Income Statement (Year Ended December 31, 2024)

Total revenue and income: $9.06 billion, up 14.9% from $7.89 billion in 2023. Revenue breakdown:
- Franchise fees: $4.50 billion (49.6% of total) — this includes a base fee of ~$3.21 billion (12–15% of sales) plus ~$1.29 billion (50% of net profit)
- Rental income: $1.60 billion (17.6%) — from leasing real estate and equipment to Operators
- Company-operated restaurant sales: $345.2 million (3.8%)
- Licensee fees: $76.8 million (0.8%) — the 10% of gross receipts from Licensed Units
- Product sales: $2.35 billion (26.0%) — primarily CFA Supply distribution and Bay Center lemon juice
- Other income: $189.0 million (2.1%) — gift cards, advertising revenues, investment income

Net earnings: $1.04 billion (11.5% net margin), down slightly from $1.06 billion in 2023 despite revenue growth, reflecting higher costs from distribution expansion, capital investment, and interest on new debt.

### Cash Flow

Cash from operations: $2.39 billion, up from $2.22 billion in 2023. Capital expenditures: $2.68 billion (reflecting aggressive new restaurant development). CFA issued $750 million in new senior notes in October 2024. Dividends paid: $37.6 million. Treasury stock repurchases: $162.0 million.

### Key Financial Observations

**Leverage:** CFA carries $2.83 billion in long-term notes payable, including a $750 million related party note (at 4.75%, 25-year term, maturing 2043) used to fund a stock repurchase from a related party. This related-party debt is secured by treasury shares. Total future lease obligations (operating + finance) exceed $6.4 billion.

**Distribution vertical integration:** CFA Supply's rapid expansion (3 centers in 2022 → 9 in 2024, serving 1,107 locations) represents a significant vertical integration move. Distribution/lemon juice revenue grew from $769 million (2022) to $2.07 billion (2024) — nearly tripling in two years.

**Asset composition:** 85% of total assets are property and equipment, reflecting the real-estate-intensive franchise model. CFA owns the land, buildings, and equipment for most Operator restaurants, leasing them to franchisees.

**Gift card liability:** The unredeemed gift card contract liability grew from $246.8 million (2022) to $305.6 million (2024), representing a significant deferred revenue position.

**Preferred stock:** In 2023, CFA issued 2,733,225 shares of Series A preferred stock (7% cumulative dividend, $196 liquidation preference) in a non-cash exchange for common stock. This restructuring appears designed for estate planning/family governance purposes.

## I. State Addenda Summary

CFA provides state-specific addenda for 13 states. The addenda modify both the FDD disclosures and the License Agreement. Key overrides:

**California:** Noncompete may not be enforceable (Bus. & Prof. Code §16600). Venue/forum cannot require out-of-state litigation for CA residents. Bankruptcy termination may not be enforceable under federal law. CFA must deliver all agreements with FDD.

**Hawaii:** 90 days notice of termination; 180 days notice of non-renewal. Registration filing required.

**Illinois:** Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act applies. Various amendments to Items 11, 17.

**Indiana:** Indiana franchise law provides termination/non-renewal/transfer protections.

**Maryland:** CFA must provide 90 days notice of termination with 60 days to cure. General release as condition of renewal may not be enforceable.

**Minnesota:** 90 days notice of termination (60 days to cure); 180 days notice of non-renewal. Transfer consent may not be unreasonably withheld. CFA must defend Licensee against trademark infringement claims at CFA's cost (if Licensee used marks properly). Noncompete enforcement limited.

**New York:** CFA cannot require waiver of NY franchise law rights. Forum selection limited for NY residents asserting NY claims. Additional cover page disclosures required.

**North Dakota:** Noncompete may not be enforceable. ND law governs ND claims. General release not required for renewal/transfer.

**Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin:** Various state-specific amendments to Items 11 and 17 per applicable state franchise laws.
