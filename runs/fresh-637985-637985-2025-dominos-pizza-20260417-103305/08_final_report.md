# Final Diligence Report — Domino's Pizza Franchising LLC
## FDD Filing 637985-2025 | Issuance: April 1, 2025 (amended July 30, 2025)

---

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System Overview**: Domino's Pizza is the world's largest pizza delivery company, a mature franchise system founded in 1963 with 7,068 U.S. stores (6,751 franchised Traditional, 25 franchised Non-Traditional, 292 company-owned) as of December 29, 2024. Internationally, 14,352 additional franchises operate through DP International Franchising. Domino's Pizza, Inc. (DPI) is publicly traded on the NYSE under ticker DPZ.

2. **Entity Structure**: The franchisor is Domino's Pizza Franchising LLC (DPF), a Delaware LLC organized in 2007 as part of a securitization transaction. DPF is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Domino's Pizza Master Issuer LLC (DPMI), which is indirectly owned by Domino's Pizza LLC (DPL, Michigan LLC) and ultimately by DPI. DPL provides all franchise management services under a servicing agreement. This structure means the contractual franchisor (DPF) is a pass-through entity; the actual operations, supply chain, marketing, training, and support come from DPL and its affiliates.

3. **Ownership History**: Founded by Thomas S. Monaghan in 1963. Monaghan sold controlling interest to Bain Capital in 1998 and retired from active management. IPO in July 2004. Now a widely-held public company.

4. **Investment Range**: Traditional Store $156,450–$743,500; Non-Traditional Store $107,450–$709,500. The wide range reflects variability in leasehold improvements ($25K–$350K) and insurance ($25K–$75K, potentially higher).

5. **Fee Burden**: The total ongoing fee burden is substantial: 5.5% royalty + 4% national advertising + up to 4% local cooperative (max 9% total advertising) = 9.5%–13.5% of Royalty Sales in fees. Additionally, technology transaction fees ($0.375/digital order), credit card processing fees ($0.0525/transaction), and annual technology costs (~$7,000/store) add to the burden.

6. **Item 19 Performance**: AWUS for 2024 was $26,467 (franchised), $26,120 (company), $26,269 (combined). Median was lower at $25,160/$26,264/$25,210. At the average, annualized gross sales are approximately $1.37M per store. EBITDA ranges from 1.7% for stores under $15K AWUS (490 stores) to 14.7% for stores over $30K AWUS (1,861 stores). Roughly 24.5% of reporting stores operate under $20K AWUS with EBITDA of 6.7% or less.

7. **Bottom-Quartile Risk**: The 490 stores with AWUS below $15,000 face a crushing 1.7% EBITDA — meaning annual EBITDA of approximately $13,000–$14,000 on ~$780K in gross sales. After debt service, owner draws, and depreciation, these stores are likely unprofitable.

8. **Financial Health**: DPF (the franchisor) is financially sound with $487M in revenue and $463M in net income on only $17.4M in assets — it is essentially a royalty collection pass-through. DPL (the service provider) generated $4.71B in revenue and $584M in net income but carries $5.0B in total debt against a member's deficit of $(3.95)B. This extreme leverage is typical of the securitization/recapitalization structure and is not necessarily a going-concern risk for a system generating this much cash flow, but it limits DPL's financial flexibility.

9. **Growth**: Net Traditional store growth of +151 in 2024, +164 in 2023, +127 in 2022. Development agreements nearly doubled from 168 to 323 over 2022–2024, signaling accelerating pipeline. Transfers grew from 443 to 550, indicating an active resale market.

10. **Territory**: No exclusive territory. The ~1 mile area of primary responsibility provides overlap protection only — franchisor can sell under different brands, adjust delivery areas, and operate through "other channels of distribution."

11. **Renewal**: 10-year initial term, 10-year renewal, but renewal requires signing then-current franchise agreement which "may contain other terms materially different."

12. **Disputes**: No arbitration, no mandatory forum. Law of the state where Store is located governs.

13. **Litigation Risk**: Active securities class action (Rhode Island v. DPI) alleging misleading net store growth guidance. Three derivative suits. Joint employment lawsuits. All pending as of filing date.

14. **Special Risks**: No state-specific risks disclosed. Michigan Franchise Investment Law provisions apply.

15. **Gag Clause**: None. Domino's explicitly disclaims confidentiality restrictions on franchisees.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

DPF is organized as a Delaware LLC specifically for the securitization structure. It earns franchise royalties and fees ($487M in 2024) and pays virtually all of it out — a management fee to DPL ($23.6M) and the rest as distributions ($478.5M). DPF retains minimal assets ($17.4M) and is not independently capitalized to provide services; those come from DPL. This means franchisee economics are ultimately dependent on DPL's operational capability, even though the franchise agreement is with DPF.

Franchisee qualifications are stringent: the Controlling Person must have been a Domino's store GM for at least 12 months (single store) or a supervisor for at least 12 months (multi-unit). This is an unusual requirement among major franchise systems — it filters for operational experience within the system rather than general business or restaurant experience. The Controlling Person must also own 51%+ of the entity and cannot have outside business involvement without written approval.

### Fee Stack

The total fee burden for a Domino's franchisee is among the highest in the QSR segment:

**Ongoing percentage fees (as % of Royalty Sales):**
- Royalty: 5.5% (weekly, via EFT)
- National Advertising Fund: 4.0%
- Local/Regional Cooperative: 1–4% (if 65%+ of co-op stores agree; franchisor can require minimum 2%)
- Maximum total advertising: 9.0%
- **Total percentage fees: 9.5% to 13.5% of Royalty Sales**

**Technology and transaction fees (per-unit/per-transaction):**
- Technology transaction fee: $0.375 per digital order (given that Domino's is ~75%+ digital ordering, this is material — at 50 orders/day, approximately $6,844/year)
- Credit card processing: $0.0525 per transaction
- PULSE annual enhancement: $780.26/year (max 5% increase)
- Third-party vendor PULSE: $1,357.04/year
- Help desk: $44/call, $28/chat
- Connectivity: $1,200/year (if no broadband)
- SmartRecruiters: $432/year
- Flex Client: $150/device

**Estimated total annual technology burden: ~$7,000+ per store** (per Item 6 footnote 7), not counting the per-digital-order transaction fee.

**One-time fees:**
- Initial fee: $0–$10,000 (often waived under incentive programs)
- PULSE initial license: $4,200
- Hardware: $15,000–$25,000
- Transfer fee: $1,500

**Other variable fees:**
- Customer Care billing: $20/$30 (punitive for excessive customer complaints)
- Audit expenses: cost + 1.5% monthly interest if >2% understatement
- Inspections: variable (travel, room, board of franchisor employees)
- Training: up to $1,250/session + $500 supplemental

### Initial Investment

The Traditional Store investment range of $156,450 to $743,500 is wide. The largest variable component is leasehold improvements ($25K–$350K), which depends on whether the franchisee is building out raw space or occupying a turnkey location. FF&E is substantial at $81K–$145K, and insurance is notably expensive at $25K–$75K (and may be "significantly higher" per the footnote). The three-month working capital reserve ($10K–$73K) does not include ongoing royalty, advertising, or inventory costs.

The Non-Traditional range ($107,450–$709,500) is only modestly lower, with the primary difference in FF&E ($62K–$136K) and insurance ($15K–$50K).

Key observations on the investment: Domino's does not require purchase of delivery vehicles, keeping the investment focused on the store itself. Zero company-owned store sales in 2024 exceeded the upper range. The optional DPD profit-sharing plan (10-year purchase commitment) may provide a meaningful offset to food costs for committed franchisees.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Domino's operates one of the tightest supply chains in franchising. DPD (Domino's Pizza Distribution LLC) is the sole approved supplier for PULSE software, fresh dough (screen and pan), and thin crust. There are only three approved pre-mix manufacturers and one approved gluten-free crust supplier. Getronics is the sole approved hardware vendor. All food ingredients, packaging, uniforms, and supplies must meet Domino's standards and be purchased from approved sources.

DPD's revenue from franchisee purchases was $2.6 billion in 2024, representing 55.7% of DPL's total revenues. An additional $141 million in technology, online ordering, credit card, help desk and miscellaneous fees was collected from franchisees (3% of DPL revenue). This means approximately 58.7% of DPL's revenue comes directly from its franchisees through supply chain and fee revenue — a significant economic dependency flowing from franchisor to franchisee.

The vendor approval process allows up to 180 days for evaluation, and the franchisor may charge for testing expenses. Product testing and new product rollouts may require exclusive purchase from DPD.

### Marketing Agreements

Coca-Cola is the exclusive beverage standard through December 31, 2030 (with volume incentive funding). Dairy Management Inc. provides dairy promotion funding. Mercury Fulfillment handles branded merchandise. Uber Eats partnership provides marketplace orders. DNAF received $25.3M and DPL received $6.7M from vendor/supplier marketing agreements in 2024.

### Training

Training requirements are distinctive: the Controlling Person must have 12 months of Domino's-specific management experience before even applying for a franchise. The Franchise Management School consists of four tracks: Admissions (45–75 hours in-market), Undergrad (3 days in Ann Arbor), Pre-Grad (45–75 hours in-market), and Grad School (2 days in Ann Arbor). Total training investment is approximately 95–155 hours of classroom training plus 1–5 hours of on-the-job training. Training fees are capped at $1,250 per session. Franchisees must train all their own managers and team members. The training director is Ella Meier, with the company since 2013.

### Technology Stack

All stores must run Domino's PULSE, which includes order tracking, driver dispatch, time clock, cash control, reporting, customer database, and commissary ordering. The GPS/Driver App is mandatory for delivery tracking. Additional Order Systems (e.g., Uber Eats integration) may become mandatory at any time. The Brand Technology modification cap of 1.5% of cumulative Royalty Sales (10-year lookback) provides some protection against runaway tech costs, but explicitly excludes initial PULSE acquisition and renewal-related requirements.

### Advertising Fund

DNAF spent $558.8M in 2024: 85.2% on media placement, 7.1% administrative, 2.2% production, 5.5% other (research, talent, PR, brand promotions). The fund is audited annually by an independent CPA. The DMAC (8 franchisees + 2+ corporate reps) provides marketing advice but does not have binding authority. The Roll-Up provision (in effect since January 2010) allows the franchisor to redirect up to 2% of local co-op contributions to the national fund. Company stores contribute on the same basis as franchisees.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory and Carve-Outs

Domino's does not grant exclusive territories under any agreement. The Traditional Store receives an "area of primary responsibility" — generally a 1-mile radius (½ mile in dense areas). The franchisor will not grant another store whose area of primary responsibility overlaps during the franchise term. However, this is protection against overlap, not exclusivity. The delivery area is defined by the franchisor and adjustable at the franchisor's sole discretion, based on "market conditions, population changes or other considerations."

Non-Traditional Stores receive only the premises as their area of primary responsibility — essentially no territorial protection.

Enclosed malls, stadiums, airports, theme parks, convention centers, and similar venues are excluded from all territories. The franchisee has the "first option" to service such facilities, but subject to franchisor approval.

### Channel Restrictions and Franchisor Rights

Critically, the franchise agreement does not prohibit the franchisor from selling under different brand names within the franchisee's area, or from distributing products through "other channels of distribution." This means Domino's could theoretically compete with its own franchisees through alternative brands or distribution channels.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial term is 10 years for both Traditional and Non-Traditional agreements. Renewal is for 10 years, subject to: written notice, no material default, substantial compliance, signing the then-current franchise agreement, maintaining or securing premises, and completing refurbishing or relocation per then-current standards.

The "materially different terms" risk is explicitly disclosed: the renewal franchise agreement "may contain other terms materially different from the terms of your original franchise agreement." This means royalty rates, advertising contributions, technology requirements, and operating standards could all change at renewal.

### Transfer Provisions

Transfer requires franchisor approval. The transferee must qualify as a new franchisee, cannot operate any other pizza delivery business, must sign the current franchise agreement, refurbish at the franchisor's request, complete training, and pay a $1,500 transfer fee. A Rider to Purchase Agreement is required at the time of any purchase agreement. The franchisor has a right of first refusal to match any third-party offer. At transfer, the parties enter into a mutual termination and release, subject to state law limitations.

### Termination

Termination by the franchisee is available only if DPF breaches and fails to cure after 30-day notice. The franchisor cannot terminate without cause. For-cause termination includes an extensive list of no-cure defaults (material misrepresentation, bankruptcy, abandonment, felony, intentional underreporting, health threats) and curable defaults with cure periods ranging from 48 hours (insurance) to 30 days (general).

The post-termination purchase option allows the franchisor to purchase for an amount based on a percentage of Royalty Sales during the prior 12 months, less deductions. If the store was open less than 12 months, the purchase price is the documented cost.

### Non-Competition

During the franchise term, the Controlling Person and all owners may not have any involvement in any similar business, anywhere — this is an unusually broad geographic scope. Post-termination, the restriction narrows to 1 year within the Store premises or 10 miles.

### Dispute Resolution

Notably, Domino's does not require arbitration or mediation and does not specify a choice of forum. The choice of law is the state where the Store is located. This is franchisee-favorable — the franchisee can litigate in their home state under their home state's laws.

### Litigation History

Three clusters of active litigation: (1) a securities class action alleging misleading net store growth guidance (filed September 2024, amended April 2025), (2) three derivative lawsuits alleging fiduciary duty breaches by directors, and (3) joint employment lawsuits where franchisee employees allege DPL is a joint employer. The concluded action (South Africa master franchise dispute, settled for $400,000 in 2015) is immaterial.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

Domino's provides one of the most detailed FPRs in the franchise industry, covering five years of AWUS data, median weekly unit sales, and EBITDA by AWUS tier.

### Sales Trends

AWUS for franchised stores grew from $25,264 (2020) to $26,467 (2024), a 4.8% increase over four years. However, this growth was not linear — there was a dip in 2022 ($25,554) before recovering. Median AWUS for franchised stores in 2024 was $25,160, approximately 5% below the average, indicating right-skew (high-volume stores pulling up the average).

Annualized at the 2024 average AWUS of $26,467, a franchised store generates approximately $1,376,284 in annual Royalty Sales. At the median ($25,160), annual sales are approximately $1,308,320.

### EBITDA Analysis

The EBITDA tier data reveals a steep profitability curve:

At the lowest tier (<$15K AWUS, 490 stores), EBITDA is only 1.7% of sales. Annualized, this is approximately $13,260 EBITDA on ~$780K in sales. After debt service, depreciation, and taxes, these stores are almost certainly unprofitable. Notably, 48.6% of stores in this tier achieved or exceeded this low threshold — meaning over half had negative or near-zero EBITDA.

At the $15K–$20K tier (1,135 stores), EBITDA improves to 6.7%. Annualized at midpoint ($17.5K AWUS), this is approximately $61,000 in EBITDA — marginally profitable.

At the $20K–$25K tier (1,426 stores), EBITDA is 10.1%. At midpoint, approximately $117,000 in EBITDA — a reasonable return.

At the $25K–$30K tier (1,350 stores), EBITDA is 12.1%. At midpoint, approximately $174,000 in EBITDA.

At the $30K+ tier (1,861 stores), EBITDA is 14.7%. At the disclosed average AWUS of $26,467 (system-wide), this tier's EBITDA would be substantially higher per store. The top tier represents 29.7% of all reporting stores.

### Variable vs. Fixed Cost Structure

Variable costs decline modestly with scale (80.9% at <$15K to 76.4% at $30K+), but fixed costs decline dramatically (20.2% to 11.4%). This confirms that fixed costs (primarily rent and manager salary) are the key driver of profitability differences. Stores with high-rent locations or overstaffed management face significantly compressed margins.

### Exclusions and Caveats

437 stores (6.5%) were excluded: 256 for incomplete/improper P&L submissions and 181 for not operating the full year. The EBITDA calculation includes Supply Chain Profit Sharing and Volume Discount rebates — these DPD program payments meaningfully improve bottom-line economics for participating franchisees.

### Siting Model

Domino's may furnish supplemental FPR data using the SIMMS siting model for specific proposed locations, store splits, or store realignments. This provides location-specific projections beyond the system-wide averages.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The Domino's system has been in steady growth mode. Traditional franchised stores grew from 6,222 (start of 2022) to 6,751 (end of 2024), a net increase of 529 stores (+8.5%) over three years. Company-owned stores declined from 375 to 292, primarily due to a major refranchising event in 2022 (114 stores sold, mostly in Arizona). Net system growth was +127 (2022), +164 (2023), +155 (2024).

Development agreements nearly doubled from 168 (end of 2022) to 323 (end of 2024), with 127 new DAs opened in 2024 alone. This signals a robust development pipeline and confidence in continued expansion.

### Transfer Trends

Transfers grew steadily: 443 (2022), 523 (2023), 550 (2024). The active transfer market indicates healthy demand for existing stores. The largest transfer markets in 2024 were California (101), Pennsylvania (55), Texas (51), Florida (44), New York (34), and Ohio (27).

### Closures

Store closures are remarkably low for a system of this size:
- Terminations: 5 (2022), 8 (2023), 2 (2024)
- Non-renewals: 1 per year
- Ceased operations: 8 (2022), 7 (2023), 6 (2024)

In 2024, only 9 total Traditional franchised stores closed out of 6,751 — a closure rate of 0.13%. This is among the lowest in the entire franchise industry.

### Projected Openings

For the fiscal year ending December 28, 2025: 190 projected new franchised stores, 6 new company-owned, with 20 signed franchise agreements where the store is not yet open. Largest projected markets: Texas (20), Florida (15), Ohio (8), Indiana (8), California (5), Georgia (5).

### Gag Clause

Domino's explicitly states: "We have not entered into agreements with confidentiality clauses that would prevent franchisees from discussing their experience with Domino's." This is franchisee-favorable and allows prospective franchisees to conduct thorough due diligence by speaking freely with current and former franchisees.

### Franchisee Organizations

Two independent franchisee organizations: Domino's Franchisee Association (San Antonio, TX) and Domino's Franchisee Forum (Elgin, IL). Four franchisor-sponsored advisory groups: DMAC (marketing), TAB (technology), OAC (operations), and SCAC (supply chain).

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda exist for California, Illinois, Hawaii, Maryland, Minnesota, and Washington. A general no-waiver/no-disclaimer provision applies to 14 states (CA, HI, IL, IN, MD, MI, MN, NY, ND, RI, SD, VA, WA, WI).

**California:** Fast Food Act compliance requirements added to Items 1, 5, 6, 7, 11. The $20/hour minimum wage for fast food workers (effective April 2024) with annual increases up to 3.5% is material for store economics, particularly for stores in the bottom AWUS quartiles where labor is already 40%+ of variable costs.

**Illinois:** Claims under Illinois franchise law excepted from any choice of forum or choice of law clause.

**Hawaii:** Standard Hawaii Franchise Investment Law disclosure language; no operative overrides.

**Maryland:** All initial fees deferred until franchisor completes pre-opening obligations (financial assurance required by MD Securities Commissioner). Releases at renewal/transfer do not apply to MD law claims. Termination upon insolvency may not be enforceable under bankruptcy law. 3-year statute of limitations for MD franchise law claims.

**Minnesota:** Forum selection overridden (no litigation outside MN, no waiver of jury trial, no liquidated damages or termination penalties). Termination requires 90 days notice with 60 days to cure; non-renewal requires 180 days notice. Transfer: franchisor will not unreasonably withhold consent. Release at renewal/transfer limited by MN Rule 2860.4400D. Interest/service charges capped at $30.

**Washington:** Washington Franchise Investment Protection Act prevails. Arbitration must be in WA or mutually agreed. Release cannot include WA Act rights except in negotiated settlement with independent counsel. Transfer fees limited to reasonable costs. Noncompete void against employees earning <$100K/year and contractors earning <$250K/year. No-poach restriction prohibited per AOD with WA Attorney General (October 2018).

## J. Unresolveds

1. **Securities litigation risk** (severity: medium): Active class action and derivative suits against DPI officers over misleading net store growth guidance. If Domino's loses or settles for a large amount, it could affect capital allocation and franchise system investment. Source: Item 3, pp.18–20.

2. **Securitization servicer risk** (severity: low): DPF's franchise services are provided by DPL under a management agreement. If DPL fails to perform, it may be replaced as servicer. This mechanism is untested. Source: Item 1, pp.10–11.

3. **Brand Technology cost ambiguity** (severity: medium): The 1.5% cap on tech modifications explicitly excludes initial PULSE acquisition and renewal requirements. New mandatory tech (Additional Order Systems) can be imposed at any time. The effective ceiling on technology costs is unclear. Source: Items 8/11, pp.34/39.

4. **Insurance cost uncertainty** (severity: medium): Insurance estimates of $25K–$75K (Traditional) may be "significantly higher" depending on state, location, and loss history. No maximum is disclosed. Source: Item 7 note 6, p.32.

5. **DPL leverage** (severity: medium): Member's deficit of $(3.95)B and total debt ~$5B. While cash flow coverage is comfortable (~4.5x), significant refinancing risk exists as debt matures. DPL's financial stress would directly impact franchise services. Source: Exhibit D balance sheet, p.303.
