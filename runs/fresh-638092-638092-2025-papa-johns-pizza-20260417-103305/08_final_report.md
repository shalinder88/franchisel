## Section A: Executive Snapshot

1. **Papa John's Franchising, LLC** is a Kentucky limited liability company (organized November 6, 2020), wholly owned by Papa John's International, Inc. (PJI, Delaware). PJF became the US franchisor on February 25, 2021, receiving all franchise and development agreements previously held by PJI. PJI had franchised Papa Johns restaurants since November 1991.

2. **System size** at year-end 2024: 3,291 total US outlets — 2,752 franchised and 539 company-owned (including 98 majority-owned joint ventures counted as company-owned). The system grew by +71 net outlets in 2024, accelerating from +40 in 2023 and +16 in 2022. Franchised outlets grew by 63 net in 2024, while company-owned grew by 8.

3. **Three franchise programs** are offered: Standard (10-year term, 5% royalty), Non-Traditional (5-year term, 5% royalty for venues like stadiums, airports, malls), and Small-Town (5-year term, 6% royalty for areas with fewer than 6,000 households). A Development Agreement is required for all operators, even single-unit.

4. **Total initial investment** ranges from $261,165 to $853,365 for a Standard/Small-Town restaurant, and $111,000 to $403,915 for a Non-Traditional restaurant. The midpoint for a standard in-line restaurant is approximately $560K, with average leasehold improvements of $335K and average equipment package of $170K.

5. **Fee burden is among the highest in QSR:** 5% royalty + 6% marketing fund (NMF Initiative through 2029) + 2% digital fee = 13% of Net Sales for a standard restaurant before any cooperative or local advertising contributions. When cooperative contributions become mandatory (post-2029 or if NMF repealed), total fees could reach 15%+ of Net Sales.

6. **Item 19 Financial Performance:** Average annual Net Royalty Sales for 2,878 standard restaurants (combined): $1,157,331 (median $1,101,782). Franchised-only average: $1,122,575 (median $1,068,262). Bottom-quartile franchise average: $697,195. Sales declined in 2024 — combined weekly PSA dropped 4.2% from $23,232 (2023) to $22,256 (2024). No cost data, EBITDA, or profitability information is disclosed.

7. **Item 21 financial health:** PJF is financially strong as a royalty-collection entity: $143.2M revenue, $108.0M net income (75.4% margin) in 2024. However, PJF holds zero cash (all swept to parent-affiliate PJUSA), total assets are only $37.4M, and distributions to PJI exceed net income in some years. Auditor: Ernst & Young, unqualified opinion, no going-concern.

8. **Growth trajectory:** Franchised outlet growth accelerated (+63 net in 2024 vs +31 in 2023). Terminations dropped to zero in 2024 from 49 (2022) and 32 (2023). However, 26 outlets "ceased operations — other reasons" in 2024, up from 1 in 2023, raising questions about classification. Transfers remain active at 106 (2024).

9. **Territory is non-exclusive:** 1.5-mile radius (0.5-mile in dense urban areas). Franchisor reserves broad rights to operate NT restaurants in territory, sell through non-restaurant channels, and unilaterally redirect online orders.

10. **Renewal carries "materially different" risk:** Must sign then-current FA, which may contain materially different terms. Must remodel, execute general release, pay $4,000 renewal fee, complete retraining.

11. **Dispute resolution in Kentucky:** Arbitration in Louisville; litigation in Jefferson County. Kentucky law governs. Seven states have addenda overriding some of these provisions.

12. **Liquidated damages are punitive:** Average royalty for 12 months multiplied by 24, equaling approximately two years of royalty on termination for franchisee breach. At the franchise average of $1.12M sales × 5% = $56K annual royalty, liquidated damages would be approximately $112K.

13. **Shaquille O'Neal endorsement:** $3.5M/year cash + expenses + $100K foundation donation. O'Neal also owns ~30% of a 9-restaurant JV in Atlanta.

14. **Gag clause present:** Some current and former franchisees signed confidentiality provisions restricting ability to speak openly, in context of settlement agreements.

15. **Heavy executive turnover:** CEO, CFO, CDO, CMO, and Chief Restaurant Officer all replaced in 2023-2024. Multiple hires from Wendy's (where Penegor was CEO).

## Section B: Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Papa John's Franchising, LLC is a pass-through entity whose sole function is managing US franchise relationships and collecting royalties. It has no operating restaurants, no employees (services provided by affiliate PJUSA), and no cash (all swept to PJUSA). The franchisor is structurally dependent on its parent PJI and affiliate PJUSA for all support, training, technology, and operational infrastructure.

PJ Food Service, Inc. operates Quality Control Centers that are the sole source for pizza dough and sauce. This creates a structural dependency — PJ Food Service's revenue from franchisees was $801 million in 2024, representing the largest single cost center for franchisees.

### Fee Stack — Every Fee from Item 6

**Ongoing percentage-based fees (standard restaurant):**

The royalty is 5% of Net Sales, payable monthly on the 10th. The franchisor may increase this to 6% if the then-current franchise agreement rate is at least that high. Small-Town restaurants pay 6% royalty with marketing fund contributions included. The Acceleration Incentive offers 0% royalty from opening through the Required Schedule Date (up to 12 months) for restaurants that open 30+ days ahead of schedule in 2025.

The Digital Fee is 2% of all Net Sales made through digital/online channels, including aggregator orders. Given that digital ordering typically represents 60-70%+ of QSR sales, this fee alone could add 1.2-1.4% of total Net Sales. The Digital Fee rate may be changed by the Marketing Fund Board.

The Marketing Fund contribution is currently 6% of Net Sales under the NMF Initiative (approved December 2023, effective April 1, 2024, through December 30, 2029). If the royalty is increased before 2029, the marketing fund rate automatically reverts to 5%. Non-Traditional and Small-Town restaurants pay 25% of the standard rate (currently 1.50%). The Development Incentive provides 0% marketing fund for 3 years for qualifying standard restaurants opened in 2025.

The Marketing Fund spent $188.8 million in fiscal 2024: 83% on media, 9% on production, 5% on research, 2% on promotions/PR, and 1% on administrative costs. No money was spent soliciting new franchisees. The Fund is a non-profit corporation with 4 directors (2 appointed by franchisor, 2 elected by franchisees via FAC); the franchisor has the tie-breaking vote on all matters.

Cooperative contributions are currently voluntary under the NMF Initiative (through December 30, 2029). During any future Mandatory Period, the minimum would be 2% of Net Sales, and combined Marketing Fund + Cooperative contributions are capped at 7% of Net Sales. Local advertising is also currently voluntary; during a Mandatory Period, franchisees would need to spend at least 8% of Net Sales on combined marketing (Marketing Fund + Cooperative + Local), with the local advertising component being the remainder after the other two.

**Combined ongoing fee burden (standard restaurant, current):**
- Royalty: 5%
- Marketing Fund: 6% (0% for qualifying 2025 openings for 3 years)
- Digital Fee: 2% of digital sales (effectively ~1.2-1.4% of total sales)
- **Total effective rate: ~12.2-12.4% of Net Sales** (before cooperative or local advertising)

**Fixed periodic fees:**
- Software Maintenance: $375/month ($4,500/year)
- Help Desk: $80/month ($960/year)
- Training: $150/year per restaurant
- **Annual technology/training burden: ~$5,610**

**Event-driven fees:**
- Transfer: $4,000 per restaurant to unaffiliated transferee; $8,000 total for affiliated
- Renewal: $4,000 (standard); $1,000 (NT/ST)
- On-Site Installation: $2,200 for 2-day standard; $1,100/additional day
- Alternative Supplier Testing: reasonable cost of inspection + actual test cost
- Papa Card Transaction: max 2% of card redemption amount

**Penalty/default fees:**
- Interest on overdue amounts: lesser of 12%/year or maximum legal rate
- Audit understatement: full cost of audit + 12% interest (triggered if understatement >5%)
- Management Fee: compensation + travel + $200/day per diem (if franchisor appoints manager)
- Liquidated Damages: average monthly royalty × 12 × 24 (approximately 2 years of royalty)
- Indemnification: unlimited

### Initial Investment (Item 7 Detail)

For a standard in-line restaurant in leased space, the total investment ranges from $261,165 to $853,365. The largest components are construction/leasehold improvements ($102,000–$365,000) and furniture, fixtures, and equipment ($77,000–$239,000). The franchisor estimates average in-line leasehold improvements at $335,000, with average architect fees of $33,000. The average tenant improvement (TI) allowance is $42,000.

Equipment for a 1,400 square foot restaurant averages $170,000, including ovens, prep tables, shelving, smallwares, phone system, Papa Card processing equipment. A free-standing conversion could cost up to $700,000, and new construction (extremely rare) up to $2,000,000.

The Information System costs $20,000–$30,000 plus ancillary services up to $8,920 (installation, support, help desk, GIS mapping). Restaurant footprint is typically 1,200–1,600 square feet.

Training expenses range from $1,000 to $30,000, covering travel, meals, lodging for a 7-week training period (may extend to 16+ weeks depending on experience). Additional funds for the first 3 months of operations are estimated at $20,000–$50,000, covering payroll for one manager and team members (excluding owner draw, royalties, and advertising).

## Section C: Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Papa Johns maintains one of the tightest supplier control regimes in QSR. Pizza dough and pizza sauce must be purchased exclusively from PJ Food Service. The Information System and Designated Software must come exclusively from PJ USA. All other products must meet franchisor specifications and come from approved suppliers.

PJ Food Service earned $801 million from franchisee transactions in 2024. PJ USA earned $70 million. These represent approximately 43% of total system revenues. The franchisor also received $5.6 million from designated third-party suppliers in 2024.

The approved supplier process requires written application, facility inspection, independent laboratory testing, and takes at least 30 days. The franchisor charges the franchisee for inspection and testing costs. Approval criteria are not disclosed to franchisees. The franchisor reserves the right to revoke approval at any time.

The Cheese Program is a voluntary pricing stabilization mechanism. Participants sign a Cheese Purchase Agreement (Exhibit G) committing to continue purchasing from PJ Food Service as long as the program has a deficit, or pay a pro-rata share of the deficit if they stop. Non-participants pay $0.10/lb more for cheese. Over the long term, program prices are designed to approximate market prices.

### Training Program Detail

Training is extensive. The Principal Operator program consists of minimum 2 weeks of station training followed by up to 14 weeks of Management Development Program (MDP) coursework in three sequential phases:
- **MDP Prep:** 12 modules covering shift management, cash, customer service, dough, food safety, inventory, opening/closing, technology
- **MDP Basic:** 12 modules at deeper operational level
- **MDP Advanced:** 13 modules at certified training stores, including P&L budgeting, labor management, business planning

A typical training week is 50–55 hours. All training occurs at designated Papa Johns restaurants (company-owned or franchised). The trainee bears all travel, lodging, and meal costs.

For multi-unit operators, Multi-Unit Management (MUM) training adds 3 weeks in an approved market plus 2 weeks in the local market.

### Technology Stack and Costs

The Information System is proprietary to Papa Johns and mandatory for all standard and Small-Town restaurants. It includes hardware, POS systems, back-office systems, inventory management, reporting, broadband connectivity, and security systems. The franchisor retains unrestricted access to all data and owns all data exclusively.

Annual technology costs: Software Maintenance $4,500 + Help Desk $960 + Training $150 = $5,610/year minimum, plus periodic installation fees ($2,200+) for upgrades. The franchisor may modify the Information System at any time and require franchisees to adopt new components within 120 days of notice, at franchisee expense, with no contractual limits on frequency or cost.

### Marketing Fund Spending

The Marketing Fund spent $188.8 million in 2024. The allocation heavily favors media (83%), with minimal spending on research (5%), production (9%), and promotions/PR (2%). Administrative costs were only 1% of the total. The fund is audited annually. The franchisor does not guarantee that spending will proportionately benefit any particular geographic area.

## Section D: Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition

Each restaurant receives a territory with a radius of 1.5 miles (0.5 miles in dense urban areas like New York). This territory is non-exclusive — the franchisor promises only not to locate or license another standard Papa Johns restaurant within the radius during the franchise term.

The Development Area provides temporary protection during the development phase. Once the restaurant associated with a Development Area opens and operates, the development area protection expires, and only the franchise territory (1.5-mile radius) remains.

### Franchisor Reserved Rights and Carve-outs

The territory carve-outs are extensive:
- **Non-Traditional Restaurants:** The franchisor may operate or license NT restaurants at suitable venues within the territory (stadiums, airports, malls, etc.). NT restaurants within territory may not offer delivery without mutual consent.
- **Other Brands:** The franchisor reserves the right to operate or franchise other food-related businesses under different brands within the territory, provided they don't sell pizza on a delivery or primarily carry-out basis.
- **Alternative Channels:** The franchisor may sell pizza and similar products through any non-restaurant channel (catalog, Internet, telemarketing) using the same or similar marks. No compensation to franchisee.
- **Online Ordering:** The franchisor defines the online trade area for each restaurant. This trade area may differ significantly from the territory, may change at any time, and may redirect orders to other Papa Johns restaurants (including company-owned and other franchisees) without notice or compensation.

### Channel Restrictions

Franchisees may solicit and accept orders outside their territory, but must operate only from the approved restaurant location. Relocation requires written consent. There is no right of first refusal for additional restaurants.

## Section E: Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The Standard franchise has a 10-year initial term with one 10-year renewal. Non-Traditional and Small-Town have 5-year terms; Small-Town gets two 5-year renewal options. Renewal requires written notice, remodel/re-equip, full compliance with all agreements, execution of the then-current form of franchise agreement (which may contain materially different terms), an approved location, payment of the renewal fee ($4,000 standard, $1,000 NT/ST), a general release, and completion of any training the franchisor deems necessary. The franchisor must still be offering franchises in the franchisee's state.

The "materially different terms" risk is explicitly disclosed and is a significant buyer concern. A franchisee who builds a successful business over 10 years has no guarantee that renewal terms will preserve the economic arrangement they originally agreed to.

### Termination Triggers

The franchisee has no right to terminate (subject to state law). The franchisor may terminate for cause, with a 30-day cure period for curable defaults (reduced to 72 hours for health/safety issues). Non-curable defaults include bankruptcy, unsatisfied judgments, dissolution, unpermitted transfers, criminal convictions, confidential information disclosure, repeated defaults, false records, failure to maintain insurance, impairment of Marks, public health danger, and government noncompliance (15-day cure for the last).

### Transfer Provisions

Any transfer of an interest in the franchise agreement, restaurant assets, or the franchisee entity (including the Principal Operator's interest) requires franchisor approval. Exceptions: transfers among existing shareholders require only 30 days' prior written notice. Transfer conditions include full compliance, qualified transferee, all amounts paid, completion of training, execution of current franchise agreement, general release, payment of $4,000 transfer fee per restaurant (unaffiliated) or $8,000 total (affiliated), and evidence of landlord and governmental consents. The franchisor has a right of first refusal (right to match any third-party offer).

### Death/Disability

The franchise must be assigned to an approved buyer within 9 months. Heirs or the estate may qualify if they meet standard franchisee qualifications and appoint an approved Principal Operator.

### Noncompete

During the term, the franchisee may not have any involvement in any competing business anywhere. Post-termination, the restriction narrows to 2 years within 10 miles of the restaurant or any other Papa Johns restaurant. Under the Development Agreement, the post-term restriction covers the Development Area or 10 miles of any Papa Johns restaurant.

### Dispute Resolution

Most disputes must be arbitrated in Louisville, Kentucky. Litigation, where applicable, must be filed in Jefferson County, Kentucky. Kentucky law governs unless prohibited by the state where the restaurant is located. Seven states (IL, MD, MN, NY, ND, RI, WA) have addenda that modify these provisions.

### Litigation History

Three matters disclosed. The most significant is the class action antitrust case alleging no-poach provisions in franchise agreements violated the Sherman Act. Settlement reached April 2022 but pending court approval as of FDD issuance. Settlement amount undisclosed but recorded in Q1 2022 G&A. The I&R Foods case (2023) involved a franchisee terminated for operating a competing pizza business, settled for $10,000 in March 2025.

## Section F: Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

Papa Johns provides a robust Item 19 disclosure based on actual 2024 Net Royalty Sales for standard (traditional) restaurants. Four tables and a 5-year trend chart are presented.

### Table 1: All Standard Restaurants (Combined)
The population of 2,878 standard restaurants (2,365 franchised + 513 company-owned) that operated the full year of 2024 generated average Net Royalty Sales of $1,157,331 and median sales of $1,101,782. The top quartile averaged $1,674,893, while the bottom quartile averaged $724,460. The range spans from $291,819 to $3,325,328. Only 44.2% of stores met or exceeded the overall average, indicating a pronounced right skew.

### Table 2: Franchised-Only
The 2,365 franchised standard restaurants averaged $1,122,575 (median $1,068,262). The top quartile franchise average was $1,641,017, while the bottom quartile averaged only $697,195. The franchised maximum ($3,056,001) was lower than the company-owned maximum ($3,325,328). The bottom quartile's annual sales of ~$697K at a 5% royalty yields annual royalty of only ~$35K — making the 13%+ total fee burden (~$90K) extremely challenging for profitability at these sales levels.

### Table 3: Company-Owned
The 513 company-owned restaurants averaged $1,317,561 (median $1,263,416), outperforming franchised stores by approximately $195K (17.4%). Company-owned stores are concentrated in major metropolitan markets (Atlanta, St. Louis, Louisville, Nashville), which likely explains much of the performance premium through higher population density and delivery demand.

### Table 4: Weekly Per Store Average Trend (2020-2024)
The combined weekly PSA peaked at $23,232 in 2023, then declined 4.2% to $22,256 in 2024. Franchised PSA dropped from $22,483 to $21,588 (down 4.0%). Corporate PSA dropped from $26,660 to $25,338 (down 5.0%). The 2024 decline reversed three years of growth and is not explained in the FDD.

### Critical Caveats
- **Sales only — no cost data:** Item 19 provides no information on food costs, labor, rent, or other operating expenses. Without cost data, prospective franchisees cannot determine likely profitability.
- **Exclusions:** Non-Traditional (170), Small-Town (151), and partial-year standard (66) franchised restaurants are excluded, totaling 387 excluded franchise outlets. Similarly, 26 company-owned restaurants are excluded.
- **Location bias:** Company-owned stores are in larger, more developed markets. Franchised stores may be in less favorable locations.
- **No same-store sales growth data:** The FDD does not present comparable-restaurant sales trends.

## Section G: Item 20 — Outlet Data

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The Papa Johns US system has grown consistently over three years:

| Year | Franchised | Company-Owned | Total |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| End 2022 | 2,658 | 522 | 3,180 |
| End 2023 | 2,689 | 531 | 3,220 |
| End 2024 | 2,752 | 539 | 3,291 |

Net growth accelerated from +16 (2022) to +40 (2023) to +71 (2024), driven primarily by franchised openings. The system projects 89 openings in the next fiscal year (81 franchised, 8 company-owned).

### Franchised Activity

Franchise openings increased from 73 (2023) to 90 (2024). More notably, terminations dropped to zero in 2024 — a dramatic improvement from 49 (2022) and 32 (2023). However, "ceased operations — other reasons" spiked to 26 (up from 1 in 2023), raising the question of whether some closures that would have been classified as terminations were instead categorized differently. The 26 ceased-operations stores were distributed across 12 states, with the largest concentrations in Indiana (4), Texas (4), South Carolina (3), Arizona (2), Delaware (2), and Hawaii (2).

Non-renewals have been zero in all three years, which is unusual for a system of this size and suggests either high renewal rates or structural factors that prevent non-renewals.

### Transfer Trends

Transfers totaled 252 (2022), 74 (2023), and 106 (2024). The 2022 spike was driven by large multi-unit transactions in Arizona (45), California (31), Ohio (26), and Pennsylvania (24). Transfer activity is a normal part of a mature franchise system. The 2024 volume (106) suggests continued market activity, with concentrations in Pennsylvania (23), Wisconsin (12), Delaware (15), and California (11).

### Company-Owned Activity

The company-owned portfolio contracted sharply in 2022 when 90 stores were sold in Texas (major refranchising). It then stabilized and grew modestly (+9 in 2023, +8 in 2024). Wisconsin saw significant restructuring: 9 stores reacquired from franchisees in 2023, then 15 sold back to franchisees in 2024 (along with 4 new company openings).

### Projected Openings

The system projects 81 franchised openings (14 Small-Town + 59 Traditional + 8 Non-Traditional) and 8 company-owned openings. Top projected markets: Florida (14 franchise + 2 company), Texas (11 franchise), Pennsylvania (9 franchise), and North Carolina (4 franchise + 2 company).

### Confidentiality Clause Flag

**Gag clause is present.** The FDD states: "In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with Papa Johns." The context is settlement agreements requiring mutual confidentiality and non-disparagement. Prospective franchisees should be aware that not all current or former franchisees will be willing or able to discuss their experience.

### Franchisee Organizations

Two organizations are disclosed:
1. **FAC (Franchise Advisory Council):** Created by the franchisor. Members elected by franchisees, with 3 members appointed by the franchisor.
2. **PJFA (Papa John's Franchise Association, Inc.):** Independent Iowa corporation. Located at PO Box 11035, Columbia, SC 29211. Phone: 803-252-7128.

## Section H: Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Auditor and Opinion

Ernst & Young LLP (Louisville, KY) issued an unqualified opinion dated March 28, 2025 on the financial statements of Papa John's Franchising, LLC for the three fiscal years ended December 29, 2024, December 31, 2023, and December 25, 2022. No going-concern language is present.

### Balance Sheet Analysis

PJF's balance sheet is unusually simple for a franchisor of this size. Total assets were $37.4 million at year-end 2024 (down from $39.4 million in 2023). The asset composition is revealing:

**Current assets ($14.7M):** Entirely accounts receivable from franchisees for royalties. Cash is zero — all cash is swept daily to PJUSA under a centralized cash management arrangement. The allowance for credit losses was $321K (down from $366K), representing less than 2.2% of gross receivables — indicating generally healthy franchisee payment behavior.

**Non-current assets ($22.7M):** Advances to related parties ($7.7M) represent excess cash flows above distributions, parked at PJUSA. The related party notes receivable ($15M) is an interest-free promissory note to PJUSA due the earlier of written demand or December 31, 2030, with a 10.25% default penalty rate.

**Liabilities ($17.2M):** Nearly all deferred revenue — $2.4M current and $14.8M non-current. These represent deferred franchise fees and royalty streams from refranchising transactions. Notable items include an $11.4M unearned royalty stream from 2024 and 2022 refranchising transactions ($2,603K and $12,150K respectively), being recognized over 10-year franchise agreement terms. Contract liabilities (deferred royalty, franchise fees, and area development fees) totaled $17.2M as of year-end 2024.

**Member's equity ($20.2M):** Paid-in capital is $15.1M (unchanged since inception). Retained earnings are only $5.1M, reflecting the pattern of distributing nearly all earnings to PJI.

### Income Statement Analysis

PJF is essentially a royalty-collection entity with a 75% net margin:

Revenue is overwhelmingly royalty fees ($141.9M, 99.1% of total). Franchise fees contributed only $1.3M. Total revenue declined 3.7% from $148.8M (2023) to $143.2M (2024), reflecting the sales decline observed in Item 19 weekly PSA data.

The most significant line item change was the 136% increase in G&A expenses from $14.8M (2023) to $34.9M (2024). The financial statement notes explain this as "updated internal cost allocations resulting in higher allocated fees under the franchise supervision and support services agreement" with PJUSA. The PJUSA franchise supervision fee was $33.9M in 2024 versus $14.1M in 2023. The trademark license fee was $803K (2024) versus $773K (2023). This reallocation likely represents a more aggressive charging of parent-company overhead to the franchising entity, reducing PJF's distributable income.

Net income was $108.0M (2024), down from $133.5M (2023) and $127.7M (2022). The decline is split roughly evenly between lower revenue (~$5.6M) and higher G&A (~$20.1M).

### Cash Flow and Distribution Analysis

PJF's cash flow statement reveals that all operating cash ($112M in 2024) is immediately advanced to PJUSA under the centralized cash management arrangement. There are no investing activities (other than inter-company advances) and no financing activities. PJF holds zero cash at all times.

Member distributions to PJI were $111M (2024), $132M (2023), and $128M (2022). In 2024 and 2022, distributions exceeded net income, eroding retained earnings. The distributions represent forgiveness of the advances to related parties — essentially, cash flows to PJUSA, then a subset is formally distributed to PJI through equity, with the remainder staying as an inter-company advance.

### Key Financial Observations

1. **PJF is a pass-through entity.** It has no employees, no cash, no operating assets. Its sole function is collecting royalties and franchise fees. All infrastructure is provided by affiliates.

2. **The G&A reallocation is notable.** The $20M increase in allocated PJUSA fees may indicate the parent group is extracting more economic value from the franchising entity, which could have implications if PJF ever needed to operate independently.

3. **The $15M interest-free note to PJUSA** is essentially a permanent loan to the parent group with no economic return to PJF. The 10.25% default penalty only applies if the note is in default.

4. **The deferred revenue structure** from refranchising ($11.4M in unearned royalty streams) suggests the franchisor captures long-term value when selling company stores to franchisees by embedding below-market royalty commitments that are recognized as revenue over the 10-year franchise term.

5. **Tax structure:** PJF is a disregarded entity for tax purposes. All income is reported on PJI's consolidated return. This means PJF's $108M net income flows up to PJI and is taxed at PJI's effective rate.

## Section I: State Addenda Summary

Seven states have addenda in Exhibit P:

### Illinois (pp.490–501)
- **Fee deferral:** All initial fees deferred until 30 days after the close of the fiscal Period in which the restaurant opens. This was "imposed due to Franchisor's financial condition" — a significant disclosure that raises questions about PJF's standalone financial viability.
- **Forum/venue override:** Forum designation voided under Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act §4, though arbitration in KY may be permitted.
- **Choice of law:** Illinois law applies if IFDA jurisdictional requirements are met.
- **Renewal compensation:** If franchisor refuses to renew, must compensate franchisee as required by IFDA §20.
- **Anti-waiver:** No provision may bind franchisee to waive IFDA compliance.

### Maryland (pp.502–510)
- **General release:** Releases required as a condition of renewal, sale, or assignment do not apply to claims under the Maryland Franchise Registration and Disclosure Law.
- **Termination good cause:** If inconsistent with Maryland law, the law applies.
- **Anti-waiver protections.**

### Minnesota (pp.511–519)
- **Noncompete limitations:** Post-term noncompete limited by Minnesota law. Covenant not to compete unreasonable to extent it conflicts with Minn. Stat. §80C.21.
- **Forum override:** Cannot require litigation outside Minnesota under Minn. Stat. §80C.21.
- **Choice of law:** Minnesota law applies to extent required.
- **General release:** May not require release that would relieve franchisor from liability imposed by Minn. Stat. §§80C.01–80C.22.
- **Anti-waiver:** No provision waives compliance with Minnesota Franchise Law.

### New York (pp.520–534)
- **Additional risk factors:** Detailed disclosures required by NY GBL §§680-695.
- **Prior franchisee litigation:** Must disclose if any existing or former franchisee has filed an action against the franchisor.
- **General release:** Not required to the extent prohibited by NY law.
- **Anti-waiver protections.**
- **NY prospectus required** for prospective franchisees domiciled in or opening in NY.

### North Dakota (pp.535–543)
- **Noncompete limitations:** Post-term restrictive covenant limited by ND law.
- **Forum/venue override:** Cannot require out-of-state litigation or arbitration if contrary to ND law.
- **Choice of law:** ND law applies if required.
- **General release exclusion.**

### Rhode Island (pp.544–551)
- **Forum override:** Litigation must be permissible in Rhode Island under RI Franchise Investment Act.
- **Choice of law:** RI law applies to extent required.
- **General release exclusion.**

### Washington (pp.553–565)
- **Release of claims:** Limitations on franchisee releases.
- **Forum selection:** Washington law may override out-of-state forum requirements.
- **Noncompete limits:** Subject to Washington law.
- **Broker advisory:** Detailed disclosure about franchise broker obligations under WA Franchise Investment Protection Act.
- **Material deviation:** Franchisor represents no material deviation between FDD and agreements.
