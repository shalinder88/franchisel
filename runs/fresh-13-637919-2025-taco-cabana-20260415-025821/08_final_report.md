## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size**: 146 total units at FY2024 end — 6 franchised (all New Mexico), 140 company-owned (all Texas). The system is shrinking: net loss of 3 units in FY2024 (4 company closures vs. 1 company opening), and zero new franchise units opened in any of the last three fiscal years.

2. **Entity structure**: Taco Cabana Franchising, Inc. is a Texas corporation formed January 17, 2023, wholly owned by Taco Cabana, Inc. (Delaware, 1991). The franchisor does not operate any restaurants. An affiliate, Texas Taco Cabana, L.P., operates the 140 company units and owns all IP. A second affiliate, NTG Franchising, LLC (Nick the Greek), operates under the same CEO (Anil Yadav) and shares corporate infrastructure.

3. **Ownership history**: Taco Cabana was founded in San Antonio in 1978. Anil Yadav/Yadav Enterprises acquired the brand (timing not specified in FDD but Yadav has been CEO since Jan 2023). The franchising entity was spun out in 2023 specifically to franchise the concept. The existing 6 NM franchisees (Taco Cabana of New Mexico, Inc., Melvin & Mary Sloan) predate the current franchising entity.

4. **Investment range**: $1,266,500–$2,776,200 for a single unit; $1,334,500–$2,684,000 for the first unit under a 5-unit Development Agreement. Does not include land acquisition.

5. **Fee burden**: 5% royalty + 3% national brand fund + 1% local marketing = 9% of Gross Sales, plus $1,840/month technology fee. At median gross sales of $1.45M, the annual fee burden is approximately $130K in percentage-based fees plus $22K in tech fees = ~$152K/year.

6. **Item 19 highlights**: Average gross sales across 143 company units: $1,490,385. Top quartile averages $2.07M. Median is $1.45M. Bottom unit does only $504K. COGS runs 28.6%, labor 31.5% — combined 60.1% of revenue before rent, G&A, franchise fees, and other costs. No EBITDA or profit data disclosed. No franchisee data included.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk**: The implied bottom-quartile average gross sales of ~$940K would leave approximately $375K after COGS and labor to cover rent, franchise fees (~$85K at 9%), insurance, utilities, and all other costs — an extremely tight margin. The absolute bottom unit at $504K with 54.1% labor alone appears unviable.

8. **Financial health of franchisor**: The franchising entity is very small — $785K total assets, $895K revenue. It generated $713K net income in FY2024 but advanced $757K to a related party in the same year and another $700K post-year-end. The entity appears to serve partly as a cash funnel to affiliated entities. Going-concern risk is mitigated by parent support but the franchisor's independent financial capacity is minimal.

9. **Growth trajectory**: System is in modest decline. No franchise sales momentum — zero new franchise units in 3 years. Only 1 projected company opening for next year. No franchise openings projected.

10. **Territory**: Protected Area exists but is narrowly defined (4 blocks in urban areas) with extensive carve-outs for ghost kitchens, captive markets, and Nick the Greek affiliate competition.

11. **Renewal**: Materially different terms explicitly acknowledged — "the terms of which likely will differ materially from your original Franchise Agreement, including, without limitation, those relating to royalty fees and advertising obligations."

12. **Disputes**: Texas law, Texas forum. No arbitration. Out-of-state litigation risk flagged as special state-mandated risk.

13. **Special risks**: CEO attention divided across Yadav Enterprises (multi-brand, 19 states), NTG Franchising, and Taco Cabana. Related-party cash movements are significant relative to entity size.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Taco Cabana Franchising, Inc. was created in January 2023 as a dedicated franchising vehicle. It has never operated a restaurant. The operational parent, Taco Cabana, Inc., was founded in 1978 and traces through various corporate iterations to the current Yadav Enterprises ownership. The IP is held by Texas Taco Cabana, L.P. and licensed to the franchisor under a 10-year, royalty-free, auto-renewing license agreement. If the IP license terminates, the IP holder is contractually obligated to honor existing franchise rights.

The franchisor offers two paths: a single-unit Franchise Agreement or a multi-unit Development Agreement. The DA requires a mutually agreed Development Area and a binding Development Schedule. The standard Franchise Agreement is attached as Exhibit C (approximately 60 pages).

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees:**
- Standalone Initial Franchise Fee: $45,000 (non-refundable, fully earned at signing)
- DA first unit: $45,000
- DA subsequent units: $35,000 each
- Development Fee: $10,000 per subsequent unit (non-refundable, intended to compensate for forgone opportunities)

No Initial Franchise Fees or Development Fees were collected in the fiscal year ending September 30, 2024.

**Ongoing Fees (Percentage-Based):**
- Royalty: 5% of Gross Sales, paid weekly via mandatory EFT
- National Brand Fund: 3% of Gross Sales, paid weekly with royalty
- Local Marketing: 1% of Gross Sales, spent on pre-approved local advertising

Combined ongoing percentage: 9% of Gross Sales.

**Fixed/Event-Driven Fees:**
- Technology Fee: $1,840/month (adjustable with 30 days notice)
- Transfer Fee: $10,000 (DA per undeveloped location; FA flat)
- Additional Training: $250/day/trainer
- Refresher Training: $250/day/trainer
- Additional Assistance: $500/day/franchisor employee
- Audit/Inspection: Full cost (triggered by reporting failures or >2% understatement)

- QA Audit Failure: All re-audit costs
- POS Updates: $0–$5,000/year
- Interest on late payments: Lesser of max legal rate or 18% per annum
- Late Fee: $100 per occurrence
- New Product/Supplier Testing: Actual cost (whether or not approved)

**Economic Impact at Median Sales ($1,451,946):**

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Franchisees may only sell products/brands designated in the Manual. All food and non-food products must meet franchisor specifications, be purchased from approved suppliers, or be purchased from designated sources. Approximately 90% of operating purchases must come from approved/designated sources. The franchisor reserves the right to become an approved supplier and to receive and retain rebates.

Currently, neither the franchisor nor affiliates are approved suppliers for any product. Three supplier rebates are disclosed: Pepsi ($1.1M), Dr. Pepper ($1M), and EcoLab ($20K) — all paid directly to franchisees based on purchasing volume.

The grey market prohibition prevents franchisees from leveraging group purchasing arrangements for resale outside the system.

### Approved Supplier Process

Franchisees may request approval of new suppliers/products in writing. The franchisor may inspect facilities, test samples, and charge the franchisee for the cost of evaluation regardless of whether approval is granted. Approval may be revoked after re-inspection at any time.

### Training Program Detail

The Initial Training Program (ITP) spans 7–10 weeks and must be completed up to 16 weeks before restaurant opening. Required attendees: the Operating Principal, the restaurant's general manager, and at least 2 additional managerial personnel designated by the franchisor.

Total training: 79 hours classroom + 415 hours on-the-job = 494 hours.

The training covers the full restaurant operation: cold prep, hot prep, grill, fryer, line serving, cashier, tortilla-making, cleaning, receiving/storage, alcohol certification, food safety, plus management-level programs (Shift Leader: 56 hrs; GM: 112 hrs; Franchise Operator: 80 hrs OJT).

No tuition for the first 3 attendees. All travel, living, and salary expenses are the franchisee's responsibility. Training location is San Antonio, TX.

If the Operating Principal fails to complete the ITP, the franchisor may terminate the Franchise Agreement. The franchisor may dismiss trainees deemed unacceptable and require replacement within 30 days.

For Development Agreements with more than 5 committed units, the franchisee must establish a franchisor-certified training facility (which may be an operating restaurant).

### Technology Stack and Costs

Required POS: Xenial POS System. Cost: $25,000–$50,000. Annual maintenance/updates: $0–$5,000.

Technology Fee: $1,840/month ($22,080/year), adjustable with 30 days written notice. This covers "various technology services provided by us and third party vendors."

The franchisor has unrestricted, independent access to all data on the computer system. Franchisees must maintain only data specified in the Manual, transmit data in required formats, give unrestricted access (including passwords), comply with PCI DSS, and may not install unauthorized software. Replacement/upgrade can be required no more than every 24 months.

### Marketing Fund Spending

**National Brand Fund**: 3% of Gross Sales. May be used for advertising, marketing, PR, promotions, market research, web/app, sponsorships, celebrity endorsements, SEO, and accounting costs. The franchisor may use the fund for administrative overhead related to Brand Fund activities.

**Critical fact**: As of the issuance date (March 25, 2025), NO Brand Fund monies have been collected. No Regional Advertising Funds have been established. Despite the 3% contractual obligation in the current FDD, the fund has never been active.

**Audit status**: No independent audit required. Unaudited statement available upon written request, no sooner than 90 days after fiscal year close.

**Local Marketing**: 1% of Gross Sales. All materials require franchisor pre-approval. Franchisor has 10 days to respond; if no consent received, material may not be used. Social media requires prior written consent; franchisees may not establish independent social media pages.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition

Under the Franchise Agreement, franchisees receive a "Protected Area" with the following dimensions:
- **Urban areas**: 4 city blocks in each direction from the front door
- **Suburban areas**: At least 30,000 full-time residents
- **Rural areas**: 1-mile radius from the front door

This is NOT an exclusive territory. The franchisor will not operate or license others to operate a traditional Taco Cabana Restaurant within the Protected Area during the term, provided the franchisee is in compliance.

### Carve-outs and Franchisor Reserved Rights

The Protected Area protection is subject to extensive carve-outs:

1. **Captive market locations**: Airports, train/bus stations, military bases, government offices, stadiums, arenas, toll roads, highway rest areas, universities, hospitals, amusement parks, museums, casinos, Indian reservations, master concessionaire locations, and "any other similar location we believe, in the exercise of our reasonable judgment, to be a captive market location."

2. **Ghost/dark/cloud kitchens**: The franchisor may operate or license delivery-only kitchens that use the Taco Cabana Marks and deliver to customers located inside the Protected Area.

3. **Wholesale/retail distribution**: The franchisor may manufacture, distribute, and sell products under the Marks through any channel other than a brick-and-mortar restaurant.

4. **Internet and metaverse**: Full reservation of online sales rights.

5. **Special events**: Sales at special events in the Protected Area are reserved.

6. **Affiliate competition**: NTG Franchising (Nick the Greek) franchisees and company units may operate in the franchisee's Protected Area. The franchisor may acquire competing brands with locations in the Protected Area.

### Channel Restrictions

Franchisees may only sell from the Franchised Location. No other distribution method or channel permitted. No geographic restrictions on customer solicitation, but see social media restrictions under Item 11.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial franchise term is 10 years from the restaurant's opening date. The franchisee may obtain one successor franchise term of 10 years, unless the franchisor decides to stop franchising or withdraw from the geographic market.

**Renewal conditions** include: timely notice; substantial compliance with expiring agreement; no defaults under any agreement with franchisor or affiliates; right to continue operating at location for full successor term; renovate and modernize to current standards; complete additional training; pay successor franchise fee (then-current IFF for new franchisees); sign general release.

**Materially different terms risk**: The FDD explicitly states that the successor Franchise Agreement "likely will differ materially from your original Franchise Agreement, including, without limitation, those relating to royalty fees and advertising obligations." This is a significant risk — the franchisee has no assurance of fee stability beyond the initial 10-year term.

### Transfer Provisions

"Transfer" is broadly defined to include any sale, assignment, pledge, mortgage, or encumbrance of any interest in the FA, the restaurant, the location, the lease, substantially all assets, or any direct/indirect ownership interests.

All transfers require prior written consent (not unreasonably withheld). Conditions include: compliance with all agreements; payment of $10,000 transfer fee; qualified transferee; modernization/upgrade; general release; reasonable sales price; transferee must complete ITP.

The franchisor has a 30-day right of first refusal on any proposed transfer (except convenience-of-ownership and spousal/child death transfers).

Upon termination or expiration, the franchisor has a 30-day option to purchase some or all restaurant assets at fair market value.

### Termination and Death/Disability

The franchisee has no right to terminate. The franchisor may terminate only with cause. Curable defaults get 30 days (10 days for monetary). Non-curable defaults include: failure to complete ITP, failure to open on time, insolvency, bankruptcy, material breach, misrepresentation, conviction, unauthorized transfer, cross-default, audit refusal, health/safety jeopardy, 3+ consecutive days non-operation, and 2+ defaults in 12 months.

On death or permanent incapacity: executor has 3 months to apply for transfer consent, 6 months to complete transfer. If not completed, franchisor may terminate.

### Noncompete Restrictions

**During term**: No interest in any "Competitive Business" — defined as any business whose Tex-Mex and Mexican-style entree sales are reasonably likely to account for 10% or more of food sales in any calendar month, and/or whose trade dress is similar to the System. Exception for pre-existing businesses listed in the agreement.

**Post-term**: 2 years, within 10 miles of the Franchised Location or any other Taco Cabana Restaurant. Also prohibited from selling/leasing the location to anyone intending to operate a Competitive Business for 2 years.

**State overrides**: California addendum notes the post-term noncompete may not be enforceable under California law. Indiana, Wisconsin, and other states have varying limitations.

### Dispute Resolution

**Governing law**: Texas (subject to state-specific overrides).
**Forum**: Franchisee can only file suit where franchisor's principal offices are located (San Antonio, TX). Franchisor may file where its offices are, where the franchisee resides/does business, where the restaurant is/was located, or where the claim arose.
**No arbitration or mediation required.**

This is a one-sided forum selection clause. The state-mandated special risk disclosure flags this as an out-of-state dispute resolution risk.

### Litigation History

No litigation disclosed in Item 3. No bankruptcy in Item 4.

### Analysis

COGS is remarkably stable across quartiles (28.3%–28.6%), suggesting consistent purchasing and menu pricing. Labor percentage varies significantly — from 28.6% at top quartile to 31.5% system-wide average, with the worst unit at 54.1%. This indicates that volume is the primary driver of unit-level economics: fixed and semi-fixed labor costs consume a dramatically higher share of revenue at lower-volume units.

At the median ($1.45M), after COGS (26.7%) and labor (30.5%), approximately $621K (42.8%) remains for all other costs: rent/occupancy, franchise fees (~$131K at 9%), insurance, utilities, G&A, maintenance, and debt service. This is a tight but potentially viable margin in low-cost Texas markets.

At the bottom of the system ($504K), only ~$75K remains after COGS and labor — clearly insufficient to cover any meaningful cost structure.

### Critical Caveats

1. **Company-operated data only** — Franchisee unit economics will differ. Company units benefit from shared overhead, volume purchasing, and institutional infrastructure that a standalone franchisee will not have.
2. **No rent or occupancy costs disclosed** — This is the second-largest variable after labor and is completely absent.
3. **Franchise fee impact not reflected** — Adding 9% franchise fees to company unit data would reduce the margin by $134K at average volume.
4. **Texas-only locations** — All company data comes from Texas. Franchisee performance in other states (e.g., the existing NM locations, or future expansion markets) may differ significantly due to labor rates, real estate costs, and brand awareness.
5. **Unaudited data** — Prepared internally by the parent entity.

### Key Financial Observations

7. **Cash drain trajectory**: Despite generating $637K in operating cash flow, the entity only increased cash by $63K because $574K net was advanced to the related party. This pattern, combined with the $700K post-year-end advance, suggests the franchisor entity is being used as a funding source for affiliated operations.
