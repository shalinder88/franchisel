## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size:** 2,369 US hotels as of December 31, 2024 (1,345 Hampton Inn + 1,024 Hampton Inn & Suites), all franchised, zero company-owned. This is one of the largest hotel franchise systems in the United States.
2. **Entity structure:** Hilton Franchise Holding LLC is a Delaware LLC formed September 2007, functioning as a pure franchise-fee-collection entity. It is wholly owned by Hilton Domestic Operating Company Inc., which is in turn owned by Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. (NYSE: HLT).
3. **Ownership history:** Hampton Inn brand first franchised in 1983; Hampton Inn & Suites first franchised in 1993. Predecessors include Promus Hotel Systems, Inc. (1995–2007) and Hampton Inns Franchise LLC (2007–2015). The current franchisor assumed this role on March 30, 2015. The Hilton family has operated guest lodging since 1946 through various corporate structures.
4. **Investment range:** Hampton Inn (89-room prototype): $15.2M–$22.2M; Hampton Inn & Suites (113-room prototype): $17.7M–$26.0M. Both figures exclude real property costs, which can be the largest component.
5. **Fee burden:** 6% royalty + 4% program fee = 10% of Gross Rooms Revenue as the base load, before adding Hilton Honors (4.9% of eligible folio), technology fees ($2,000–$4,000+/month), distribution and sales program fees, quality assurance penalties, and event-driven charges. Effective all-in fee burden is materially higher than 10%.
6. **Item 19 highlights:** Average room rate $145.82 (HI) / $152.19 (H&S) in 2024. Average occupancy 71.7%. RevPAR Index averages 121.0, meaning Hampton hotels outperform their competitive sets by approximately 21% on revenue per available room. However, only 33% of franchisee-managed Hampton Inn hotels exceed the average room rate, indicating significant right-tail skew.
7. **Bottom-quartile risk:** Room rates go as low as $77.60 (HI) / $86.35 (H&S). Occupancy can be as low as 30.5%. RevPAR Index ranges from 39.5 (severe underperformance) to 334.4. The median-to-average gap in room rates suggests a long tail of lower-performing properties.
8. **Financial health of franchisor:** The entity had $1.50B in total revenues and $1.49B in net income in 2024 — a 99%+ operating margin. It distributed $1.5B to Hilton affiliates. Clean audit opinion from CBH. This is an asset-light franchise royalty conduit, not an operating company.
9. **Growth trajectory:** Hampton Inn brand was essentially flat in 2024 (-1 net unit). Hampton Inn & Suites grew modestly (+9 net). Combined pipeline of 305 signed-not-opened properties and 52 projected openings suggests continued moderate growth, but the system is mature.
10. **Territory:** The standard franchise agreement provides NO exclusive territory. The franchisor retains unrestricted rights to compete under all other Hilton brands within any area. A Restricted Area provision may be negotiated but is discretionary, time-limited, and applies only to the Hampton brand.
11. **Renewal:** The franchise is NON-RENEWABLE. Re-licensing is at the franchisor's sole discretion and may involve materially different terms, conditions, and a mandatory PIP.
12. **Disputes:** Litigation venue is the Eastern District of Virginia or Fairfax County, VA (with fallback to New York). Governing law is New York. Multiple state addenda modify venue, liquidated damages, and termination provisions.
13. **Special risks:** Pending antitrust class actions related to revenue management software (IDeaS) and competitive data exchanges (STR, Amadeus) could have systemic implications for the franchise system.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure
Hilton Franchise Holding LLC is a single-purpose franchise entity that licenses intellectual property from its affiliate Hilton International Holding LLC (HIH) on a royalty-free basis, then sublicenses it to franchisees. The entity has minimal operating expenses and functions primarily as a fee conduit, upstream all net income to Hilton Worldwide via retained earnings distributions.

CEO Christopher J. Nassetta has led Hilton since December 2007. CFO Kevin J. Jacobs has been in role since September 2013. The board of Hilton Worldwide includes directors with backgrounds in consumer products, food service, government, and technology.

### Fee Stack — Initial Fees
- **Franchise Application Fee (New Development/Conversion):** $100,000 plus $400 for each guest room over 150
- **Franchise Application Fee (Change of Ownership):** $200,000
- **Franchise Application Fee (Re-licensing):** $100,000
- **PIP Fee:** $10,000
- **OnQ Computer System:** $27,590–$83,959
- **Other IT (GRO, Digital Floor Plan, Opening Tool):** $5,100
- **Training:** $5,000–$17,500
- **Opening Process Services:** $6,000
- **Procurement Fee (if using HSM):** 4%–10% of project cost

If an application is withdrawn before approval, a $7,500 processing fee is retained. In 2024, actual franchise application fees ranged from $0–$100,000 (new development) and $0–$200,000 (change of ownership), reflecting negotiated discounts.

### Fee Stack — Ongoing Fees
- **Monthly Royalty:** 6% of Gross Rooms Revenue (non-negotiable formula, but modified in 66 instances in 2024)
- **Monthly Program Fee:** 4% of GRR (may increase by up to 1% of GRR over the term)
- **Hilton Honors:** 4.9% of eligible guest folio (waived for on-property enrollments)
- **Hilton Advance Program:** 1.35% of Digital Direct Revenue (max $30/stay)
- **Technology (monthly):** OnQ connectivity $400–$600 + HW maintenance $1,114–$2,568 + Connected Room $417–$779 = approximately $1,931–$3,947/month in core tech fees alone
- **Brand Conference:** $2,500/attendee (GM + Director of Sales required, annual)
- **Quality Assurance penalties:** Non-compliance fee $65–$145/room (max $50K/6mo), re-evaluation up to $4,500, special audit $5,500, PIP failure $10,000/6mo, Stay Score failure up to $300/room, Service Quality Improvement $20,000–$50,000/6mo
- **Liquidated Damages:** Average monthly royalty × 60 (effectively 5 years of royalty), triggered by franchisor termination

### Initial Investment
**Hampton Inn (89-room new construction):** $15,218,226 to $22,193,244
The largest component is construction and leasehold improvements at $11.1M–$16.1M. Furniture, fixtures, and equipment add $1.2M–$1.7M. Technology systems total approximately $101K–$196K. Contingencies (10% of construction) add $1.1M–$1.6M. Three months of additional operating funds require $400K–$600K.

**Hampton Inn & Suites (113-room new construction):** $17,723,268 to $25,958,214
Proportionally higher construction ($12.9M–$18.7M) and FF&E ($1.5M–$2.1M) costs reflecting the larger room count and suite configuration (81 rooms + 32 studio suites). Three months operating funds $500K–$900K.

Both exclude real property costs, market studies, insurance premiums, and interest, which can add millions depending on location.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
Franchisees must comply with Hilton's Standards (compiled in the confidential Manual) for all purchases. However, most items can be purchased from any source meeting the Standards. Key exceptions:
- **OnQ/HPMS software:** Must be purchased from HSS (proprietary, no third-party market)
- **Exterior signage:** Must be from a licensed vendor
- **Trademarked items:** Must be from approved suppliers

Hilton Supply Management (HSM) negotiates bulk pricing but purchasing through HSM is generally not mandatory. In 2024, HSM earned $19.6M in rebates/allowances, $38.7M in administrative fees, and $8.8M in direct sales from franchisees across all Hilton brands. HSM procurement fee is 4%–10% of project cost. Total Hilton affiliate revenues from franchisee purchases (excluding royalty/program fees) were $1.1B in 2024.

Supplier approval for non-approved products takes 60–90 days.

### Training Program Detail
1. **GM/Commercial Leader Training:** Up to $1,200, virtual, within 90 days of start
2. **HPMS Training:** No fee, mandatory for all front desk/management before opening
3. **OnQ Rate & Inventory / GRO:** No fee, web-based, 80% pass score required
4. **Hilton Honors Training:** No fee, online via Hilton University
5. **Core Sales Skills:** Up to $600/attendee, 6-week Foundations or 2-week Accelerated track
6. **Annual Compliance:** $25–$50/person/topic from third-party vendors (harassment, cyber security, etc.)
7. **Brand Conference:** $2,500/attendee, in-person, annual, GM + Director of Sales required

Franchisees bear all wages, travel, lodging, and meal costs for trainees.

### Technology Stack and Costs
The mandatory technology stack is extensive:
- **OnQ/HPMS** (property management, reservations, revenue management): $27,590–$83,959 initial + $400–$600/month connectivity + $1,114–$2,568/month maintenance. Must refresh every 3 years.
- **Guest Internet Access (StayConnected):** $45,467–$82,766 initial. Refresh every 4 years.
- **GRO (Revenue Optimization, IDeaS):** ~$3,400 initial, no ongoing fee currently.
- **Connected Room (streaming/IoT):** $22,345–$32,585 initial + $417–$779/month ongoing.
- **Delphi (Sales & Events, Amadeus):** $900–$16,900 initial + $858/user/year.
- **Digital Floor Plan, Digital Key, Opening Tool:** Included in OnQ or minimal additional cost.

Ongoing technology costs can exceed $2,000–$4,000/month before considering refresh cycles. Franchisor may change requirements at any time with no cap on frequency or cost.

### Marketing Fund Spending
The Monthly Program Fee (4% of GRR) funds advertising, reservation systems, technology programs, quality assurance, and administration. Key facts:
- There is no separate audited financial statement of program fee spending
- The fund is NOT a trust and the franchisor is NOT a fiduciary
- The franchisor has sole discretion over allocation, timing, and media
- No obligation to make expenditures proportionate to any individual franchisee's payments
- Primarily internet advertising, plus TV, radio, print, and direct email

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition
**There is no protected territory in the standard franchise agreement.** This is one of the most significant risk factors for prospective franchisees. The franchisor explicitly states:

> "There are no provisions in the standard Franchise Agreement granting franchisees a protected area or territory. You will not receive an exclusive territory."

### Restricted Area (Negotiable)
The franchisor may, at its sole discretion, agree to a "Restricted Area Provision" that protects an area around the hotel for a "Restrictive Period" shorter than the franchise term. However:
- This is not standard and must be specifically negotiated
- It only applies to the Hampton Brand, not to other Hilton brands
- The franchisor retains the right to operate other Hilton brands (Hilton Garden Inn, Home2, Tru, Spark, etc.) within the Restricted Area
- The franchisor may acquire competitors who operate in the area

### Channel Restrictions and Franchisor Rights
The franchisor has unrestricted rights to:
- Own, operate, franchise, and license any competitive lodging under any brands
- Modify the System (add, alter, delete elements)
- Use System resources in other businesses
- Enter into strategic partnerships (Small Luxury Hotels, Autocamp, Evermore Resort, Field Station)
- Be acquired by or merge with competitors

No relocation permitted. No options, rights of first refusal, or similar rights to acquire additional franchises. No mechanism for resolving conflicts between the franchised hotel and competing hotels.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- **New Construction:** 22 years from Effective Date
- **Conversion:** 10–20 years from Opening Date
- **Change of Ownership:** Remaining term or as approved

**CRITICAL: No right to renew.** The franchise agreement explicitly states the franchisee has no renewal or extension right. Re-licensing is at the franchisor's sole discretion and the franchisee "may be asked to sign a contract with materially different terms and conditions from the original Franchise Agreement" and must comply with any PIP conditions. This means a franchisee who has invested $15M–$26M+ in a hotel may face expiration of the franchise with no guarantee of continuation.

### Transfer Provisions
**Permitted Transfers** (no change of control): Publicly traded equity, small private equity transfers (<50%), or institutional fund transfers may proceed without notice. Others (affiliates, family, death, cumulative >50% change) require 60-day notice, consent, and $5,500 fee.

**Change of Ownership Transfers:** Require 60-day notice, full compliance, transferee meets all current requirements, signs current-form FA, pays $200,000 COO fee, agrees to PIP. If transferee has SBA financing, fees must be escrowed at closing.

### Termination and Death/Disability
Franchisee cannot terminate before term expiration without paying liquidated damages. Franchisor has extensive termination rights with varying cure periods (10–30 days) for curable defaults and immediate termination for non-curable defaults (pattern of non-compliance, bankruptcy, 5-day closure, reputational harm, fraud, becoming a Competitor, etc.).

On death: Transfer to immediate family within 1 year, subject to qualification requirements.

### Noncompete Restrictions
- **During term:** Cannot be licensor/franchisor of competing brand. May be franchisee of competitor or manage competitor property.
- **Post-term:** NONE. No post-term non-compete for franchisee, managers, or Management Company.

### Dispute Resolution
- **Venue:** U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia (Alexandria) or Fairfax County, VA state court. If no jurisdiction: New York.
- **Puerto Rico:** Arbitration (AAA/JAMS, Federal Rules of Civil Procedure)
- **Governing law:** New York (without choice-of-law principles)
- **State overrides:** Multiple states (ND, MN, CA, MI, WA, IL, MD, RI) have addenda that may limit or modify venue, governing law, liquidated damages, and termination provisions.

### Litigation History
Five pending antitrust class actions are noteworthy. Three involve allegations that hotel companies, including Hilton Worldwide, improperly shared information through IDeaS (revenue management software) and used algorithmic pricing to set room rates. One involves Amadeus (sales system) and one involves STR (competitive benchmarking). These are systemic industry actions that could affect how franchisees are required to use franchisor-mandated technology systems.

Concluded actions include state enforcement actions for mandatory guest fee disclosure practices (Texas $2.1M settlement, Nebraska $300K settlement) and franchisee disputes.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### Data Population
As of December 31, 2024:
- Total US Hampton hotels: 2,368 (1,350 HI + 1,018 H&S)
- Comparable Hotels: 1,960 (1,107 HI + 853 H&S)
- Company-Managed Comparable: 15
- Franchisee-Managed Comparable: 1,945

"Comparable Hotels" excludes those with <1 year of operation, brand changes, substantial property damage, large capital projects, or significant room count changes.

### Room Rates (2024)
**Hampton Inn:** Average $145.82, median $136.40, range $77.60–$315.97
- Only 362 of 1,100 franchisee-managed (33.0%) exceeded the average
- Only 546 of 1,100 (49.8%) exceeded the median

**Hampton Inn & Suites:** Average $152.19, median $145.67, range $86.35–$285.65
- Only 325 of 845 franchisee-managed (38.3%) exceeded the average
- Only 422 of 845 (49.7%) exceeded the median

### Occupancy (2024, Combined)
Average 71.7%, median 71.9%, range 30.5%–96.6%.
- 987 of 1,945 franchisee-managed (50.7%) exceeded the average

### Index Performance (2024, Combined)
**Occupancy Index:** Average 109.8 (above fair share), range 43.4–224.0
**RevPAR Index:** Average 121.0 (above fair share), range 39.5–334.4
- 1,026 of 1,931 franchisee-managed (53.1%) exceeded the average RevPAR Index

### Critical Caveats
1. **No cost build-up or EBITDA disclosed.** Prospective franchisees have no franchisor-provided data to construct profitability estimates.
2. **Comparable Hotels definition** excludes properties that may be underperforming, creating survivorship-like bias.
3. **Company-managed hotels consistently outperform** franchisee-managed on occupancy (80% above average vs. 50.7%) but underperform on RevPAR Index, suggesting different market positioning.
4. **Self-selected competitive sets** for STR index data may not be representative.
5. **Revenue is top-line only** — the fee burden (10%+ of GRR before loyalty and tech) is not deducted.

### Key Financial Observations
1. **Asset-light pure franchise conduit:** 99%+ operating margin. Total expenses of $11.4M against $1.5B in revenue. This entity has virtually no operating cost base of its own.
2. **Massive distributions:** Virtually all net income is distributed upstream to Hilton affiliates ($1.5B in 2024, exceeding net income by $12.4M, funded from prior retained earnings).
3. **Minimal cash:** Only $317K cash on hand at year-end. The entity relies entirely on Hilton affiliate support for liquidity.
4. **Large intercompany receivable:** $703.9M due from Hilton affiliates is the largest asset. This effectively represents a claim on the parent — if the parent faced distress, this receivable could be impaired.
5. **Growing deferred revenues:** $558.9M in deferred revenue represents unrecognized upfront franchise fees that will be recognized over contract terms — a strong indicator of system health.
6. **Revenue growth:** Franchise royalty fees grew +12.3% (2023) and +7.8% (2024), driven by system expansion and rate increases.

## I. State Addenda Summary

Eleven states have addenda that modify the franchise agreement and/or disclosure document:

| State | Material Overrides |
|-------|-------------------|
| **North Dakota** | ALL liquidated damages deleted; ND law supersedes NY law; out-of-state venue deleted; jury trial waiver deleted |
| **Minnesota** | Liquidated damages prohibited; 90-day termination notice (60-day cure); 180-day non-renewal notice; no out-of-state litigation; no jury trial waiver |
| **California** | Liquidated damages may be unenforceable; CA termination/non-renewal laws may supersede FA; NY governing law and VA venue may not be enforceable |
| **Michigan** | Termination only for good cause (30-day cure); no out-of-state litigation; transfer denial only for good cause; non-renewal requires fair compensation (if <5yr term) |
| **Washington** | Transfer fees limited to reasonable actual costs; non-compete restrictions limited by income thresholds ($100K employee, $250K IC); no-poach provisions void; WA law prevails |
| **Maryland** | MD law may supersede on termination/renewal; franchisee may sue in MD; 3-year SOL; no waiver of MD franchise law rights |
| **New York** | NY Franchises Law governs NY causes of action; GBL §§680-695 may supersede FA; general release on renewal/transfer may not be enforceable |
| **Illinois** | IL FDA governs termination/non-renewal; governing law/venue/jury waiver cannot waive IL rights |
| **Hawaii** | General release cannot relieve franchisor from HI franchise law liability |
| **Rhode Island** | Out-of-state venue and foreign governing law void for RI FIA claims |
| **Virginia** | Cancellation without reasonable cause is unlawful |

North Dakota and Minnesota provide the strongest franchisee protections, effectively eliminating liquidated damages and mandating longer notice periods for termination.
