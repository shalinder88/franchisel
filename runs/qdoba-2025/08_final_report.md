## A. Executive Snapshot

1. Qdoba is a fast-casual Mexican restaurant system operating 827 locations (652 franchised, 175 company-owned) as of September 28, 2025, with units typically 1,500–3,000 sq ft seating 15–80 guests.

2. The franchisor entity is less than three years old: Qdoba Franchisor LLC was formed September 7, 2023 as part of a securitization transaction in which QRC transferred all U.S. franchise agreements and intellectual property to the new entity; QRC continues to manage day-to-day operations under a management agreement.

3. The brand has changed hands three times since 2003 — Jack in the Box (2003–2018), Apollo Global Management (2018–2022), and the current MRC/Butterfly Equity combination (2022–present) — introducing continuity risk that prospective franchisees should weigh.

4. Total initial investment ranges from $548,100 to $1,294,000 for a traditional unit, with leasehold improvements ($200K–$525K) and FF&E ($185K–$330K) dominating the capital stack; non-traditional units run $234,500–$898,000.

5. The ongoing fee burden is substantial: 5% royalty plus 4.5% marketing contribution plus IT charges of $6,300–$18,600/year plus 0.21% of weekly gross sales, with the marketing fee subject to increase up to 6% by majority vote and IT support fees modifiable by the franchisor at any time without cap.

6. Item 19 discloses a system-wide average net sales figure of $1,697,254 across 464 restaurants and a proforma EBITDA (inclusive of fees) of $254,430 at 15.3% of net sales for 397 restaurants, though the data is unaudited, self-reported, drawn from varying trailing periods, and excludes depreciation, development fees, and administrative costs.

7. The bottom-quartile average net sales of $969,398 implies that roughly one quarter of the system operates at volumes that would produce thin-to-negative unit economics after a full fee load, and the disclosed EBITDA low of ($214,733) confirms that material losses are possible.

8. **[Recovered — Item 21]** Qdoba Funding Holdco LLC, the securitization entity that holds all franchise agreements and IP, reported total revenues of $381.0M in FY2025 with net income of $32.8M. However, the entity carries $244.1M in securitized long-term debt (Class A-2 Notes) and $145.1M in operating lease liabilities — total obligations of $389.2M and a debt-to-equity ratio of approximately 3.1:1. The parent extracted $55.8M in affiliate distributions in FY2025 against $32.8M in net income, meaning capital is being extracted faster than it is earned. A $22.7M management fee (6.0% of total revenues) flows to QRC annually as a related-party cost.

9. The system is growing: franchised units increased from 447 to 652 over the three reported fiscal years, largely driven by 110 company restaurants sold to franchisees in FY2023, with 57 new franchised openings and only 1 termination in FY2025.

10. No exclusive territory is granted; the franchisor may place additional restaurants, non-traditional locations, or alternative distribution channels (grocery, internet, kiosks, catalogs, carts) within or adjacent to a franchisee's protected area, subject only to a generally 2-mile-radius protected territory that is itself negotiable and carved out for multiple categories.

11. Renewal requires signing a then-current franchise agreement that "may be materially different" from the original, paying a $10,000 renewal fee, executing a general release, and completing any required remodel — a combination that limits franchisee leverage at the 10-year mark.

12. All disputes must be mediated and litigated in San Diego, California under California law, regardless of where the franchisee operates — but this provision is materially overridden by state addenda for franchisees in Illinois, Minnesota, North Dakota, Rhode Island, and New York.

13. The franchisor takes a first-priority security interest in all business assets and requires spousal guarantees regardless of ownership interest, both of which the FDD flags as special risks.

14. **[Recovered — State Addenda]** Ten states have material addenda (CA, HI, IL, MI, MN, NY, ND, RI, VA, WA). Minnesota and New York impose the strongest protections: 90-day termination notice, 60-day cure period, and good cause requirements. The post-term noncompete is unenforceable or limited in California, Michigan, Minnesota, North Dakota, and New York. California caps late-payment interest at 10% (vs. base 18%).

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

The FDD is issued by Qdoba Franchisor LLC, a Delaware limited liability company formed on September 7, 2023, headquartered at 350 Camino De La Reina, Suite 400, San Diego, California 92108. The entity was created as part of a securitization transaction completed November 28, 2023, in which Qdoba Restaurant Corporation (QRC) transferred all U.S. franchise agreements and intellectual property to the new LLC. QRC continues to manage franchise operations under a management agreement. The ultimate sponsor is Butterfly Equity LP. Two affiliates are disclosed: QMG Stored Value Cards LLC and Qdoba of Canada Inc, which operates 12 restaurants.

The FDD runs 437 pages. Items 1 through 23 narrative text is fully readable. Financial statements in Exhibit A used CID-encoded fonts; all figures were recovered via character-code mapping (retry, pages 79–82). ### Fee Stack

**Initial fees.** The development fee is $10,000 per restaurant (nonrefundable, minimum one restaurant), credited toward the franchise fee. The franchise fee is $40,000 for traditional locations and $20,000 for non-traditional locations. When the franchise term is less than 10 years, the fee is prorated at $4,000 per year payable over the first 6 months. A VetFran discount of $10,000 is available for honorably discharged veterans. The last fiscal year fee range was $0 to $40,000.

**Ongoing fees.** The royalty is 5% of gross sales for traditional restaurants and 6% for non-traditional locations. The national marketing fee is 4.5% of gross sales (1.75% for licensees), with the franchisor authorized to increase it to 6% upon a majority vote of franchisees. Local advertising has no currently required minimum, though $10,000 to $25,000 is required for grand opening marketing. IT charges: a base services fee of $6,300 to $18,600 per year per restaurant, an IT support fee of $250 per period plus 0.21% of weekly gross sales (modifiable by the franchisor at any time without contractual limit), project services fees of $299 to $1,600 per project plus costs, and several one-time technology fees. An LMS fee of $24 to $40 per month per restaurant is charged. Lease administration runs $100 per month for subleased locations.

**Event-driven fees.** Renewal $10,000. Transfer up to $5,000. Relocation $5,000. Extension $1,500 per year. Site visits $500 per visit plus expenses (first two free). Training support (third or subsequent restaurant) $3,500 per trainer. Late payment interest 18% per annum (10% for California franchisees per state addendum). If an audit reveals understatement of 2% or more, franchisee pays full audit cost plus 18% interest.

### Initial Investment

For a traditional Qdoba restaurant, the estimated initial investment ranges from $548,100 to $1,294,000. The largest components: leasehold improvements $200,000–$525,000; FF&E $185,000–$330,000; IT $42,600–$101,000. Non-traditional locations range from $234,500 to $898,000. A $100,000 cash incentive is available for qualifying restaurants (first 150 qualifying locations). No financing offered by the franchisor.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Control

Franchisees must purchase all food and beverage through a single franchisor-designated distributor network. All suppliers must be approved; the approval process takes 3 to 6 months. Required purchases represent an estimated 50% to 90% of establishing costs and approximately 40% of ongoing operating costs. The franchisor collects a supply chain fee of $0.25 per case, which totaled $1,304,194 in FY2025, plus a customer fund fee of $0.03 per case. QRC's total revenue derived from required franchisee purchases was $50,845,000 (13.2% of QRC's total revenue of $383,058,000).

### Training and Operating Control

The designated operator training program runs approximately 7 weeks (315 hours). General manager training is approximately 6 weeks (270 hours). The franchisor charges nothing for the first four trainees; franchisees bear wages, travel, and living expenses. For the third and subsequent restaurants, the franchisee's own trained managers conduct training, with a $3,500-per-trainer fee payable to the franchisor. Franchisees operating 10 or more locations must maintain at least one certified training restaurant per 10 units.

### Technology Burden

The technology stack is prescribed with no alternates permitted without written franchisor consent. POS: Windows-based, PA-DSS compliant, minimum 2 terminals with 8GB RAM each, estimated cost $24,000–$35,000. Average annual maintenance: less than $17,000 per restaurant. No contractual limits on frequency or cost of technology upgrades. The franchisor has independent access to all franchisee systems and data.

### Marketing Fund

The national marketing fund is administered by the franchisor with sole discretion over spending. The fund is not independently audited. FY2025 expenditures: 17% production, 32% media placement, 18% agency fees, 19% other, 14% administrative overhead. Fund surplus: $115,478 at FY2025 end. The 14% administrative allocation represents franchisor overhead charged against franchisee marketing contributions.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

Neither the franchise agreement nor the development agreement grants an exclusive territory. The franchisee receives a "Protected Territory" described as generally a 2-mile radius around the restaurant (negotiable; not fixed by formula). The protection is not contingent on achieving any sales volume threshold.

Carveouts from the protected territory are extensive: pre-existing Qdoba restaurants, non-traditional locations (airports, colleges, military, similar venues), and dissimilar distribution channels (grocery stores, internet sales, kiosks, catalogs, carts). The franchisor retains the right to operate or license any of these formats within or adjacent to the protected territory without compensation.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

Franchise agreement term: typically 10 years. Renewal conditions: franchisee must not be in default, must sign a then-current franchise agreement (which may be materially different), complete any required remodel, pay $10,000 renewal fee, and execute a general release. The "materially different" language is a material risk.

### Transfer

Transfer requires franchisor consent. Transfer fee up to $5,000. Franchisor holds right of first refusal. Transferor remains liable after transfer — scope and duration not specified in Items text; must be confirmed in the franchise agreement.

### Termination and Death/Disability

Franchisee's right to terminate is limited: franchisee may terminate only if the franchisor fails to cure a material breach after notice. Death or disability of a controlling owner: qualified manager must be installed within 30 days; approved buyer must be assigned within 12 months.

### Noncompete Restrictions

During the franchise term: may not operate any Mexican food business where burritos, tacos, or bowls constitute 20% or more of the menu. Post-termination: 2 years within 5 miles of any Qdoba restaurant existing at termination (not just the franchisee's own location) — nationwide scope. **State-specific limitations apply:** unenforceable in California, limited to reasonable time/area in Michigan, limited to 2 years within the franchise area in Minnesota, generally unenforceable in North Dakota, limited to reasonable scope in New York.

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes: nonbinding mediation before litigation. Forum: San Diego, California. Governing law: California. **State addenda override:** Forum selection is voided or limited for Illinois, Minnesota, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Washington, and New York franchisees. Choice of law is overridden for Illinois, Minnesota, North Dakota, and Rhode Island franchisees. Arbitration, where applicable, must take place at a mutually agreed site for North Dakota franchisees.

### Litigation History

Two matters disclosed. *Q of Hasbrouck Heights v Qdoba*, settled October 2023: franchisor paid $600,000 and granted 1% royalty reduction through March 2028 with 14-month term extension. *Fiesta Ventures v Qdoba*, filed November 2024: settlement reached; FVB termination upheld but FVDM franchise reinstated; settlement not yet signed or dismissed as of issuance date. No bankruptcies reported.

### Key Financial Observations

- **Auditor:** Deloitte & Touche LLP — unqualified opinion.
- **Debt structure:** $244.1M long-term securitized debt (Class A-2 Notes). A revolving credit facility drew $8.0M in FY2025; $20.0M was repaid. Cash interest paid: $22.4M in FY2025.
- **Leverage:** Total liabilities $419.7M / equity $135.9M = 3.1:1 debt-to-equity. Long-term debt plus operating lease liabilities = $389.2M combined obligation.
- **Affiliate distributions vs. income:** $55.8M distributed to affiliate vs. $32.8M net income earned in FY2025. Member's contribution declined from $92.5M to $74.0M — the parent is extracting capital faster than the entity earns it.
- **Operating cash neutrality:** $56.2M operating cash flow vs. $56.0M used in financing (primarily affiliate distributions). The entity's operating cash flow is essentially channeled to the parent rather than retained.
- **Management fee:** $22.7M annually to QRC = 6.0% of total revenues. This is a substantial related-party cost.
- **Asset composition:** Intangibles (tradename $167.0M + goodwill $132.4M) represent 54% of total assets. Neither has been impaired.
- **Historical limitation:** The FY2024 column covers only the 10-month period from November 28, 2023 (securitization close) to September 29, 2024. Annualization is required for any year-over-year comparison.

## I. State-Specific Addenda — Exhibit H [RECOVERED]

*Source: Pages 335–400+. Ten states with registration addenda.*

### States with Addenda

California, Hawaii, Illinois, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington.

Each state has: Disclosure Addendum + Amendments to Franchise Agreement, License Agreement, and Development Agreement (where applicable).

### California (pages 335–339)

- **Interest rate cap:** Maximum 10% annually on late payments (vs. 18% in base FDD).
- **Noncompete may be unenforceable** under California law.
- **Bankruptcy termination may be unenforceable** under federal bankruptcy law.
- **General release limitation:** Cal. Corp. Code § 31512 voids waiver of rights under the Franchise Investment Law. Bus. & Prof. Code § 20010 voids waiver of rights under the Franchise Relations Act. General releases must exclude claims under these statutes.
- **Anti-fraud protection:** No disclaimer or statement signed by franchisee shall waive any fraud-in-the-inducement claim.
- **Item 19 warning added:** Performance representations do not reflect costs of sales, operating expenses, or other costs that must be deducted to obtain net income or profit.
- **California franchise law controls** if inconsistent with the Franchise Agreement.

### Hawaii (pages 340–347)

- **Fair market value compensation on termination:** Company must compensate franchisee for fair market value of inventory, supplies, equipment, and furnishings purchased from Company or designated suppliers. If termination is for purpose of converting to company-owned, Company must also compensate for loss of goodwill. Company may offset amounts owed.
- **General release limitation:** Release requirement excludes claims under the Hawaii Franchise Investment Law.
- **Anti-fraud protection:** Same as California — no waiver of fraud-in-the-inducement claims.

### Illinois (pages 348–362)

- **Forum selection voided:** Any provision designating jurisdiction or venue outside Illinois is void under Section 4 of the Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act (arbitration outside Illinois is permitted).
- **Illinois law applies** if jurisdiction requirements of the Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act are met; overrides California choice of law.
- **Termination provisions:** If inconsistent with Section 19 of the Illinois Act, Illinois law controls.
- **Anti-waiver:** Section 41 of the Illinois Act voids any condition purporting to waive compliance with any provision of the Act.

### Michigan (pages 364–366)

- **Noncompete limitation:** Post-term noncompete is "enforceable only during a reasonable time and within a reasonable area." If found unreasonable, a court may revise it.
- **Filing does not constitute approval** by the state.

### Minnesota (pages 367–382) — Most extensive

- **Noncompete limited:** Minn. Stat. § 80C.21 limits duration and scope. Post-term noncompete limited to 2 years after termination/expiration within the area of the franchise.
- **Termination good cause required:** Minn. Stat. § 80C.14 requires good cause for termination and provides 90 days' notice with 60 days to cure for most defaults.
- **General release limitation:** Minnesota law prohibits requiring franchisee to waive rights under Minn. Stat. Ch. 80C.
- **Forum selection:** The San Diego forum clause "shall not in any way abrogate or reduce any rights of the franchisee as provided for in Minnesota Statutes, Chapter 80C."
- **Governing law:** Minnesota law applies to claims arising under the Minnesota Franchise Act.
- **Civil remedy statute of limitations:** 3 years from act or omission, or 1 year after reasonable discovery (Minn. Stat. § 80C.17, subd. 5).

### New York (pages 375–382)

- **Termination subject to NY GBL Article 33:** Franchisor may not terminate or refuse to renew without written notice stating all reasons at least 90 days prior to effective date.
- **Cure right:** Franchisee has 60 days to cure upon notice of default.
- **Noncompete limitation:** Post-term noncompete enforceable only if reasonable in scope.
- **Anti-waiver:** Provisions inconsistent with NY GBL Article 33 are superseded by the Article.
- **Forum selection:** Pursuant to NY GBL § 687.4 and § 687.5, any lawsuit must be permitted in New York.

### North Dakota (pages 383–389)

- **Forum selection voided:** Section 51-19-09 voids any requirement to litigate outside North Dakota.
- **Governing law:** North Dakota law applies notwithstanding California choice of law.
- **Noncompete limitation:** Post-term noncompete covenants are generally unenforceable in North Dakota.
- **Arbitration:** Arbitration must take place at a site mutually agreed to.
- **General release limitation:** Franchisor may not require a general release as condition of renewal or transfer if it would release claims under North Dakota franchise law.
- **Waiver prohibition:** Any waiver of compliance with North Dakota franchise law is void.

### Rhode Island (pages 390–393)

- **Forum selection:** Section 19-28.1-14 prohibits restriction of jurisdiction to a forum outside Rhode Island.
- **Governing law:** Rhode Island law applies to claims under the Rhode Island Franchise Investment Act.
- **General release limitation:** Release does not apply to claims under Rhode Island franchise law.

### Virginia (page 394)

- Registration statement filed pursuant to Virginia's Retail Franchising Act (Section 13.1-557 et seq.).
- The franchise, as offered, meets the requirements of the Virginia Retail Franchising Act.

### Washington (pages 395–400+)

- **Forum selection limited:** Washington franchisees may bring suit in Washington state courts for violations of Washington franchise law.
- **General release limitation:** Release of claims not permitted if it would release claims under Washington's Franchise Investment Protection Act (RCW 19.100).
- **Termination subject to Washington law** (RCW 19.100.180).
- **Material change:** If there is a material change after registration but before closing, a revised FDD must be provided.
