## A. Executive Snapshot

1. Qdoba is a fast-casual Mexican restaurant system operating 827 locations (652 franchised, 175 company-owned) as of September 28, 2025, with units typically 1,500–3,000 sq ft seating 15–80 guests.

2. The franchisor entity is less than three years old: Qdoba Franchisor LLC was formed September 7, 2023 as part of a securitization transaction in which QRC transferred all U.S. franchise agreements and intellectual property to the new entity; QRC continues to manage day-to-day operations under a management agreement.

3. The brand has changed hands three times since 2003 — Jack in the Box (2003–2018), Apollo Global Management (2018–2022), and the current MRC/Butterfly Equity combination (2022–present) — introducing continuity risk that prospective franchisees should weigh.

4. Total initial investment ranges from $548,100 to $1,294,000 for a traditional unit, with leasehold improvements ($200K–$525K) and FF&E ($185K–$330K) dominating the capital stack; non-traditional units run $234,500–$898,000.

5. The ongoing fee burden is substantial: 5% royalty plus 4.5% marketing contribution plus IT charges of $6,300–$18,600/year plus 0.21% of weekly gross sales, with the marketing fee subject to increase up to 6% by majority vote and IT support fees modifiable by the franchisor at any time without cap.

6. Item 19 discloses a system-wide average net sales figure of $1,697,254 across 464 restaurants and a proforma EBITDA (inclusive of fees) of $254,430 at 15.3% of net sales for 397 restaurants, though the data is unaudited, self-reported, drawn from varying trailing periods, and excludes depreciation, development fees, franchise fees, and administrative costs.

7. The bottom-quartile average net sales of $969,398 implies that roughly one quarter of the system operates at volumes that would produce thin-to-negative unit economics after a full fee load, and the disclosed EBITDA low of ($214,733) confirms that material losses are possible.

8. The system is growing: franchised units increased from 447 to 652 over the three reported fiscal years, largely driven by 110 company restaurants sold to franchisees in FY2023, with 57 new franchised openings and only 1 termination in FY2025.

9. No exclusive territory is granted; the franchisor may place additional restaurants, non-traditional locations, or alternative distribution channels (grocery, internet, kiosks, catalogs, carts) within or adjacent to a franchisee's protected area, subject only to a generally 2-mile-radius protected territory that is itself negotiable and carved out for multiple categories.

10. Renewal requires signing a then-current franchise agreement that "may be materially different" from the original, paying a $10,000 renewal fee, executing a general release, and completing any required remodel — a combination that limits franchisee leverage at the 10-year mark.

11. All disputes must be mediated and litigated in San Diego, California under California law, regardless of where the franchisee operates, which the FDD itself flags as a special risk.

12. The franchisor takes a first-priority security interest in all business assets and requires spousal guarantees regardless of ownership interest, both of which the FDD flags as special risks.

13. Financial statements are issued for Qdoba Funding Holdco LLC (formed September 2023, less than three full years of history available), audited by Deloitte & Touche LLP with an unqualified opinion, but the numerical content is CID-encoded in the PDF and could not be independently verified during this extraction.

## B. Pass 1 — Front Matter, Formats, Entry Structure, Fee Stack, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

The FDD is issued by Qdoba Franchisor LLC, a Delaware limited liability company formed on September 7, 2023, headquartered at 350 Camino De La Reina, Suite 400, San Diego, California 92108. The entity was created as part of a securitization transaction completed November 28, 2023, in which Qdoba Restaurant Corporation (QRC) transferred all U.S. franchise agreements and intellectual property to the new LLC. QRC continues to manage franchise operations under a management agreement. The ultimate sponsor is Butterfly Equity LP. Two affiliates are disclosed: QMG Stored Value Cards LLC and Qdoba of Canada Inc, which operates 12 restaurants.

The FDD runs 437 pages. Items 1 through 23 narrative text is fully readable. The financial statements in Exhibit A are partially visible but use CID-encoded fonts that prevent numerical extraction. The auditor's opinion letter on pages 77–78 is readable. The document lists 14 exhibits (A through N) in the table of contents, but only Exhibit A (financial statements) and the auditor's report were partially surfaced during extraction; all other exhibits — including the franchise agreement, development agreement, license agreement, franchisee lists, state addenda, and various ancillary agreements — are listed but their operative content was not directly read.

The cover page identifies two special risks mandated by the FTC format: (1) out-of-state dispute resolution requiring all proceedings in California, and (2) spousal liability through a personal guaranty required regardless of a spouse's ownership interest in the franchise entity.

### Fee Stack

**Initial fees.** The development fee is $10,000 per restaurant (nonrefundable, minimum one restaurant), credited toward the franchise fee. The franchise fee is $40,000 for traditional locations and $20,000 for non-traditional locations. When the franchise term is less than 10 years, the fee is prorated at $4,000 per year payable over the first 6 months. A VetFran discount of $10,000 is available for honorably discharged veterans. The last fiscal year fee range was $0 to $40,000.

**Ongoing fees.** The royalty is 5% of gross sales for traditional restaurants and 6% for non-traditional locations, with the franchisor retaining discretion to charge lower rates in unusual situations. The national marketing fee is 4.5% of gross sales (1.75% for licensees), with the franchisor authorized to increase it to 6% upon a majority vote of franchisees. Local advertising has no currently required minimum, though $10,000 to $25,000 is required for grand opening marketing. IT charges layer significantly: a base services fee of $6,300 to $18,600 per year per restaurant, an IT support fee of $250 per period plus 0.21% of weekly gross sales (modifiable by the franchisor at any time without contractual limit), project services fees of $299 to $1,600 per project plus costs, and several one-time technology fees (tech support license $199, tech project management $2,250, license activation $549). An LMS fee of $24 to $40 per month per restaurant is charged for the Yoobic platform. Lease administration runs $100 per month for subleased locations.

**Event-driven fees.** Renewal costs $10,000. Transfer costs up to $5,000. Relocation costs $5,000. Extension costs $1,500 per year. Site visits cost $500 per visit plus expenses, with the first two visits free. Training support for a third or subsequent restaurant costs $3,500 per trainer. Additional or refresher training runs up to $1,600 per trainee. The Q-Cash stored value card program costs $7.75 per month. Late payment interest accrues at 18% per annum. If an audit reveals understatement of 2% or more, the franchisee pays the full cost of the audit plus 18%. Amendment fees are the greater of $500 or actual attorney fees.

### Initial Investment

For a traditional Qdoba restaurant, the estimated initial investment ranges from $548,100 to $1,294,000, excluding real property costs and liquor license fees. The largest components are leasehold improvements at $200,000 to $525,000, furniture, fixtures, and equipment at $185,000 to $330,000, and information technology at $42,600 to $101,000. Non-traditional locations range from $234,500 to $898,000. A development agreement for one restaurant adds the development fee, bringing the range to $549,100 to $1,299,000.

A $100,000 cash incentive is available for qualifying restaurants, limited to the first 150 qualifying locations and subject to a signing deadline. No financing is offered by the franchisor or its affiliates. The franchisor takes a first-priority security interest in all business assets, which means any third-party lender would be subordinated unless the franchisor agrees otherwise.

## C. Pass 2 — Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Control

Franchisees must purchase all food and beverage through a single franchisor-designated distributor network. All suppliers must be approved, and the approval process takes 3 to 6 months. Required purchases represent an estimated 50% to 90% of establishing costs and approximately 40% of ongoing operating costs. The franchisor collects a supply chain fee of $0.25 per case, which totaled $1,304,194 in FY2025, plus a customer fund fee of $0.03 per case with $85,000 remaining in the fund. QRC's total revenue derived from required franchisee purchases was $50,845,000, representing 13.2% of QRC's total revenue of $383,058,000. This figure deserves scrutiny: it indicates the franchisor has a material financial interest in the supply chain beyond royalty income.

Insurance requirements are extensive: commercial general liability of at least $2 million ($5 million for non-traditional locations), automobile liability of at least $1 million, workers' compensation as required by law, employer's liability of at least $1 million, all-risk property coverage, and builder's all-risk coverage during construction.

### Training and Operating Control

The designated operator training program runs approximately 7 weeks (315 hours) beginning 28 weeks before the projected opening date and is proficiency-based rather than time-based. General manager training is approximately 6 weeks (270 hours) beginning at least 12 weeks before opening. A third and fourth trainee must also begin at least 12 weeks before opening. The franchisor charges nothing for the first four trainees, but franchisees bear all wages, travel, and living expenses. For the third and subsequent restaurants, training is conducted by the franchisee's own trained managers, with a $3,500-per-trainer fee payable to the franchisor. Franchisees operating 10 or more locations must maintain at least one certified training restaurant per 10 units. A separate 9-hour food safety management certification through a third party is also required.

### Technology Burden

The technology stack is prescribed with no alternates permitted without written franchisor consent. The POS system is Windows-based and PA-DSS compliant, requiring a minimum of 2 terminals with 8GB RAM each and a Dell Optiplex server running Windows 11 Pro, at an estimated cost of $24,000 to $35,000. The kitchen display system requires at least 2 controllers with 22-inch or larger monitors. Back-office operations run on the Fourth platform (MacromatiX, Hot Schedules, Fourth Analytics). Digital menu boards require 4 to 5 commercial-grade displays with 2 media players. Network costs run $165 to $650 per month plus approximately $2,500 in equipment rental on 60-month terms.

Average annual maintenance is stated at less than $17,000 per restaurant. Critically, the FDD discloses no contractual limits on the frequency or cost of technology upgrades, meaning the franchisor can mandate new systems at any time with costs borne by the franchisee. The franchisor has independent access to all franchisee systems and data. PCI-DSS and point-to-point encryption compliance are required.

### Marketing Fund

The national marketing fund is administered by the franchisor with sole discretion over spending. The fund is not independently audited. FY2025 expenditures broke down as follows: 17% production, 32% media placement, 18% agency fees, 19% other, and 14% administrative overhead. The fund carried a surplus of $115,478 at FY2025 end. The 14% administrative allocation is notable and should be probed, as it represents franchisor overhead charged against franchisee marketing contributions.

## D. Pass 3 — Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Territory Rights

Neither the franchise agreement nor the development agreement grants an exclusive territory. The franchisee receives a "Protected Territory" described as generally a 2-mile radius around the restaurant, though this radius is negotiable and not fixed by formula. The protection is not contingent on achieving any sales volume threshold.

The carveouts from the protected territory are extensive. Pre-existing Qdoba restaurants are excluded. Non-traditional locations — defined to include airports, colleges, military bases, and similar venues — are excluded. Dissimilar distribution channels including grocery stores, internet sales, kiosks, catalogs, and carts are excluded. The franchisor retains the right to operate or license any of these formats within or adjacent to the protected territory without compensation to the franchisee. Under the development agreement, an additional carveout applies: if a franchisee fails to open a committed restaurant within one year of the scheduled date, the franchisor may place a unit in that territory.

Franchisees have no contractual right to open additional restaurants beyond those specified in their development agreement and no right to operate through alternate distribution channels. The practical effect is that the franchisor retains broad discretion to densify markets and expand through channels that may compete with existing franchisees' trade areas.

## E. Pass 4 — Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The franchise agreement term is typically 10 years. Renewal is available provided the franchisee is not in default, but the renewal conditions are consequential: the franchisee must sign a then-current franchise agreement (which may be materially different from the original), complete any required remodel, pay a $10,000 renewal fee, and execute a general release of claims against the franchisor. The "materially different" language means the economic terms, operating requirements, and territory protections at renewal could differ substantially from those in effect during the original term.

### Transfer

Transfer requires franchisor consent and is subject to multiple conditions: all amounts owed to the franchisor must be current, no defaults may exist, the franchisee must execute a general release, the transferee must be qualified and approved, the restaurant must be refurbished to current standards, and the transferee must complete training. The transfer fee is up to $5,000. The franchisor holds a right of first refusal on all transfers. Notably, the transferor remains liable after transfer — the extent and duration of this continuing liability was not specified in the extracted text and should be confirmed in the franchise agreement.

### Termination and Death/Disability

The franchisee's right to terminate is limited: the franchisee may terminate only if the franchisor fails to cure a material breach after notice. Death or disability of a controlling owner triggers a requirement to install a qualified manager within 30 days and assign the franchise to an approved buyer within 12 months.

### Noncompete Restrictions

During the franchise term, the franchisee may not operate any Mexican food business where burritos, tacos, or bowls constitute 20% or more of the menu. Post-termination, the noncompete extends for 2 years within a 5-mile radius of any Qdoba restaurant — not just the franchisee's own former location, but any Qdoba location nationwide, which materially restricts post-exit options.

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes must proceed through nonbinding mediation before litigation. Forum selection is San Diego, California. Governing law is California. The franchisor may assign the franchise agreement without restriction. The franchisor holds a first-priority security interest in all business assets.

### Litigation History

Two matters are disclosed. In Q of Hasbrouck Heights v Qdoba, settled October 2023, the franchisor paid $600,000 and granted a 1% royalty reduction through March 2028 with a 14-month term extension. In Fiesta Ventures v Qdoba, filed November 2024, a settlement was reached in which the FVB termination was upheld but the FVDM franchise was reinstated; this settlement had not been signed or dismissed as of the FDD issuance date. No bankruptcies are reported.
