# 08 Final Diligence Report — H&R Block Tax Services LLC (639774-2025)

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System Identity**: H&R Block Tax Services LLC, a Missouri limited liability company and indirect wholly owned subsidiary of H&R Block, Inc. (NYSE: HRB), operates one of the largest tax preparation franchise systems in the United States. The franchisor was organized on July 1, 2008, via merger with its predecessor, H&R Block Tax Services, Inc. The parent company, incorporated in Missouri on July 27, 1955, unconditionally guarantees the franchisor's performance under franchise agreements (Exhibit C).

2. **System Size and Trajectory**: As of the end of fiscal year 2025 (June 30, 2025), the system comprised 8,688 total outlets — 1,987 franchised and 6,701 company-owned. The franchised outlet count has declined every year, falling from 2,561 at the start of FY2023 to 1,987 at FY2025 end — a net loss of 574 franchised outlets (22.4%) over three fiscal years. Meanwhile, company-owned outlets grew from 6,665 to 6,701. This divergence signals a strategic reacquisition program: 438 franchise outlets were reacquired by the franchisor over the three-year period (151 + 169 + 118), accounting for the majority of franchise outlet losses. Only one new franchised outlet opened in FY2025 (in Georgia), and zero are projected for FY2026. By contrast, 138 new company-owned outlets are projected for FY2026.

3. **Investment Range**: Total initial investment for a new single retail office ranges from $34,080 to $158,750, including an initial franchise fee of $2,500. The wide range reflects variations in leasehold improvement costs ($0–$50,000), real property lease costs ($1,400–$30,000 for 3 months), and furniture/equipment costs ($15,000–$30,000).

4. **Fee Burden**: The royalty structure is uniquely tiered. On the first $5,000 of calendar-year revenue, the franchisee pays 60% (or 50% with early payment). On revenue above $5,000, the rate is 40% (30% early-pay). An incentive rate of 20% applies to incremental revenue above the franchisee's 2-year average, available only after 2 full Tax Seasons. These are among the highest royalty rates in franchising — a prospective franchisee should understand that the effective royalty on a small-volume location is extremely steep.

5. **Item 19**: No financial performance representations are made. The franchisor explicitly refuses to provide any information about actual or potential financial performance of franchised or company-owned outlets.

6. **Financial Health**: Parent revenue was $3.76 billion in FY2025 (up 4.2% from FY2024), with net income of $605.8 million. However, the parent is highly leveraged — total equity is only $88.9 million versus $3.18 billion in liabilities. The company aggressively returns capital to shareholders: $197.3 million in dividends and $437.1 million in share repurchases in FY2025 alone.

7. **Growth Trajectory**: The franchise system is in sustained contraction. Zero new franchised outlet projections for FY2026 confirm this is a strategic wind-down of the franchise channel. The company-owned side is growing, suggesting H&R Block is consolidating operations.

8. **Territory**: Non-exclusive. The franchisor reserves extremely broad rights to compete inside the franchise territory through internet/virtual channels, company-owned offices, acquired businesses, and non-Licensed Mark brands. No options or ROFR for additional franchises.

9. **Renewal**: No automatic renewal. Franchisor may offer a successor franchise at its sole discretion under terms that "may be materially different."

10. **Noncompete**: During term — 45-mile radius around territory and any Network Office. Post-term — 25 miles for 2 years plus non-solicitation of clients. These restrictions are subject to state law modifications in 11 states.

11. **Dispute Resolution**: Litigation in Missouri (Jackson County). No arbitration clause. Subject to state-specific amendments.

12. **Gag Clause**: Confirmed. Current and former franchisees have signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with the franchisor.

13. **Special Risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution (Missouri); seasonality of business (Jan–Apr revenue concentration).

14. **Litigation History**: Seven matters disclosed, including FTC settlement ($7M), franchise takeover dispute ($840K), and an ongoing wrongful termination suit. One suit initiated against a franchisee in the most recent fiscal year (noncompete enforcement).

15. **Bottom-Quartile Risk**: This franchise presents significant buyer risk: (a) extremely high effective royalty rates, (b) sustained franchise system contraction, (c) no FPR to evaluate profitability, (d) aggressive franchisor competition inside territory, (e) no automatic renewal, and (f) gag clause on franchisee speech.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

H&R Block Tax Services LLC is an indirect wholly owned subsidiary of H&R Block, Inc. The franchisor was created on July 1, 2008, when its predecessor (H&R Block Tax Services, Inc., established March 31, 1993) merged into it. The parent company, H&R Block, Inc., was incorporated in Missouri on July 27, 1955, and is publicly traded on the NYSE under the ticker HRB.

The franchisor operates under a license from HRB Innovations, Inc. (Delaware), which owns the H&R Block Proprietary Marks. This license is for an indefinite term but may be terminated by either party with 30 days' notice. If terminated, the parent will substitute itself and assume all obligations to franchisees. Key operating affiliates include Emerald Financial Services (financial products), Franchise Partner Inc. (lending), H&R Block Insurance Agency (insurance), and Companion Insurance (POM program).

The franchise offers tax return preparation, electronic filing, financial products distribution, bookkeeping, payroll services, and training. Major required products include POM Extended Service Plan, Tax Identity Shield, Emerald Card, Refund Transfers, and Video Appointments. Optional services include Refund Advance and H&R Block Small Business.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Franchise Fee**: $2,500 (lump sum, non-refundable, uniform). This is notably low compared to most franchise systems, reflecting the revenue-heavy royalty structure.

**Royalty — Tiered Structure**:
The royalty structure is one of the most distinctive and burdensome in franchising. The tiered system works as follows:

- **First $5,000 of calendar-year revenue**: 60% royalty (50% if paid within 4 days of reporting period end and no amounts overdue). This means that on a franchisee's first $5,000 of gross revenue each year, the franchisor takes $3,000 (or $2,500 with early payment). This is effectively a revenue floor guarantee for the franchisor.

- **Revenue above $5,000**: 40% royalty (30% with early payment). For a typical franchise location generating $100,000 in annual revenue, the standard royalty on the remaining $95,000 would be $38,000, bringing total royalty to $41,000 (41% effective rate). With early payment, total would be $31,000 (31% effective rate).

- **Incentive Royalty Rate**: 20% on incremental revenue above the franchisee's 2-year average. Available only after completing 2 full Tax Seasons and only with early payment. This rewards growth but still extracts 20% of incremental revenue.

- **Product-Specific Royalties**: Business services revenue carries a 15% royalty (10% early-pay). POM and Second Look carry 20% Product-Specific Royalty.

**Early-Pay Discount**: The discount (10% reduction on most tiers) requires payment within 4 days of the reporting period end AND no other amounts overdue. Missing either condition forfeits the discount on that payment.

**Reporting Periods**: Semi-monthly during February and March (aligned with tax season ramp-up), monthly from May through January, with special April periods around Tax Season end.

**POM Extended Service Plan**: Required. Per-premium charge (did not exceed $15 last Tax Season) plus $50 deductible per approved claim. Premium costs determined annually by actuarial evaluation and released before Tax Season. Revenue from POM is subject to 20% Product-Specific Royalty.

**Transfer Fee**: $2,500 (may be waived at franchisor's discretion).

**Audit Fee**: Payable only if audit reveals ≥2% understatement of revenue — cost of the inspection or audit itself.

**Additional Training**: $35 annual fee per participant for optional additional training. Required annual training (~19 hours) is provided free, but franchisee pays employee wages.

**Term Loan Default Interest**: If franchisee defaults under either security agreement, loan interest rate increases by 4%.

**Automatic Debit**: Franchisee must sign documents authorizing automatic debit of business checking account for royalty payments.

### Initial Investment ($34,080–$158,750)

The investment table includes 16 components for a new single retail office. The largest variable cost is leasehold improvements ($0–$50,000), which depends heavily on the condition of the leased space. Space requirements are 800–1,200 square feet with minimum 5 parking spaces. Annual lease costs range from $7–$100 per square foot, reflecting wide geographic variation.

Equipment costs ($10,000–$12,000) include computer hardware meeting Block Tech Ready standards. A typical 6-desk office hardware setup costs approximately $10,800. Furniture and decor for a 6-workstation office costs $15,000–$30,000.

Additional funds for 3 months of working capital range from $430 to $12,000. The low end seems unrealistically low — a prospective franchisee should plan for significantly more working capital given the seasonal nature of the business (revenue concentrated in Jan–Apr with expenses year-round for lease, insurance, and training).

No company-owned outlets or territories were sold during the preceding fiscal year, so there is no comparable transaction data for the purchase of established locations.

## C. Supplier Control, Operations, Training, Technology Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

The franchisor maintains significant control over the franchise supply chain. All tax preparation software is proprietary and must be used exclusively — no alternative software is permitted. Computer hardware must be purchased through approved supplier Coupa to specific H&R Block configurations. Financial products (Emerald Card, Refund Transfers, Refund Advances, Spruce, Emerald Advance) must be offered exclusively through the franchisor's approved banking partner under the Franchise Financial Products Distribution Agreement (Exhibit I-3).

Insurance must be obtained through the Franchise Insurance Program administered by H&R Block Insurance Agency, Inc. (affiliate) or through an independent carrier rated at least A-VII by A.M. Best. If purchasing independently, minimum coverages are: general liability of $2 million per occurrence / $10 million aggregate, E&O of $3 million per claim and aggregate, and 100% replacement cost property insurance.

The franchisor derives no direct revenue from franchisee purchases from non-affiliated suppliers. No officer holds an equity interest in any approved supplier. No purchasing cooperatives exist. These facts partially mitigate the supply chain concentration concern.

### Training Program

Initial Training comprises 42 hours of virtual/in-person instruction plus 19 hours of on-the-job training (61 total hours). The first session is in-person in Kansas City, MO, with subsequent sessions virtual. Training is facilitated quarterly and supervised by Roxane Harris (VP, U.S. Franchise/Block Advisors). Training staff has up to 15 years of tax industry experience.

Training covers tax software (11+2 hrs), office operations (6+6 hrs), products and services (3+1 hrs), client service model (2+3 hrs), systems training (3+3 hrs), compliance (2+1 hrs), marketing/growth (3+3 hrs), and general topics (5 hrs). Training is provided free of charge; franchisees pay their own travel, lodging, and employee wages.

Annual required training is approximately 19 hours, provided free. Optional additional training costs $35 per participant per year. The Income Tax Course (ITC) is offered virtually and in-person at no charge (materials fee applies).

### Technology Stack and Costs

The Block Tech Ready (BTR) standards are comprehensive and prescriptive. Requirements include:
- **Operating system**: Windows 11 Enterprise (Block-managed updates and user accounts)
- **Security**: Block-managed disk encryption and antivirus on all tax office computers
- **Hardware**: Specific HP computer models (400/600/800 G5-G9 series) purchased through Coupa
- **Network**: Cradlepoint E3000 router with security subscription; minimum 5 Mbps internet (10 Mbps recommended); cellular failover available
- **Payment devices**: First Data FD150/RP10/Clover Flex/Mini/Verifone P400
- **Printers**: Lexmark MS823N/MS810N
- **Scanners**: Kodak-Alaris models
- **Monitors**: ViewSonic touchscreen for client-facing, HP Elite display for virtual tax prep

Prohibited technologies include NAS devices, site-to-site VPN, unauthorized wireless access points, Active Directory/domain controllers, non-Block antivirus, and third-party software on POS/Backup computers. Non-H&R Block businesses must not share the tax office network.

Average hardware cost for a 6-desk office: ~$10,800. Periodic upgrade cost: $3,000–$5,000 (3–5 year replacement cycle). The franchisor has independent access to all data generated and stored in the franchise computer system, including Client Data, sales revenue, hardware data, and limited employee information.

### Marketing/Advertising

The franchisor controls all advertising programs. Franchisees receive a Brand Management Budget allocation of the greater of $500 or 1% of prior fiscal year Net Amount Subject to Royalties, available around July 15 each year. All expenditures require prior approval. The program is discretionary and may be discontinued.

Franchisees may place additional advertising at their own expense with prior written approval. Unsolicited email advertising is prohibited. All promotional plans must be submitted for approval before use.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

The franchise territory is expressly non-exclusive. Rural territories are typically defined as municipal boundaries of a city, town, or village. Metropolitan territories vary but may be limited to a specific address. The minimum territory granted may be as small as a single address — this is among the smallest territory definitions in franchising.

The franchisor reserves sweeping rights to compete inside the franchise territory:
- Internet and virtual tax services under any marks (including H&R Block marks)
- Non-Authorized Services under any marks
- Authorized Services under non-H&R Block marks
- Optional Services under H&R Block marks if the franchisee declines to offer them
- Products/services through acquired businesses, regardless of territorial overlap
- The right to be acquired by a competitor operating in the territory

The franchisor is not required to compensate the franchisee for exercising any of these rights. No options, rights of first refusal, or preferential rights to acquire additional franchises are granted. If a franchisee closes or abandons a location, the franchisor may operate it and amend the territory to exclude it.

Continuation of franchise and territory rights does not depend on achieving any sales volume, market penetration, or other performance contingency. The franchisee may not operate an internet website, sell online, or solicit online unless authorized in writing.

**Encroachment Risk Assessment**: HIGH. The combination of (a) non-exclusive territory, (b) no minimum territorial size, (c) broad franchisor competition reservations, (d) sustained franchise reacquisitions, and (e) aggressive company-owned outlet growth creates significant encroachment risk. The franchisor's stated competitive rights effectively allow it to capture revenue from within the franchise territory through multiple channels.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The franchise term ends on June 1 immediately following the 10th full Tax Season after the Effective Date — approximately 10 years depending on when in the calendar year the franchise begins. There is no automatic renewal. At expiration, the franchisor may, in its sole discretion, offer a successor franchise for the territory (or a portion of it) under the then-current franchise agreement, "the terms of which may be materially different from the terms of your FLA." This "materially different" language means the franchisee has no assurance that renewal terms will be similar to their existing agreement. The lack of automatic renewal, combined with materially different successor terms, creates significant renewal risk.

### Termination by Franchisee

A franchisee may terminate the FLA upon 30 days' written notice before June 1 of any year, provided the franchisee is not in default. However, termination requires a substantial payment: 25% of the average Gross Sales of the Franchised Business for the 3 prior years. If the franchisee has been in business less than 3 years, the payment is 100% of the prior year's Gross Sales. This is an exceptionally punitive termination provision that effectively traps franchisees — particularly underperforming ones — in the system. This provision is subject to state law modifications.

### Termination by Franchisor

The franchisor may terminate with cause only. For curable defaults, the franchisee has at least 15 days to cure. Incurable defaults include: prohibited competition or disclosure, more than 3 defaults in any 12-month period, criminal conviction likely to adversely affect the business, fraud, e-file prohibition or suspension, assignment for benefit of creditors, receivership, bankruptcy, refusal to allow inspection of records, maintaining false books or records, prohibited assignments, and termination of another franchise agreement where the franchisee or related persons are parties (cross-default). The cross-default provision means that a problem with one franchise can cascade to all of a multi-unit operator's agreements.

### Post-Termination Obligations

Upon termination or expiration, the franchisee must: completely de-identify the business (remove all H&R Block marks), pay all sums owing, return all files, assign telephone numbers and any leases covering real property that the franchisor elects to assume. If the franchisee owns the real property, it cannot be used for a similar-service business by anyone other than the franchisor or an approved transferee for 2 years following termination.

### Transfer

All transfers require franchisor approval, except for certain organizational restructurings (individual to wholly-owned entity, entity to substantially-same entity, non-controlling equity changes). Conditions for approval include: payment of all amounts owed, absence of default, payment of a $2,500 transfer fee, transferee meeting franchisor qualifications, and potentially signing the then-current FLA. The franchisor has a right of first refusal — upon receiving the price, terms, and conditions of a bona fide offer, the franchisor has 30 days to match.

### Death or Disability

The estate or legal representative must complete a transfer within 6 months. This relatively tight timeline adds pressure during an already difficult period.

### Noncompete

During the franchise term, the franchisee and all Associates (including spouses, officers, directors, shareholders, partners, members, and trustees) are prohibited from owning or working at any business that sells products or services similar to Authorized Services, within 45 miles of the Franchise Territory OR within 45 miles of any Network Office. Given the density of H&R Block offices, this effectively bars competitive activity in most markets.

Post-term, a similar 2-year prohibition applies within 25 miles of the Franchise Territory, plus non-solicitation of clients. An exception exists if the franchisee terminates under §15 and meets all requirements. Both provisions are subject to state law modifications (11 states have amendments).

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes must be litigated in state or federal court in Jackson County, Missouri. Missouri law governs. No arbitration or mediation clause exists. The FLA contains litigation limitation provisions (§39). State-specific amendments may modify the forum and choice of law provisions for franchisees in California, Illinois, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

H&R Block explicitly does not provide any financial performance representations. The FDD states: "We do not make any representations about a franchisee's future financial performance or the past financial performance of company-owned or franchised outlets. We also do not authorize our employees or representatives to make any such representations either orally or in writing."

The franchisor may provide actual records of a specific existing outlet if the franchisee is purchasing that outlet. Any unauthorized financial performance information should be reported to Roxane Harris at H&R Block Tax Services LLC, the FTC, and state regulatory agencies.

The absence of an Item 19 FPR, combined with the gag clause that restricts some franchisees from speaking openly, significantly limits a prospective franchisee's ability to evaluate the economics of an H&R Block franchise before investing.

## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Income Statement Analysis

Revenue has grown consistently over the three-year period:
- FY2023: $3,472.2 million
- FY2024: $3,610.3 million (+4.0%)
- FY2025: $3,761.0 million (+4.2%)

Service revenues (the core business) drove this growth: $3,156.9M → $3,302.3M → $3,473.9M. However, royalty/product/other revenues declined: $315.3M → $308.0M → $287.1M — a 9% decline consistent with the franchise system contraction.

Net income from continuing operations: $561.8M → $598.0M → $609.5M (8.5% growth over 2 years). The company has a small discontinued operations charge each year ($8.1M, $2.6M, $3.7M after tax).

Diluted EPS grew more rapidly due to share repurchases: $3.51 → $4.12 → $4.39 (25% growth over 2 years). Shares outstanding declined from ~194M to ~164M.

This gives a debt-to-equity ratio of approximately 35:1. The company is technically solvent but heavily leveraged, with equity comprising only 2.7% of total assets. The aggressive share repurchase and dividend program has depleted equity over time.

### Cash Flow Analysis

Operating cash flow remains strong: $680.9M (FY2025), $720.9M (FY2024), $821.8M (FY2023). The decline is driven by working capital changes (receivables growth, tax timing) rather than operational deterioration.

Capital deployment is heavily weighted toward shareholder returns:
- Dividends: $197.3M (FY2025) — $1.50 per share, up from $1.28 and $1.16
- Share repurchases: $437.1M (FY2025) — continuing aggressive buyback program
- Capital expenditures: $82.0M (FY2025)
- Business acquisitions: $35.5M (FY2025) — mostly franchise reacquisitions

Franchise lending activity: $21.7M funded, $23.8M received — roughly breakeven, consistent with a shrinking franchise portfolio.

### Key Financial Observations

1. **Leverage**: The parent's equity position ($88.9M) is minimal relative to its liabilities ($3.18B). While the strong cash generation makes this sustainable in normal conditions, it leaves little cushion for significant adverse events.

2. **Revenue quality**: Service revenue is growing while royalty/product revenue declines — reflecting the shift from franchise to company-owned. The company is successfully converting franchisees into company-operated locations without losing overall revenue.

3. **Shareholder returns**: The company returned approximately $634M to shareholders in FY2025 ($197M dividends + $437M buybacks). This exceeds net income ($606M), funded partially by existing cash balances.

4. **Goodwill concentration**: $802M in goodwill (25% of assets) creates potential write-down risk if operating performance deteriorates, particularly for the Wave reporting unit ($159M).

5. **POM deferred revenue**: $149.3M of deferred POM revenue, with $87.4M expected to be recognized within 12 months. This represents a significant warranty liability pool.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State-specific amendments to the FDD and FLA exist for 11 states: California, Illinois, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin.

**Common override themes across states**:

**Forum Selection**: Maryland, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Washington, and Wisconsin either void or limit the Missouri forum selection clause, allowing franchisees to litigate in their home state for claims arising under state franchise law.

**General Release Limitation**: California, Illinois, Maryland, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Washington, and Wisconsin limit or void provisions requiring franchisees to waive claims under state franchise law as a condition of renewal or transfer.

**Noncompete Limitation**: California (generally unenforceable), Maryland, Minnesota, North Dakota, Washington, and Wisconsin limit or modify the post-term noncompete provisions.

**Termination Good Cause**: Michigan requires good cause for termination with 30 days' notice and opportunity to cure. Minnesota requires 90 days' notice of termination (60 days to cure) and 180 days' notice of non-renewal. Wisconsin's Fair Dealership Law provides additional protections.

**Fair Market Value**: Michigan requires fair compensation for the fair market value of franchisee's inventory, supplies, equipment, fixtures, and furnishings upon non-renewal (with conditions).

**Governing Law**: Multiple states override Missouri choice of law for claims arising under their respective state franchise laws.

### Overall Assessment

This is a mature, declining franchise system operated by a well-resourced public company. The key buyer concern is not the franchisor's financial viability (which is strong) but rather the strategic direction: H&R Block is systematically consolidating the franchise channel into company-owned operations. A prospective franchisee should carefully consider whether entering or remaining in a franchise system that projects zero new franchise openings and actively reacquires existing franchises is a sound long-term investment — particularly given the 60%/40% royalty rates, non-exclusive territory, no FPR, and gag clause.
