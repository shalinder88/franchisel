# Final Diligence Report — Subway FDD (638763-2025)

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Brand and System**: Subway is the world's largest quick-service restaurant franchise by unit count, operating 19,502 US franchised outlets as of December 31, 2024. The system has been contracting — losing 1,645 US locations over three years (2022–2024). There are zero company-owned outlets.

2. **Franchisor Entity**: Doctor's Associates LLC, a Delaware limited liability company formed originally in the 1960s. The entity converted from a Florida LLC to a Delaware LLC on May 29, 2024, as part of a major corporate restructuring following the Roark Capital acquisition.

3. **Ownership and Private Equity**: Underground Purchaser LLC (Roark Capital) acquired all equity interests in Subway's former parent on April 30, 2024. Roark is an Atlanta-based private equity firm managing multiple franchise brand portfolio companies. The Roark portfolio includes other QSR and franchise brands that may directly compete with Subway franchisees.

4. **Securitization**: A US Securitization Transaction closed June 20, 2024, creating $5.685 billion in fixed-rate senior secured notes (Series 2024-1 at $3.35B and Series 2024-3 at $2.335B), plus $93M in variable-rate term notes and a $400M revolving facility. DAL became a guarantor entity with substantially all revenue-generating assets pledged as collateral. This is one of the largest franchise securitizations in history and has material implications for franchisee claims in a hypothetical default scenario.

5. **Investment Range**: $263,000–$630,000 for a traditional single restaurant; $227,000–$458,000 for non-traditional. Multi-unit development ranges from $246,123 to $604,245 per restaurant.

6. **Fee Burden**: The ongoing fee stack is substantial: 8% royalty + 4.5% advertising + $75/month technology + $57/month POS hardware + $155/month digital menu boards + payment processing fees + 1.9% loyalty program + various other technology and compliance fees. A franchisee with $500,000 in gross sales would pay approximately $62,500 in royalty alone, plus $22,500 advertising, plus an estimated $6,000–$10,000 in technology and payment processing fees — approximately $91,000–$95,000 annually before rent, COGS, or labor.

7. **No Financial Performance Representation**: Subway does not provide any FPR in Item 19. For a system of nearly 20,000 US outlets, this is a significant information gap. Prospective franchisees have no franchisor-provided basis for projecting revenue or profitability.

8. **Shrinking System**: Net loss of 631 outlets in 2024, 443 in 2023, and 571 in 2022. The system opened only 435 new locations in 2024 while losing 881 to closures ("ceased operations — other reasons") plus 4 terminations and 30 non-renewals. Approximately 52% of "openings" in 2024 were actually reopenings of previously closed locations.

9. **Financial Health**: Total revenue $975.3M, net income $397.0M in 2024. However, the massive swing from $15.4M net income in 2023 to $397.0M in 2024 was driven primarily by restructured affiliate fee arrangements following the securitization — license fees to SIP dropped from $445M to $245M and service agreement expenses dropped from $294M to $93M. Massive distributions of $683.3M were made to the parent entity in 2024.

10. **Territory and Competition**: No exclusive territory whatsoever. No radius restrictions. Franchisor and all affiliates (including Roark portfolio companies) retain unlimited rights to compete through any channel. This is one of the most aggressive no-territory provisions in franchising.

11. **Gag Clause**: Subway has signed confidentiality clauses with current or former franchisees restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience. This limits prospective franchisees' ability to conduct due diligence.

12. **Dispute Resolution**: All disputes must be arbitrated in Connecticut under Florida law. Damages are limited (no punitive, consequential, or incidental damages). Claims can only be brought against DAL, not against affiliates or individuals. These provisions substantially favor the franchisor.

13. **Litigation**: 59 actions plus 34 franchisor-initiated actions disclosed in Exhibit L (23 pages). Represents approximately 0.2% of global franchisees.

14. **Special Risk**: Out-of-state dispute resolution in Connecticut — the only risk required to be highlighted.

15. **State Addenda**: 10 states have addenda (CA, HI, IL, MD, MN, ND, NY, RI, VA, WA) with various overrides to forum selection, noncompete, termination, and other provisions.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Doctor's Associates LLC traces its history to the founding of the Subway sandwich chain in the 1960s. The entity has undergone significant structural changes, most recently converting to a Delaware LLC and being acquired by Roark Capital in 2024. The corporate structure is now highly layered: UPL → SWSH → SSH → SUSH → Subway Funding Holdco → Subway Funding LLC → DAL. All US trademarks and IP were transferred to a separate entity, Subway US IP Holder LLC, which licenses them back to DAL under a 99-year royalty-free agreement.

The securitization structure means that DAL's assets — including its right to collect royalties and fees from franchisees — are pledged as collateral for $5.685 billion in notes. This creates a situation where franchisee fee streams are the primary revenue backing a massive debt structure. The implications for franchisees in a potential restructuring scenario are material but not explicitly addressed in the FDD.

Franchise World Headquarters LLC (FWH) manages day-to-day operations as the securitization manager, receiving $49.3M in management fees in 2024. Business Developers are independent contractors who assist with franchise sales and receive approximately half the initial franchise fee and up to one-third of royalties.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Franchise Fee**: $15,000 (standard). Reduced to $7,500 for existing franchisees, veterans, non-traditional locations, and affiliate Subway owners. Satellite fee: $5,000 (waived if co-located with Base Restaurant). Short-term satellite: $1,000.

**Royalty**: 8% of total gross sales, payable weekly. Gross sales includes all sales of every kind. Reduced rates (6.5%–8%) available for airports, train stations, and captive travel plazas. Certain Development Agreement holders may receive 7.5%–8%.

**Advertising Fund**: 4.5% of total gross sales, payable weekly, deposited into SFAFT. Reduced rates (0.5%–2%) for certain non-traditional locations; 2%–3.5% for certain DA holders. Legacy franchise agreements (covering >2/3 of system) contain provision for 2/3 vote to increase advertising percentage, but current agreements fix it at 4.5%. In 2024, SFAFT spent: 41.1% national media, 30.3% digital, 10.1% R&D, 7.3% media production, 11.2% other.

**Technology Fees** (monthly): Restaurant Technology Fee $75, POS Hardware-as-a-Service $57 (HP RTaaS), Digital Menu Board HaaS $155 (or CapEx option $7,150–$8,200 + $39/month), Kount Fraud Protection $0.0068/digital transaction, Terminal Fee $10/month (Fiserv), Switching Fee $10/month (FreedomPay), Terminal Software Fee $2.30/month, Legacy Support Fee $200/month for non-compliance.

**Payment Processing**: Adyen (until Q4 2025): $0.005 authorization + $0.005 capture + $0.01 refund + network/interchange fees (0.22 per transaction to 2.4% + $0.10). Global Payments Program (starting Q4 2025): $0.00–$0.10 acquirer fee + network/interchange. Chargeback fee: $1.50 per chargeback (Adyen).

**Loyalty Program**: Sub Club at 1.9% of gross sales per loyalty transaction. 30.9% reimbursement on $2 Rewards and Surprise Rewards. No reimbursement for Sub Club Punch Card redemptions.

**Event-Driven Fees**: Transfer $7,500 (reduced: $3,200 standard, $200 death/disability/divorce, $2,000 entity changes) + $3,000 per satellite. Renewal $3,750. Additional Term $187.50/year. Noncompete violation $15,000 per business + 8% of gross sales. Trademark violation $250/day. Audit: full overdue amount if under-reported >2%. Late fees: 12% interest + 10% late fee per annum + $50 bank change + $20 bounced check. Restaurant Excellence Revisit: $140.69 (increasing 3% annually). Restaurant Design Charge: $1,000 + $250/revision. Training: free for 2; $7,500 each additional. Catering (ezCater): ~5% or ~13% + ~2.75% CC.

### Initial Investment

The estimated initial investment for a single traditional restaurant ranges from $263,000 to $630,000, with a midpoint of $430,000. The largest components are leasehold improvements ($75,000–$250,000) and equipment/furniture/décor ($110,000–$210,000). Non-traditional locations range from $227,000 to $458,000. The typical Subway restaurant measures approximately 1,375 square feet (range 300–2,000 sq ft) with monthly rent of $1,000–$6,000.

The Additional Funds estimate ($15,000–$45,000 for three months) covers payroll but excludes royalty, advertising fees, food costs, and owner's draw. This three-month period is not a representation of when the franchisee should expect to break even.

Additional cost risks include: Fresh Forward 2.0 décor requirements (costs for non-traditional and drive-thru formats "cannot be estimated at this time"), grease trap requirements ($10,000–$15,000), municipal impact fees ($5,000–$25,000), and Nevada architect requirements ($1,500–$3,000).

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

All food, equipment, beverages, and products must be purchased exclusively from approved distribution centers or approved sources designated by the franchisor. The approval criteria are treated as proprietary trade secrets and are not disclosed to franchisees. Franchisees are not permitted to independently source products even when approved products become unavailable — the franchisor will coordinate substitutions or provide waivers.

Limited Time Offering (LTO) products may be automatically shipped to franchisees without prior approval, and franchisees are responsible for all shipment costs. This creates an operational burden where franchisees must absorb costs for products they did not voluntarily order.

Vendor rebates are received by the franchisor from preferred vendors and are recorded as revenue ($136.5M in vendor funds revenue in 2024). These rebates are "intended to be used for the benefit of franchisees" but the exact mechanism of benefit pass-through is not specified.

### Training Program

Initial training consists of 40 hours of classroom training over 2 weeks at a designated location, plus 60 hours of on-the-job training in local restaurants. There is no fee for 2 persons but $7,500 is charged for each additional trainee. The franchisee bears all personal expenses (transportation, lodging, meals, wages). University of Subway provides ongoing online continuing education. Remedial training may be required if the franchisor determines the restaurant is not meeting standards.

### Technology Stack

The technology burden is substantial and growing. Required systems include: SubwayPOS (HP hardware, $57/month), BigFix Endpoint Management Software (for remote access to POS), Subway Payment Manager (SPM) for integrated payments, digital menu boards ($155/month lease or $7,150–$8,200 purchase), and high-speed broadband connectivity. The franchisor reserves the right to impose additional technology requirements and fees including a Digital Technology Fee (amount undisclosed) and various "new technology and digital initiatives."

The required technology list includes (or may include): cloud storage, SMS campaigns, social media applications, mobile device management, guest experience surveys, biometric devices, guest WiFi ($75/month), remote ordering kiosks, tablets/iPads ($700–$1,000), Internet TV/LCD monitors, digital menu boards, and wireless internet routers ($15–$25/month).

### Marketing Fund

The Subway Franchisee Advertising Fund Trust (SFAFT) collects 4.5% of gross sales from all franchisees and company-owned outlets. There is no obligation to spend a proportionate share in any particular franchisee's market. The SFAFT US Advisory Board includes franchisee representatives who provide input on advertising strategies. In 2024, spending was heavily weighted toward national media (41.1%) and digital (30.3%).

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### No Protected Territory

This is one of the most significant risk factors for a Subway franchisee: there is **no exclusive territory, no radius restriction, and no population-based limitation**. The franchisor and all affiliates retain unlimited and unrestricted rights to:

- Open or license additional Subway restaurants at any location
- Produce, distribute, and sell food products under the Subway mark or any other mark through any channel
- Use internet, catalog, telemarketing, call center, and other direct marketing methods
- Operate competing brands through Roark Capital portfolio companies

The FDD explicitly states: "We and our affiliates have unlimited rights to compete with you and to license others to compete with you." A prospective franchisee could invest $430,000 in a location only to have the franchisor or another franchisee open a competing Subway across the street with no recourse.

Channel restrictions apply to the franchisee but not the franchisor. The franchisee may not sell from any unapproved location and must obtain written approval for internet couponing, billboards, or POP materials.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The standard franchise term is 20 years with the right to renew for an additional 20-year term. However, renewal requires: (1) compliance with all material provisions, (2) maintaining premises, (3) 12–18 months advance written notice, (4) satisfaction of all monetary obligations, (5) execution of the **then-current** franchise agreement and payment of the renewal fee ($3,750), (6) compliance with then-current qualifications and training, (7) execution of a Renewal Addendum, and (8) execution of a **general release**.

The requirement to sign the "then-current form of franchise agreement" at renewal creates a "materially different" risk — the terms available in 20 years could be substantially less favorable than today's terms, and the franchisee has no right to negotiate.

### Transfer Provisions

All transfers require franchisor approval (which will not be "unreasonably" withheld). The franchisor has a right of first refusal with a 30-day matching period. Transfer conditions include: buyer qualification, transfer fee payment, training completion, full payment of all amounts owed, no existing defaults, general release, restaurant in full compliance, and execution of a Transfer Addendum.

Transfer fees: $7,500 standard (reduced to $3,200 for transfers to new/existing franchisees, $200 for death/disability/divorce, $2,000 for entity changes). Satellite restaurants must transfer with the Base Restaurant except in isolated circumstances.

The reactivation fee ($1,500 per restaurant) for cancelled transfers adds additional cost friction to the transfer process.

### Termination Triggers

The franchisee has no right to terminate the franchise agreement. The only recourse for franchisor default is seeking arbitration within 60 days. Non-traditional and school lunch franchisees may stop after 30 days' notice.

The franchisor can terminate for cause with: 10-day cure for payment defaults, 30-day cure for other defaults. Fourteen non-curable defaults trigger immediate termination upon notice, including: failure to open/complete training, material misrepresentation, felony conviction, unauthorized trade secret disclosure, abandonment (just 2 business days), unauthorized transfer, underreporting >3% of gross sales for 3 or more weeks, insolvency/bankruptcy, trademark misuse, repeated failures (3 in 12 months or same violation twice in 6 months), health/safety violations (72-hour cure), immediate health/safety concerns, failure to pay third parties, and dishonest/unethical conduct.

Post-termination obligations include: de-identification, return of Operations Manual, automatic assignment of telephone numbers, cancellation of social media and domain names, mutual lease release, and payment of remaining sublease obligations.

### Noncompete Restrictions

During term: No direct or indirect association with a competitive business **anywhere** — one of the broadest in-term noncompetes in franchising.

Post-term: 1 year within a 3-mile radius of any Subway restaurant that operates or operated within the prior year. Violation penalty: $15,000 per competing business plus 8% of its gross sales. For non-traditional locations, the noncompete is limited to the specific facility or school.

State overrides may limit the enforceability of these provisions, particularly in California (Business and Professions Code Section 16600 voids post-term noncompetes).

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes must be arbitrated through AAA or ADRC (at the filing party's discretion) in Connecticut. Florida law governs. The franchisee may not bring litigation in court. Claims may only be brought against DAL — not against affiliates, officers, directors, or employees. Damages are limited: no incidental, exemplary, contingent, punitive, or consequential damages (except where prohibited by governing law).

Additional dispute-related fees include: Probationary Case Management ($500), extension ($250), Interim Order Case Management ($250), and Litigation Expense fee (5% of gross sale consideration, max $5,000).

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

**Subway does not provide any Financial Performance Representations.** The FDD states: "We do not make any representations about a franchisee's future financial performance or the past financial performance of company-owned or franchised outlets. We also do not authorize our employees or representatives to make any such representations either orally or in writing."

For a system of nearly 20,000 US outlets with over 50 years of operating history, the absence of an FPR is notable. Prospective franchisees cannot use Item 19 to evaluate potential revenue, costs, or profitability. They must rely entirely on their own research, which is further complicated by the gag clause limiting franchisee speech.

If you are purchasing an existing outlet, the franchisor may provide actual records of that specific outlet. Any other financial performance information should be reported to management.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The US Subway system has been consistently shrinking:

| Year | Start | Opened | Total Lost | End | Net Change |
|------|-------|--------|------------|-----|------------|
| 2022 | 21,147 | 529 | 1,100 | 20,576 | -571 |
| 2023 | 20,576 | 455 | 898 | 20,133 | -443 |
| 2024 | 20,133 | 435 | 1,066 | 19,502 | -631 |

Over three years, the US system has shrunk by 1,645 net outlets — a decline of approximately 7.8%. In 2024, new openings (435) were dwarfed by closures (881 ceased operations + 30 non-renewals + 4 terminations + 139 reacquired). Notably, approximately 52% of the 435 "openings" in 2024 were actually reopenings of previously closed locations, meaning true new development was only about 210 units.

### Transfer Activity

State-by-state transfer data shows active transfer markets particularly in California, Florida, Texas, New York, and Ohio. The franchisor reacquired 139 locations in 2024 (up from 98 in 2022 and 90 in 2023), typically holding them briefly before reselling to new franchisees.

### Projected Openings

As of December 31, 2024: 120 franchise agreements signed but not yet open, plus 150 projected new franchise agreements in the next fiscal year. No company-owned openings projected.

### Gag Clause

"During the last three fiscal years, we have signed confidentiality clauses with current or former franchisees which restrict them from speaking openly with you about their experience with us. You may wish to speak with current and former franchisees, but be aware that not all such franchisees will be able to communicate with you." (p. 111)

This is a material disclosure that limits prospective franchisees' ability to conduct due diligence by speaking with current and former Subway operators.

### Key Financial Observations

1. **Securitization guarantee**: DAL guarantees $5.685B in securitized notes, collateralized by substantially all revenue-generating assets. This is an enormous contingent liability relative to the company's $390M in total assets.

2. **Restricted cash**: $90.0M restricted for securitization note reserves (interest, principal, commitment fees) — this cash is not available for operations.

3. **Intangible assets**: $250.5M net, consisting of reacquired contract rights (weighted-average life 7 years) and noncompete agreements (1-2 year life). Amortization was $97.0M in 2024.

4. **Vendor funds revenue**: Growing rapidly ($57.7M → $100.5M → $136.5M over 2022–2024). These are commissions and rebates from preferred vendors based on franchisee purchases — essentially rent on the supply chain.

5. **Tax status**: DAL is a disregarded entity for federal income tax purposes (single-member LLC). Income is taxed at the parent level. State/local income taxes are not significant.

6. **Uncertain tax positions**: $1.161M liability (including $639K in accrued interest and penalties).

7. **No impairment**: No indications of impairment identified for 2024 and 2023.

## K. Contradictions

1. **C1 — Operating expense discontinuity**: Operating expenses dropped from $941.8M (2023) to $567.4M (2024) while revenue remained essentially flat ($971.9M vs. $975.3M). This was driven by restructured affiliate fee arrangements post-securitization — specifically the shift from a guaranteed-profit license arrangement with SIP to a royalty-free license from SUIPH, and the termination of the MSA. Net income jumped from $15.4M to $397.0M.

   **Resolution**: Explained in Notes 1 and 4 to the financial statements. The 2024 financial statements reflect a hybrid year (two different operating models). Neither the 2024 nor prior-year financials represent a normalized run-rate for the post-securitization entity. A prospective franchisee evaluating franchisor financial health should understand that the apparent dramatic profit improvement does not reflect operational changes — it reflects accounting restructuring of intercompany fee arrangements.
