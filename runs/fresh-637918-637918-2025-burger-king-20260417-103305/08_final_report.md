## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Burger King Company LLC** is a Florida limited liability company formed in February 2022 as part of an internal reorganization, succeeding Burger King Corporation (founded 1954, dissolved December 2022). BKC is a wholly-owned indirect subsidiary of Restaurant Brands International Inc. (RBI), a Canadian corporation listed on the NYSE and TSX, which also owns Tim Hortons, Popeyes, and Firehouse Subs.

2. As of December 31, 2024, there were **6,701 BURGER KING Restaurants** in the United States (5,524 franchised, 1,177 company-owned) and 19,732 worldwide. The system experienced a net decline of 77 U.S. outlets in 2024, though this figure is heavily distorted by the May 2024 acquisition of Carrols Restaurant Group Inc., which converted 1,023 franchised locations to company-owned.

3. The **total investment** for a traditional freestanding restaurant ranges from $2,049,200 to $4,705,600 (excluding real estate), with the lowest non-traditional format (Indoor MRS) at $348,400 to $789,100.

4. The **ongoing fee burden** is significant: 4.5% royalty, up to 4.5% advertising (currently 4.5%), plus digital services fee ($110/month + 1% of digital sales, capped at $4,500/year), plus up to 2.0% investment spending, plus rent if leasing from BKC. Combined ongoing fees can approach 11%+ of Gross Sales before rent.

5. **Item 19** provides extensive sales distribution data. Traditional franchisee-owned restaurants averaged $1,658,463 in Gross Sales for 2024, with median of $1,561,502. Remodeled restaurants showed average sales uplift of 12.3% and traffic uplift of 11.1%. No cost build-up or EBITDA is disclosed — buyers must construct their own pro-forma.

6. **Gag clause**: Current and former franchisees signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with the predecessor.

7. The system trajectory shows **sustained contraction**: total U.S. outlets declined from 7,104 in early 2022 to 6,701 at end of 2024 (-403), with 1,433 franchisees having ceased operations in 2024 alone. Only 48 new franchised outlets are projected.

8. **No exclusive territory** is provided under any agreement form. BKC explicitly reserves the right to open or authorize restaurants near existing locations.

9. **Material litigation**: 10 pending cases including FIF ($500M+ claim), Arrington antitrust class action (employee no-hire), Plymouth County/Carrols acquisition (Delaware Chancery), and privacy class actions.

10. **RBI financial health**: Total revenues $8.4B, net income $1.445B, but significant leverage with $13.5B long-term debt against $4.8B total equity. BK segment adjusted operating income was $410M in 2024.

11. **Dispute resolution**: Litigation must be in Florida courts under Florida law, subject to state addenda.

12. The **successor franchise agreement** may have "materially different terms" — a significant renewal risk.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure
BKC operates as a Florida LLC, offering franchises since September 2022 under the BURGER KING brand. The franchise system supports two ownership types: Individual/Owner-Operator (personal responsibility, Operating Partner) and Entity (corporation/LLC/LP with Managing Owner holding at least 25% equity). The Crown Your Career Program allows qualifying BKC employees to purchase restaurants after 1-3 years, with financing available at Prime + 1-4%.

### Fee Stack

**Ongoing Fees**:
The total ongoing fee burden is among the highest in the QSR industry. The standard royalty of 4.5% of Gross Sales is supplemented by an advertising contribution of up to 4.5% (currently at maximum), creating a 9.0% base ongoing burden before any other fees. Add the digital services fee ($110/month + 1% of digital sales, capped at $4,500/year), BK University ($700/year), service desk ($750-$1,200/year), and the Burger King Foundation ($1,000/year), and the total ongoing burden approaches 10-11% of Gross Sales for a typical restaurant, before rent.

The Investment Spending marketing fee of up to 2.0% of Gross Sales is determined collectively by franchisees in the DMA, adding another potential layer. Static menu board kits ($200-$300/month) apply if you lack outdoor digital menu boards.

**Incentive Programs**:
The 2024 Multi-Unit DIP offers graduated royalty reductions for new development: Year 1 at 2.5% stepping up to 4.5% by Year 6. Advertising follows a similar stepped pattern from 2.0% to Base Fee by Year 6. Development Agreement franchisees with A/B FSS grades receive reductions of 2.5% in Year 1 stepping down to 0% by Year 7.

The Fuel the Flame Co-Investment Agreement locks advertising at 4.5% through December 2026, with a potential revert to 4.0% if the average BK restaurant 4-Wall EBITDA is below $230,000 — an important threshold whose current status is not disclosed.

The Reclaim the Flame 2 program offers franchisees four royalty rate options (4.5%, 5.0%, 5.5%, or 6.0%) with cash contributions from BKC tied to FSS remodel grades. Failure to meet remodel deadlines triggers a +3.0% royalty penalty.

**Event-Driven Fees**:
Transfer fees are modest ($2,000 first restaurant + $500 each additional). Training fees ($7,500/trainee) apply at initial opening and transfer. The deferred remodel default is punitive: royalty increases to 6.0% or 7.5% until the remodel is completed to specifications. Background checks range from $395 to $15,000 depending on applicant origin.

**Rent (BKL)**:
For BKC-owned property, minimum annual rent is 10% of capitalized site acquisition and construction costs, escalating 12% every 5 years. For BKC-leased property, minimum rent is 125% of BKC's rent plus 10% of costs. Percentage rent adds 8.5% of monthly Gross Sales up to $133,333 and 10% above that threshold, in excess of monthly base rent. These rent structures are significant and can consume a material portion of unit economics.

### Initial Investment

The traditional freestanding restaurant requires $2,049,200 to $4,705,600, with the largest components being construction ($1.0-1.8M), real property/occupancy ($300K-$1.5M), equipment ($250.6K-$304.6K), and signage/drive-thru ($150K-$230K). The Double Drive Thru is required for most facility types and adds $80K-$120K to remodel costs.

Non-traditional formats offer significantly lower entry points: co-brand ($896K-$1.68M), in-line ($945K-$1.94M), mall food court ($656K-$1.13M), indoor MRS ($348K-$789K), and big-box ($565K-$979K).

## C. Supplier Control, Operations, Technology

### Supplier Restrictions
BKC maintains extensive supplier control: 65-90% of establishment costs and 30-60% of non-occupancy operating costs must come from approved suppliers. BKC is the sole supplier of technology platforms for ordering, delivery, and loyalty services. Sole suppliers also cover network services, digital menu boards, gift cards, cashless payments, quality inspections, food, beverages, cleaning supplies, and packaging. This creates significant supply chain dependency and limits franchisee negotiating power on operating costs.

### Training
Initial training fee is $7,500 per trainee. The Restaurant Management Program includes classroom and in-restaurant components. ServSafe certification is mandatory for Operating Partner/Managing Owner, Managing Director, and restaurant manager. BK University Team Member Training and Shift Certification programs must be implemented for all staff. Continuing operations training is also $7,500 per person. The head of training is Greg Moscetti, who joined BKC in June 2025.

### Technology
The technology stack is extensive and mandatory: POS systems from approved vendors (minimum 2 front counter, 2 drive-thru devices), interior/exterior digital menu boards, kiosks, kitchen display systems, and data reporting systems. The Royal Perks Loyalty Program is mandatory. Digital services fee of $110/month plus 1% of digital sales (capped at $4,500/year) covers the technology platform for ordering, delivery, and loyalty via the BK app, website, and third-party platforms.

### Advertising Fund
BKC administers the national advertising fund. The advertising contribution is currently 4.5% of Gross Sales (the maximum permitted). The fund may use all media forms. BKC does not use fund money for advertising primarily for franchise sales. No audit results or spending breakdown is disclosed.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels

BKC provides **no exclusive territory** under any agreement form — Franchise Agreement, TRA, MTRA, or Development Agreement. The FDD states explicitly: "You will not receive an exclusive territory. You may face competition from other franchisees, from outlets that we own, or from other channels of distribution or competitive brands that we control."

BKC and affiliates reserve the right to sell BURGER KING branded products through alternative channels (grocery, convenience stores, internet). Military base development rights are granted to the Army & Air Force Exchange Service.

The encroachment risk is HIGH. BKC's sister brands (Tim Hortons, Popeyes, Firehouse Subs) operate in the same or nearby markets, and BKC expressly states that other BK restaurants "may compete with your Restaurant or may affect customer trading patterns."

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
The standard franchise term is 20 years for freestanding restaurants, potentially shorter for non-traditional locations or limited property control. There is **no right of renewal** — only an option to obtain a successor Franchise Agreement up to 20 years if the franchisee is in compliance, has property control, and meets then-current successor requirements. The successor FA **"may have materially different terms"** — a significant risk that the economics of the business could change substantially at renewal.

### Termination
The termination framework has aggressive cure periods. Health/safety and operational standards violations require cure within 5 days. Royalty/advertising nonpayment requires 10-day cure. Most other defaults get 30 days. Financial criteria failure gets 180 days. Non-curable defaults include bankruptcy, felony conviction, fraud, understatement of gross sales, unauthorized transfer, and repeated breaches after notice. Cross-default provisions link the FA to Crown Your Career, Reclaim the Flame, and Carrols Refranchise programs.

### Transfer
All transfers require BKC consent. Transfer fees are $2,000 for the first restaurant plus $500 each additional. Buyer must be approved, complete all training, pay all amounts current, sign current FA (which may differ substantially from seller's FA), and provide a general release. BKC has right of first refusal on matching third-party terms. Crown Your Career restricts transfers during the first 5 years for any reason; Carrols Refranchise restricts transfers until all remodels are completed.

### Death/Disability
Heir must be approved by BKC or sell the interest within 12 months. BKC has the option to operate and/or manage the restaurant during the transition period.

### Noncompete
During the term: Cannot own, operate, or have any interest in any other hamburger business. Post-term: 1 year within 2 miles of the restaurant, cannot own or operate a hamburger business. Crown Your Career adds: no other business activities until the later of the 5th anniversary of the first FA or full loan repayment.

### Dispute Resolution
No mandatory arbitration. Mandatory non-binding mediation for development disputes. Voluntary arbitration at franchisee's election. Delivery restaurant franchisees waive development dispute rights. Litigation venue: U.S. District Court, Southern District of Florida, or state court in Miami-Dade County. Florida law governs, subject to state addenda in Exhibit P.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### Sales Distributions (January 1 – December 31, 2024)

**Traditional Restaurants** (5,837 qualifying of 6,229 total):
The consolidated average was $1,671,613 with median of $1,579,183. Franchisee-owned averaged $1,658,463 (median $1,561,502); company-owned averaged $1,730,667 (median $1,638,579). The distribution shows 29.1% of consolidated restaurants above $1.9M, but also 4.1% at $700K-$900K and 0.7% below $700K. Only 43% of restaurants meet or exceed the average — the upper tier pulls the average well above the median.

**Non-Traditional Restaurants** (775 qualifying of 874):
Franchisee-owned averaged $1,321,324 with median $1,155,563. The tail risk is more pronounced: 15.9% below $700K and 13.9% at $700K-$900K. The high was $7,387,029 and the low was $208,320.

**Fuel Co-Branded** (320 restaurants in 4 categories):
Full Size (59): $1,446,035 average. Large (135): $1,256,658. Small (34): $1,281,881. Kiosk (83): $973,531. Kiosk locations show meaningful tail risk at 18.1% below $700K.

### Remodel Uplift Results (893 restaurants)
Full remodels (840): 11.4% average sales uplift, 9.9% median. Scrape & Rebuild (53): 26.1% average uplift. SDT to Double DT conversion (316): 15.8% average. Legacy to Modern image (705): 13.2% average. Multi-year data shows uplift sustaining at 13.4% average cumulative at 3+ years post-remodel.

### Critical Caveats
Sales figures do NOT reflect costs, operating expenses, or net income. No EBITDA or cost build-up is disclosed. Buyers must construct their own pro-forma. Data is based on franchisee-reported Gross Sales. Exclusions include restaurants that temporarily closed, permanently closed, or opened during the measurement period.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### System Trajectory
The U.S. system has been contracting: from 7,104 total outlets at the start of 2022 to 6,701 at end of 2024, a loss of 403 locations over three years. The franchised base declined from 7,053 to 5,524 (-1,529), partially offset by growth in company-owned from 51 to 1,177 (+1,126), largely driven by the Carrols acquisition.

In 2024 specifically: 63 franchised outlets opened, but 0 were terminated, 49 were not renewed, 1,061 were reacquired by the franchisor (primarily Carrols), and 69 ceased operations for other reasons. Table 2 shows 254 transfers to new owners in 2024, up from 245 in 2023 and 103 in 2022. Only 48 new franchised and 2 new company-owned outlets are projected.

### Gag Clause
**PRESENT**: "In some instances, during the last three fiscal years, current and former franchisees signed provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with our predecessor." This is a material disclosure — prospective franchisees should be aware that some former franchisees cannot freely discuss their experience.
