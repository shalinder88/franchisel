# Final Diligence Report — 639418-2025 Planet Fitness

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Planet Fitness Franchising LLC** is a Delaware limited liability company formed June 13, 2018, as part of a securitization transaction. The company serves as the franchisor for Planet Fitness fitness facilities in the United States. The system has been operating since 2003 through predecessor entity Pla-Fit Franchise, LLC.

2. **System size**: As of December 31, 2024, the Planet Fitness system comprised 2,568 total locations — 2,298 franchised and 270 company-owned — making it one of the largest franchise systems in the fitness industry. Net growth in 2024 was +113 locations (+97 franchised, +16 company-owned), representing a modest deceleration from +141 in 2023 and +144 in 2022.

3. **Ultimate parent**: Planet Fitness, Inc., a publicly traded Delaware corporation. The franchisor sits within a securitization structure: Planet Fitness Franchising LLC → Planet Fitness Master Issuer LLC → Planet Fitness SPV Guarantor LLC → Planet Fitness Holdings, LLC → Pla-Fit Holdings, LLC → Planet Fitness, Inc.

4. **Investment range**: $1,525,000 to $5,221,500 for a single location. The wide range is primarily driven by whether fitness equipment (from sole-source affiliate PF Equipment) is financed ($1.525M–$3.71M) or purchased outright ($2.54M–$5.22M). Typical facility is 15,000–25,000 square feet in strip centers, malls, or freestanding locations.

5. **Fee burden**: 7% royalty on EFT Dues Draft, plus 2% NAF (can increase to 3%), plus LAF of at least $60,000/year or 7% of cumulative Monthly EFT, plus a 20% Join Fee on each new membership. Combined advertising cap is 9% of EFT Dues Draft. Periodic re-equipping ($363K–$1.1M every 5–9 years) and remodeling ($250K–$1.2M every 12 years) represent significant ongoing capital requirements.

6. **Item 19 highlights**: Average annual EFT revenue for the 2,197 reporting franchised clubs ranged from $1,205,580 (bottom third) to $2,613,753 (upper third). Corporate-club EBITDA ranged from $266,824 (21% of net revenue, bottom third) to $1,089,084 (42%, upper third). The bottom-third EBITDA margin of 21% means a typical bottom-quartile club generates approximately $267K in EBITDA before debt service, taxes, depreciation, and capital expenditures — a thin margin relative to the $1.5M+ total investment.

7. **Financial health**: The franchisor entity reported $350.1M in total revenue and $301.3M in net income for 2024. However, it distributed $306.9M in dividends to its parent — exceeding net income and increasing the accumulated deficit to ($50.1M). The entity holds only $258K in cash and $146.3M in intangible assets (virtually its only asset). Approximately $2.25B in securitized senior notes are outstanding at the parent/Master Issuer level, with 2024 notes issued at 5.77%–6.24% rates.

8. **Growth trajectory**: Positive but decelerating. 78 projected new franchised locations and 9 company-owned for the next fiscal year. 84 franchise agreements signed but not yet opened. The transfer volume of 270 in 2024 (up from 100 in 2023) suggests significant ownership churn.

9. **Territory**: No exclusive territory under the Franchise Agreement. Area Development Agreement provides limited protection with 5 material carve-outs (non-traditional locations, brand acquisitions, multi-territory acquisitions, pre-existing locations, refranchising after breach).

10. **Renewal**: 12-year initial term with successor franchise available for $20,000 fee, but subject to then-current agreement terms which "may contain materially different terms and conditions."

11. **Dispute resolution**: Mediation and arbitration in Portsmouth, NH; NH law governs. Multiple state addenda override venue and choice-of-law provisions.

12. **Special risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution flagged on Special Risks page. Gag clause confirmed — some franchisees have signed confidentiality provisions restricting their ability to speak openly.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Planet Fitness Franchising LLC operates within a complex securitization framework. The franchisor is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Planet Fitness Master Issuer LLC, which issues securitized notes backed by the franchise system's revenue streams. Planet Fitness Holdings, LLC, acting as Manager under a management agreement, provides all franchisee support services. The Manager receives a fee of $20,000 fixed plus $18 per franchised club and $40 per corporate club annually. This structure means the franchisor's obligations to franchisees are performed by an affiliate (Holdings) rather than the franchisor itself, though the parent Planet Fitness, Inc. guarantees Holdings' performance.

The franchise concept targets the "Judgement Free Zone" consumer — a budget-conscious, health-conscious individual seeking a non-intimidating fitness environment. Two membership tiers exist: Classic and Black Card (which includes tanning, massage/hydromassage, guest privileges, and multi-club access).

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees:**
The initial franchise fee is $20,000, though this is currently waived for locations opened under Area Development Agreements. The ADA fee is $10,000 per location ($60,000 if reacquiring a terminated ADA territory). A $4,000 Construction Development Plan review fee applies if not using designated architects.

Equipment represents the largest initial cost: $363,000–$1,103,000 for fitness equipment (from sole-source affiliate PF Equipment) and $762,000–$1,061,000 for non-fitness equipment (tanning beds, TVs, lockers, flooring, etc.). If financed (10-30% down), the equipment down payment ranges from $36,300–$330,900 for fitness and $76,200–$318,300 for non-fitness equipment.

**Ongoing Royalty and Join Fee:**
The 7% royalty is calculated on the EFT Dues Draft — the total gross monthly and annual membership fees billed via electronic funds transfer, regardless of actual collection. This is a critical distinction: the franchisee pays royalty on billed amounts even if members' payments decline or are returned. Monthly decline/return rates at corporate clubs ranged from 3.0% to 34.8% of gross EFT in 2024.

In addition to the royalty, franchisees pay a Join Fee of 20% of the regular monthly membership fee (or 5% of prepaid memberships) for each new member, charged once per membership.

**Advertising Requirements:**
The NAF contribution is 2% of EFT Dues Draft (with a 3% contractual cap; higher requires 66%/51% vote). For 2026 specifically, it is 2% EFT Dues Draft plus 1% of Monthly EFT. Monthly NAF percentages vary by quarter (3.6% Q1, 1.5% Q2-Q4), with December adjusted to hit the annual 2% target.

Local Advertising Fund spending must be at least the greater of $60,000 or 7% of cumulative Monthly EFT per year. Q1 minimum is $24,000 or 10% cumulative Monthly EFT. Combined LAF + NAF are capped at 9% of EFT Dues Draft.

Special marketing programs may require up to 7% of Monthly EFT for a single month (credits toward LAF). Franchisees must participate in all national campaigns, which may require waiving enrollment fees or offering lower membership prices.

In FY2024, NAF spending was: 24% production, 53% media placement, 7% administrative, 16% other (agency fees, research). The NAF is not required to be audited. An unaudited annual statement is available on written request.

**Event-Driven Fees:**
- Franchise Agreement Transfer: $10,000 + up to $10,000 in out-of-pocket costs
- ADA Transfer: $5,000 per unmet development obligation + up to $5,000 out-of-pocket
- Successor Franchise Fee: $20,000
- Securities Offering Fee: up to $100,000 + up to $100,000 in expenses
- Cure Period Extension Fee: up to 4% of EFT Dues Draft (monthly, during extended cure)
- Debrand Deficiency Fee: $35,000 for strength/amenity equipment + $35,000 for cardio
- Late Development Fee: $50,000 per missed ADA opening deadline
- Management Fees: up to 10% of monthly gross revenue (if franchisor must step in)

**Periodic Capital Requirements:**
- Re-equipment: $363,000–$1,137,000 every 5–9 years (frequency depends on usage tier — high-use clubs: cardio 5yr/other 7yr; normal: 6yr/8yr; low-use: 7yr/9yr)
- Remodeling: $250,000–$1,200,000 every 12 years
- Both timelines can be accelerated with a 66% all-business or 51% franchised-business vote

### Initial Investment Table

The total estimated initial investment ranges from $1,525,000 to $5,221,500, excluding real estate acquisition costs. The largest components are leasehold improvements ($1.25M–$2.14M) and equipment (combined $112K–$2.16M depending on finance vs. purchase). Additional funds for the first three months of operation range from $68,000 to $469,000.

Franchisees may not borrow more than 80% of their initial investment. The franchisor does not offer direct or indirect financing or guarantee any obligations. Dense urban areas may significantly exceed the estimated range.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Planet Fitness exercises exceptionally tight supplier control. PF Equipment, a franchisor affiliate, is the sole supplier of fitness equipment in the United States. In FY2024, PF Equipment generated approximately $255 million in gross revenue from franchisee equipment sales, plus a $12.6 million volume-based rebate. Assetco (company-owned locations) received approximately $9.6 million in equipment discounts from PF Equipment. This related-party dynamic means franchisees are captive to an affiliate supplier with no alternative and no transparency into the markup structure.

For non-equipment purchases, franchisees must use franchisor-approved suppliers or meet franchisor specifications. The franchisor may limit suppliers or designate single sources. Approximately 97% of startup purchases and 11-40% of ongoing costs must come from designated/approved sources.

Alternative supplier approval is possible but requires the franchisor to evaluate the supplier against 8 criteria, with a response within 45 days and potential evaluation costs up to $25,000.

The franchisor reserves the right to receive Vendor Revenue (commissions) from suppliers, limited to certain "Permitted Categories" for franchisees paying 7%+ royalty. Vendor Revenue outside these categories must be contributed to the NAF (unless price reductions are offered instead).

### Training Program

Training totals 94 hours: 74 classroom + 20 on-the-job. Phase 1 (Owner Orientation) is 14 hours covering philosophy, pre-open processes, legal compliance, and site selection. Phase 2 (Pre-Sale and Operations) is 40 hours covering day-to-day operations, staffing, and Q&A. Additional workshops (manager and regional manager) add 32 hours, plus 8 hours of e-learning. Training occurs at Hampton, NH headquarters and a designated PF business. No training fee for initial program (up to 3 people), but franchisees bear all travel/lodging/meal costs. Failure to complete training to franchisor satisfaction is grounds for FA termination.

POS training is 1 day on-site from the designated POS vendor at ~$800/day/person.

Ongoing training: up to 2 refresher sessions of 5 days each per calendar year for 2 people, plus potential refresher workshop fees of $500–$1,500.

### Technology Stack

Franchisees must use the designated POS System, which includes club management, member management, and point of sale functions. Hardware costs $5,000–$13,000, monthly software support is $199/club, security services are $50–$100/month, and annual transaction processing costs range from $9,000–$53,000/location. CRM software reimbursement is $100/year.

The franchisor has unlimited daily access to all franchisee POS data. There are no contractual limits on the frequency or cost of required technology upgrades. The POS vendor handles royalty and fee collection via automatic EFT withdrawals.

### Marketing Fund and Advertising

The NAF is administered solely by the franchisor with final authority on all marketing decisions. A marketing subcommittee (chosen by the franchisee association board) serves in an advisory capacity only. Corporate locations contribute to the NAF on the same basis as franchised locations. The franchisor owns all creative concepts and materials created with NAF funds.

The NAF is not required to be audited, though an unaudited annual statement is prepared. In FY2024, the largest allocation was media placement (53%).

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory

**Franchise Agreement**: No exclusive territory whatsoever. The franchisor retains unrestricted rights to open or franchise additional PF locations anywhere, sell through any channel, operate competing non-PF fitness businesses near your location, acquire or merge with competitors, and enter commercial partnership agreements requiring your participation. The franchisor may also require you to sublease up to 10% of your location's square footage for its own use.

**Area Development Agreement**: Provides limited protection within a designated Development Area during the ADA term, conditioned on compliance with the Development Schedule. However, 5 material carve-outs significantly limit this protection:

1. Pre-existing PF locations are excluded and may be relocated within the Development Area.
2. Non-traditional locations (airports, military, hotels, universities, corporate offices, housing complexes) may be developed in the Development Area — franchisee gets a 60-day right of first refusal.
3. Franchisor may refranchise locations after a franchisee's material breach.
4. Brand acquisitions and conversions are permitted.
5. Multi-Territory Acquisitions are permitted with a 30-day ROFR (overridable if 66% of affected area developers commit).

After the ADA expires, a limited right of first refusal exists only if all conditions are met.

### Channel Restrictions

Franchisees may not use the Internet for selling PF-branded merchandise or non-membership products/services. All social media and digital presence requires franchisor approval. The franchisor may sell products and services through any channel, including e-commerce, without limitation.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial franchise term is 12 years from the date operations commence. The term may be extended by up to 1 year or reduced by up to 2 years to align with lease expiration. A successor franchise may be granted for another term equal to the initial term, but the successor franchise agreement "may contain materially different terms and conditions." This is a significant risk — the renewal terms are not guaranteed to match the original agreement.

Successor franchise conditions include: substantial compliance during the prior term, cure all deficiencies, remodel as required, pay $20,000 successor fee, sign general release, meet then-current training requirements, and establish continued possession of the premises. If the franchise expires without renewal and the franchisor permits continued operation, the royalty may temporarily increase by up to 4% above the then-current rate.

### Termination

The franchisor may not terminate without cause. Curable defaults include payment defaults (cure periods as short as 24 hours), opening delays, operational and conduct issues, and unfair competition — with cure periods ranging from 24 hours to 60 days. Some conduct and unfair competition defaults are only curable by causing the offending owner to relinquish ownership entirely.

Non-curable defaults resulting in immediate termination include: insolvency/bankruptcy, abandonment or loss of control without consent, material misrepresentation, cross-defaults (multiple defaults across agreements), and anti-terrorism law violations.

A notable franchisee-favorable provision: if the franchise has been open 3+ years, the EFT Dues Draft has remained in the lowest 5% of similarly-sized PF businesses for 12 consecutive months, and the franchisee can demonstrate unprofitability, the franchisee may terminate on 12 months' notice. This provides a floor exit for chronically underperforming locations.

### Transfer Provisions

All transfers require prior written consent. For third-party transfers: $10,000 transfer fee plus up to $10,000 in out-of-pocket costs; transferee must complete training and sign the then-current franchise agreement (which may have materially different terms). The franchisor has a right of first refusal to purchase the franchise at the bona fide offer price. Upon termination/expiration, the franchisor has a 60-day purchase option.

ADA transfers: must transfer all franchise agreements and the ADA together. Transfer fee is $5,000 per unmet development obligation. Minimum holding period: 2 years from ADA date (or 1 year if controlling owner has 2+ years PF experience).

Death or disability is treated as a transfer, with a 6-month deadline for ADA controlling interests.

### Non-Competition

During the term: no involvement in any Competitive Business by franchisee, owners, or immediate family members. Competitive Business is broadly defined to include any fitness, exercise, athletic services, digital fitness content/classes/instruction/advice operation.

Post-term: 2 years at the franchise location, within 15 miles of the location, and within 15 miles of any PF business. If the franchisor exercises its ROFR, the restriction extends to 5 years from closing within 15 miles of any PF business or development rights sold to the franchisor. With 2,568+ locations, the 15-mile radius restriction is extremely broad and could effectively prevent former franchisees from operating any fitness business in most metropolitan areas.

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes are resolved through mediation and arbitration in Portsmouth, New Hampshire (or the franchisor's then-current headquarters city). New Hampshire law governs. Either party may seek injunctive relief from any court of competent jurisdiction. Multiple state addenda override these provisions — particularly Minnesota (must be in MN), California (may not be enforceable), North Dakota (out-of-state venue may not be enforceable), and Wisconsin (Fair Dealership Law supersedes).

### Litigation History

The active case involving CDO Chip Ohlsson relates to his prior role at Wyndham Hotels, not his Planet Fitness activities. A counterclaim alleges he fraudulently or negligently misrepresented aspects of hotel franchise transactions. This is currently in discovery.

The prior Conway judgment ($8.8M) against Planet Fitness founders for misrepresentation regarding ownership interests is notable for governance risk, though the individuals involved have left the company. The NY OAG settlement (2015) regarding "free"/"unlimited" advertising of tanning services indicates historical compliance issues.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### EFT Revenue Data

Planet Fitness discloses extensive financial performance data. For the 2,197 franchised clubs reporting for the full year 2024 (out of 2,298 total, with 101 excluded for partial-year operations):

The system is divided into thirds by Annual EFT Revenue. The median franchised club in the middle third generated $1,794,689 in annual EFT revenue. At the 7% royalty rate, this represents approximately $125,628 in annual royalty payments alone, before NAF, LAF, Join Fees, and other fees.

The bottom-third average of $1,205,580 is notable: with only 58% of bottom-third clubs exceeding this average, a significant number of franchised clubs operate at even lower revenue levels. The absolute low across all clubs is $283,269 — suggesting some locations are severely underperforming.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The Planet Fitness system has shown consistent growth but is decelerating:
- 2022: +144 net (franchised: +22 net after 114 reacquired + 118 opened; corporate: +122 net after 114 acquired from franchisees)
- 2023: +141 net (franchised: +119; corporate: +22)
- 2024: +113 net (franchised: +97; corporate: +16)

The 2022 and 2023 figures were significantly affected by a single large franchisee-to-corporate transaction (114 clubs in 2022, 4 in 2023). Organic franchised growth (new openings) has been: 118 (2022), 123 (2023), 100 (2024) — a declining trend.

### Transfer Activity

Transfer volume spiked to 270 in 2024 (from 100 in 2023 and 206 in 2022). Top states for 2024 transfers include Illinois (54), Minnesota (31), Indiana (29), Colorado (26), Virginia (17), Iowa (17), California (12), Mississippi (11), and Oregon (10). This volume suggests significant portfolio restructuring — some of these may reflect large multi-unit transactions. The transfer footnote excludes transfers where less than 50% beneficial ownership changes, individual-to-entity, and transfers to heirs, suggesting the actual total ownership activity is higher.

### Terminations and Closures

Remarkably low: only 1 termination (Illinois) and 2 ceased operations (California, Ohio) across 2,298 franchised clubs in 2024. Zero non-renewals. Zero reacquisitions. This exceptionally clean closure record is unusual for a system of this size and suggests either strong unit economics across the portfolio or very tight franchisee compliance management.

### Projected Openings

84 franchise agreements signed but not yet open. 78 projected new franchised outlets and 9 company-owned for the next fiscal year. Top projected states: California (18 franchised + 1 corporate), Texas (10 franchised), Ohio (7 franchised), New York (4 corporate), New Jersey (4 franchised).

Confirmed. "During the last 3 fiscal years, some franchisees have signed confidentiality clauses with us. In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with us." This is a yellow flag for due diligence — prospective franchisees should be aware that not all current/former franchisees will be able to share their experiences freely.

## I. State Addenda Summary

Nine states have addenda: California, Illinois, Maryland, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, and Wisconsin.

**California**: Post-term non-compete may not be enforceable. General release for renewal/transfer may be unenforceable. NH governing law and binding arbitration venue may not be enforceable. Punitive damages waiver may not be enforceable. Material modifications require DFPI filing.

**Illinois**: Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act governs. Sections 705/19 and 705/20 provide termination/nonrenewal rights.

**Maryland**: General release not required as a condition of renewal/transfer/sale. Franchise fees must be deferred until franchisor satisfies material obligations. Out-of-state litigation provisions may not be enforceable. Minimum 3-year limitations period.

**Minnesota**: 90 days notice of termination with 60 days to cure (vs. shorter periods in the standard FA). 180 days notice of non-renewal. Transfer consent must not be unreasonably withheld. No out-of-state litigation or arbitration. No jury trial waiver. No liquidated damages or termination penalties. General release prohibited. Franchisor must indemnify for trademark use.

**New York**: Registration is not a state recommendation. Franchisor cannot use negotiation to impose less favorable terms. Good faith termination requirements per NY GBL Article 33.

**North Dakota**: Non-compete covenants may be unenforceable (NDCC 9-08-06). General release may be unenforceable. Out-of-state jurisdiction, NH choice of law, punitive damages waiver, jury trial waiver, and limitations provisions may not be enforceable.

**Rhode Island**: RI Franchise Investment Act governs profit-sharing, noncompete, and transfer provisions.

**Virginia**: No disclaimer of reliance on FDD representations.

**Wisconsin**: Wisconsin Fair Dealership Law supersedes inconsistent FA provisions. Prohibits termination/cancellation/non-renewal without good cause. 90 days prior written notice with 60 days to cure.

Minnesota and North Dakota provide the strongest franchisee protections, effectively neutralizing several of the more franchisor-favorable provisions in the standard agreements.
