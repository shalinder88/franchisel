## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size**: 224 licensed Express Units and 14 company-owned (all multi-brand KFC/TB operated by KFC) = 238 total at year-end 2024. The Express system is small relative to the broader Taco Bell Traditional franchise system (~6,207 units collecting royalties in 2024).

2. **Entity structure**: Taco Bell Franchisor, LLC is a special purpose vehicle (SPV) formed in 2016 as part of a securitization transaction. It has no employees. Taco Bell Corp. (TBC), a California corporation formed in 1962, manages all operations under a management agreement. Ultimate parent is YUM! Brands, Inc. (Louisville, KY). The franchisor entity collects franchise fees and royalties, which flow to the securitization trust for the benefit of note holders.

3. **Ownership history**: TBC operated Taco Bell since 1962 (Traditional) and Express since 1991. In 2016, a securitization restructuring created the current SPV franchisor. Follow-on securitizations occurred in 2018 and 2021, with another planned for 2025. Total securitization notes outstanding: $3.74 billion across 5 tranches (1.946%–4.970%).

4. **Investment range**: Express Units $287,350–$855,700; Power Pumpers $354,850–$770,700; Existing Units $152,250–$1,766,250+. Construction estimates are based on the Dallas, TX market and vary significantly by location.

5. **Fee burden**: The 10% royalty rate on Gross Sales is among the highest in QSR and nearly double the 5.5% rate for Traditional Taco Bell franchises. Add the $0.19/digital transaction fee, $750/year All Access fee, $275/quarter merchandising fee, and up to $23,100/year in technology fees, and the effective fee load is substantial. There is no required advertising fund contribution, but the national brand benefits from the Traditional system's advertising spend.

6. **Item 19**: No financial performance representation whatsoever. The franchisor explicitly declines to provide any information about revenues, costs, or profitability of Express Units. This is a significant gap for prospective investors.

7. **System trajectory**: Licensed Express Units declined from 251 at start of 2022 to 224 at end of 2024 — a net loss of 27 units (10.8% contraction). New openings averaged ~8/year but were outpaced by closures averaging ~15/year. The dominant closure category is "ceased operations — other reasons" (not termination or non-renewal), suggesting natural attrition of non-traditional locations.

8. **Financial health of franchisor**: The SPV generated $727.3M in total revenue and $729.0M in net income in 2024 (interest income causes NI to exceed revenue). Operating profit margin is effectively 100% as the entity has no operating expenses — all costs are borne by TBC. This financial strength reflects the entire Taco Bell franchise system, not Express alone.

9. **Growth trajectory**: Only 8 new licensed Express Units projected for next fiscal year (CA, FL, GA, HI, TX). No new company-owned projected. The system is in managed decline for Express format.

10. **Territory**: Non-exclusive license for a single specified location. No territorial protection of any kind. Franchisor, KFC, Pizza Hut, and Habit Burger Grill can all open units anywhere without regard to proximity to your Express Unit.

11. **Renewal**: No renewal rights. Successor policy is purely discretionary and can be modified or cancelled at any time. Successor agreements may have materially different terms and conditions.

12. **Disputes**: No current litigation or bankruptcy to disclose. Venue is Orange County, CA courts; governing law is New York — subject to material state overrides in 11 states.

13. **Special risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution (Orange County, CA). The Express format's non-traditional location dependency creates heightened business risk — if the host facility (college, airport, gas station) fails, the Express Unit has limited independent viability.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Taco Bell Franchisor, LLC is a Delaware LLC formed February 23, 2016, operating from 1 Glen Bell Way, Irvine, California 92618. It is part of a securitization chain: TBC → Taco Bell Funding, LLC (Issuer) → Taco Bell Franchisor Holdings, LLC → Taco Bell Franchisor, LLC. A parallel entity, Taco Bell IP Holder, LLC, owns all US trademarks and licenses them to the franchisor for 99 years.

Key affiliates providing services to licensees: TBA (real estate/leasing), YRSG (development services, Plano TX), Yum Connect (technology), TBAS (IT service desk), GCTB LLC (gift cards). Sister brands under YUM umbrella: KFC, Pizza Hut, Habit Burger Grill.

This FDD covers Express Units only — a smaller-format quick-service concept for non-traditional locations (colleges, airports, gas stations, malls, dual-brand facilities). Traditional/In-Line/End-Cap Taco Bell units are offered under a separate FDD.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
Approximately 100% of purchases must comply with franchisor specifications and approved suppliers. This represents an estimated 40-70% of establishment cost and ~40% of operating expenses. Key supply chain elements:

- **RSCS** (Restaurant Supply Chain Solutions): Exclusive purchasing agent for all US Taco Bell units. Operated as cooperative; members include Taco Bell, KFC, and Pizza Hut co-ops.
- **Taco Bell Co-op**: Membership optional but significant advantages (patronage dividends, voting rights). Eligibility: licensees who are also franchisees or operate 25+ units. Cost: $10 membership + $400/unit.
- **McLane Foodservice**: Primary food/supply distributor.
- **Pepsi exclusivity**: Required through Dec 31, 2026. Coca-Cola products explicitly prohibited.
- **Third-party aggregators**: DoorDash, Uber Eats/Postmates, Grubhub — participation MANDATORY. TBC received $46.5M in aggregator brand licensing royalties in FY2024.
- **Affiliate revenues from licensees**: $53.0M (tech hardware/support), $39.1M (leases), $199K (YRSG development services), $168K (Mastercard rebates — credited back to licensees pro rata).

### Training Program
Minimum 7 weeks (may extend to 8), all on-the-job at approved company-owned units. 350-400 hours total OJT plus 8-10 hours food safety certification. Weeks cover progression from team member basics through multi-restaurant leadership. Food safety recertification every 3 years. Tuition-free for licensee + 1 manager; $350/additional trainee. Licensee pays all travel and living expenses. Supervised by Jamie Harrison, Interim Chief People Officer.

### Technology Stack and Costs
Mandatory technology requirements are extensive and expensive:
- **FOH**: Annspire POS (Elo terminals), TKDS (kitchen displays), SmartHub (dual redundant servers), digital menu boards (Stratacache), kiosks (EloTouch), Verifone P400 payment terminals (5 per typical unit), speed of service timers and headsets (HME)
- **BOH**: Comcast broadband (managed service), BOH Computer (Altametrics e*Restaurant → Tracks 2025), Recommended Ordering app
- **Security**: PCI compliance, annual Tech Check assessment (may be required)
- **Total technology costs**: Up to $7,500/year to franchisor + up to $16,600/year to third parties + up to $84,500 one-time to third parties

Network 3.0 testing for 2025 rollout may add ~$50/month to broadband costs.

### Marketing/Advertising
No required advertising fund contribution. No licensee advertising cooperatives. Franchisor has no obligation to advertise in your area or territory. Licensee must purchase One-Step Merchandising at $275/quarter. All advertising materials using Trademarks require prior written approval. The national Taco Bell brand benefits from advertising funded primarily by the Traditional franchise system through NAFA, which Express licensees do not contribute to.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory
None. The License Agreement provides no territorial protection or exclusivity. The license is for a single specified location only. The franchisor may grant temporary impact protection through the Integrated Expansion Policy (a separate document) but this is discretionary.

### Encroachment Risk
Maximum encroachment exposure:
- Franchisor can open additional Taco Bell units of any type anywhere
- KFC, Pizza Hut, and Habit Burger Grill can open anywhere without regard to proximity
- Other licensees and franchisees can be located anywhere
- If purchasing existing units, you may be required to waive any impact protection under the Integrated Expansion Policy
- Franchisor retains exclusive right to pre-packaged products (retail tacos, sauces, etc.) through all distribution channels with no compensation to licensees

### Channel Restrictions
No right to sell pre-packaged food under Trademarks. No delivery or sale from Unit without written consent. No internet, social media, catalog, or telemarketing sales. Licensee has no options, rights of first refusal, or similar rights to acquire additional licenses.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- Initial term: 10 years (Express); 10-20 years (Power Pumpers); 9 months to 5 years (KT Units)
- **No renewal rights**. The License Agreement does not provide renewal rights. A successor policy exists but is purely discretionary — subject to modification or cancellation at any time.
- Successor requirements: operational/financial approval, Unit upgrade (offset, scrape/rebuild, or major remodel), successor fee (≥$11,250), sign release
- **MATERIALLY DIFFERENT TERMS RISK**: The successor agreement may have materially different terms and conditions than the original License Agreement. This is a critical risk — you could operate successfully for 10 years and then face substantially different economic terms for continuation.

### Transfer Provisions
- Requires prior written consent with extensive conditions
- Transfer fees: $7,500 (1-5 units), $1,500/unit (6+), $150,000+ for RA transfers
- Franchisor has 30-day ROFR on any proposed transfer (can exercise on one, all, or any number of units)
- 5-year transfer lock on units purchased from franchisor — consent may be withheld in sole discretion
- Franchisor has right of first offer during 5-year post-APA period
- Relationship Agreement: 6-month acquisition moratorium after signing

### Termination and Death/Disability
- Voluntary termination by licensee = DEFAULT → liquidated damages (greater of $100,000 or 11% of last 12 months Gross Sales)
- 30-day cure for monetary/operational defaults
- Non-curable defaults: untrained manager, denial of access, unauthorized transfer, loss of possession, felony, misrepresentation, bankruptcy, pattern of repeated defaults
- Death/disability: Heirs must notify within 120 days. Franchisor approval needed. If disapproved, 6 months to sell. If not sold, franchisor purchases at FMV.
- **Cross-default**: Any breach of ANY agreement with franchisor/affiliates = breach of ALL agreements.

### Noncompete
- During term: No Mexican-style food restaurant interest (except ≤10% publicly traded). Applies to licensee, immediate family, employees, shareholders.
- Post-term: 1 year, 10-mile radius, only if terminated due to licensee breach.
- Relationship Agreement: Broader — no QSR, Mexican casual dining, or Mexican quick casual dining (during term + 1 year post-sale).

### Dispute Resolution
- No arbitration or mediation for License Agreement disputes
- Forum: Federal or state courts, Orange County, California
- Governing law: New York
- Prevailing party attorneys' fees
- State overrides apply in IL, MN, CA, NY, IN, and others

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

**No financial performance representation is made.** The franchisor explicitly states it does not make "any representations about future financial performance or the past financial performance of any company-owned or licensed Express Units."

This is a significant gap. Prospective licensees have no official guidance on:
- Average or median gross sales for Express Units
- Cost structure or operating margins
- Revenue per square foot for non-traditional locations
- Profitability benchmarks or quartile distributions
- EBITDA or cash flow expectations

The franchisor notes that if you are purchasing an existing Unit, they may provide the actual records of that specific Unit. Any other financial performance information or projections should be reported to management, the FTC, and state regulators.

Given the 10% royalty rate and the system's contraction (27 net licensed unit losses in 3 years), the absence of an FPR is a material concern. Prospective licensees must conduct independent due diligence by contacting existing licensees (listed in Exhibit F) to understand unit economics.

### Key Financial Observations

1. **SPV structure**: The entity has virtually zero operating expenses. It is a pure fee-collection vehicle. TBC bears all operational costs under the management agreement, and the management fee is paid by the Issuer (not the Company). This means the financial statements do not reflect the true cost of supporting the franchise system.

2. **Revenue growth**: 27% over 2 years ($574M→$727M), driven primarily by the Traditional franchise system (6,207 units vs. 224 Express units). FY2024 benefited from a 53rd week adding $12.8M.

3. **Royalty rate insight**: Traditional franchise royalty is typically 5.5% vs. Express at 10%. The Express premium is significant and may explain why the Express system is shrinking while Traditional grows.

4. **Massive distributions**: $708.9M distributed to member in 2024 (97% of net income). These are predominantly non-cash — franchise fees flow through the securitization structure to note holders. The entity retains only interest income from the restricted cash reserve.

5. **Franchise incentives growing**: $43.4M total ($4.3M current + $39.1M long-term), up from $38.3M in 2023. These represent cash/equipment incentives to franchisees to drive new development or same-store sales growth. The growth may indicate franchisor is having to offer more incentives to maintain or grow the system.

6. **Securitization notes**: $3.74B outstanding across 5 tranches with anticipated repayment dates from May 2026 to August 2031. The first tranche ($937.5M at 4.97%) has an anticipated repayment of May 2026 — imminent refinancing. Another follow-on securitization is planned for 2025.

7. **Debt service coverage**: Issuer maintained DSCR above 1.75:1 (well above 1.1:1 rapid amortization trigger). Leverage ratio below 5.0:1. All covenants in compliance.

8. **Accounts receivable surge**: $42M→$58M (+37% YoY). This warrants monitoring — could reflect timing, system growth, or collection pressure.

9. **Deferred franchise fees**: $78.5M total. Contract liabilities of $64.5M (unamortized upfront fees) + $14.0M (deposits for stores not yet opened). These represent future revenue already committed.

10. **Tax status**: Disregarded entity — no income taxes at Company level. Income taxed at TBC/YUM level.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda exist for 11 states. Material overrides include:

**California**: 180-day non-renewal notice required; liquidated damages clauses may be unenforceable under CA Civil Code §1671; CA Franchise Relations Act governs termination/non-renewal rights; general release requirement limited by CA Corp Code §31512.

**Illinois**: Illinois law governs interpretation and enforcement (overrides NY choice of law); forum selection clause for Orange County CA is void for IL causes of action.

**Indiana**: Unlawful to limit litigation in any manner per Indiana Code §23-2-2.7-1(10).

**Minnesota**: Liquidated damage provisions are VOID. 90-day termination notice with 60-day cure required. 180-day non-renewal notice required. Litigation cannot be required outside Minnesota. General release provisions prohibited. Franchisor must indemnify MN licensees against third-party trademark infringement claims.

**New York**: NY General Business Law Article 33 rights preserved and non-waivable. Licensee may terminate on any grounds available by law (replaces the "termination by licensee = default" provision). Choice of law/forum does not waive GBL rights.

**Washington**: WA Franchise Investment Protection Act applies. Attorneys' fees provision applies only if franchisor is the prevailing party.

**Michigan**: Prohibits certain unfair franchise provisions; such provisions are void if present.

**North Dakota**: Certain franchise agreement provisions voided per ND Franchise Investment Law.

**Maryland, Rhode Island, Wisconsin**: Standard state-specific provisions and anti-waiver clauses.

These state addenda materially modify the contract terms for licensees in those states, particularly regarding termination notice, liquidated damages, dispute resolution forum, and governing law.

## J. Unresolveds

1. **What financial performance can an Express licensee expect?** No FPR, and the system is shrinking. Without unit-level economics, prospective licensees are flying blind. **Severity: HIGH.** Source: Item 19.

2. **Why are franchise incentives growing ($34M→$43M)?** Are Express units specifically receiving incentives to stay open? The financial statement notes describe incentives for "new unit openings" and "same-store sales growth" but don't break down by unit type. **Severity: MEDIUM.** Source: Exhibit G notes.

3. **What is the breakdown of $663M in franchise/license fees between Traditional and Express?** The FDD covers only Express, but the financials cover the entire Taco Bell Franchisor entity (6,207 units). Express represents only ~3.6% of total units. **Severity: MEDIUM.** Source: Item 21 notes.

4. **What does the Integrated Expansion Policy actually provide?** Referenced multiple times as a source of possible temporary impact protection, but not included in the FDD body. **Severity: MEDIUM.** Source: Items 1, 12.

5. **Why did 7 company-owned Express units appear in Texas in 2024 (reacquired from licensee)?** What happened to that licensee? Was it a failure or a strategic consolidation by KFC? **Severity: LOW.** Source: Item 20 Table 4.

6. **What are the specific terms of the 2025 planned follow-on securitization?** Could affect fee structures or covenant requirements. Subsequent events note (through March 26, 2025) found "no items requiring disclosure," but the securitization is described as "planned." **Severity: LOW.** Source: Item 1, Exhibit G Note 6.

## K. Contradictions

1. **Royalty rate mismatch**: The FDD states 10% royalty for Express Units. The financial statement notes state "usually 5.5% of sales for traditional franchise agreements and usually 10% of sales for non-traditional units." This is consistent for Express but highlights a significant cost disadvantage vs. Traditional. No contradiction per se, but the differential is not prominently flagged in the FDD body. Items involved: Item 6, Exhibit G Note 2(h). Resolution: Confirmed consistent.

2. **System trajectory vs. projected growth**: Item 20 shows a declining system (-27 licensed units over 3 years), yet Table 5 projects 8 new units. These are not necessarily contradictory — openings can occur even in a declining system — but the projection may be optimistic given recent closure rates of 11-20/year. Items involved: Item 20 Tables 3, 5. Resolution: Noted but not contradictory.

3. **"No renewal rights" vs. successor policy**: Item 17 states clearly "No renewal rights" but then describes an elaborate successor policy. The distinction is legally meaningful (successor ≠ renewal) but may be confusing to prospective licensees. Items involved: Item 17(b), (c). Resolution: Legally distinct; noted for clarity.
