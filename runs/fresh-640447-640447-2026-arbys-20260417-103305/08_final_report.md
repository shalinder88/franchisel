# Final Diligence Report — Arby's Franchisor, LLC — 2026 FDD (Filing 640447)

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size and presence**: Arby's operates 3,265 restaurants across the United States as of December 28, 2025, comprising 2,344 franchised units (including 4 multi-brand locations) and 921 company-owned units. An additional 243 franchised restaurants operate internationally and in Puerto Rico. The system is one of the largest QSR chains in the U.S. with a deli-inspired sandwich concept featuring Meatcraft® proteins.

2. **Entity structure**: The franchisor is Arby's Franchisor, LLC, a single-member Delaware LLC formed in 2015 as a special-purpose entity in a securitization transaction. The entity is an indirect subsidiary of Arby's Restaurant Group, Inc. (ARG), which is itself owned by Inspire Brands, Inc. Inspire Brands is backed by Roark Capital Management and controls a massive portfolio including Buffalo Wild Wings, Dunkin', Baskin-Robbins, Jimmy John's, Sonic, Subway, and numerous others.

3. **Ownership history**: ARG was the original franchisor from May 2006 until November 2015, when a securitization transaction transferred all franchise agreements to the current franchisor entity. Inspire Brands was formed in February 2018 through the merger of Arby's parent and Buffalo Wild Wings' parent. The Dave's Hot Chicken brand was added to the Roark portfolio in May 2025, and Subway was added in April 2024.

4. **Investment range**: The total initial investment for a free-standing leased Arby's Restaurant ranges from $868,550 to $2,456,600. For a non-free-standing location, the range is $651,550 to $1,379,600. Building costs represent the largest component ($400,000–$850,000 for free-standing).

5. **Fee burden**: The ongoing fee burden is substantial. The standard royalty rate is 4% of Gross Sales for Traditional Restaurants (6.2% for Non-Traditional). The advertising obligation totals a minimum of 5.2% of Gross Sales (AFA dues of 3.22%–5.0% plus local market and cooperative area advertising). At full rate, a franchisee pays approximately 9.2%+ of Gross Sales in royalties and advertising before any other fees. The Standard Incentive program significantly reduces first-year royalty to 0.5%, ramping to 4% by Year 6.

6. **Item 19 financial performance**: The 2025 franchised Traditional Restaurant population of 2,182 units generated an average AUV of $1,274,787 (median $1,201,669). The top quartile averaged $1,856,734 while the bottom quartile averaged only $809,495. Notably, 57% of all franchised restaurants fell below the system average. No cost, expense, or profitability data is disclosed.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk**: The 546 restaurants in the 4th quartile averaged $809,495, with the lowest at $390,125. At the standard 4% royalty rate, a restaurant at the bottom quartile average would pay approximately $32,380 in royalties and $42,094 in AFA/advertising fees — a combined fee burden of approximately $74,474, or over 9.2% of sales, before any other operating costs.

8. **Financial health**: The franchisor entity is highly profitable, with net income of $152.95M on $186.1M total revenue (82% margin). However, revenue has declined from $204.9M (2023) to $186.1M (2025), primarily due to the refranchising of company-owned stores reducing affiliate royalty revenue. The entity has no external debt at its level, though the Issuer (parent) has $781.7M in securitization notes outstanding with an anticipated repayment date of July 2027.

9. **Growth trajectory**: 2025 was a transformational year for the franchise system. Franchised openings surged to 136 (from 33 in 2024), driven by 115 company-owned stores sold to franchisees (primarily in FL, MI, NC, TN). However, the overall system shrank by approximately 100 units net, with 70 franchised cessations and 44 company closures. Projected openings for 2026 are modest: 11 franchised, 0 company-owned.

10. **Territory**: The Protected Area for free-standing locations is typically a 1-mile radius but excludes non-traditional venues. Franchisees face encroachment risk from franchisor-operated non-traditional locations inside their Territory, alternative distribution channels (internet, retail), and affiliated Inspire Brands concepts.

11. **Renewal**: The successor franchise agreement will be on the then-current standard form — which may have materially different terms and conditions. This is a significant risk factor for long-term franchisees.

12. **Disputes**: All litigation must be filed in Atlanta, Georgia (subject to state-specific overrides). No arbitration or mediation. Georgia law governs. This forum selection is flagged as a special risk on the cover page.

13. **Special risks**: The sole flagged special risk is out-of-state dispute resolution requiring litigation in Atlanta, Georgia.

14. **Gag clause**: The FDD explicitly acknowledges confidentiality agreements that restrict some current and former franchisees from speaking openly about their experience.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Arby's Franchisor, LLC is a special-purpose vehicle in a complex corporate structure. The entity was created solely for the securitization transaction and has limited permitted activities. All management functions are performed by ARG under a management agreement, for which the franchisor pays a management fee of $31.3M (2025). The franchisor entity does not operate restaurants and has no employees — it simply holds franchise agreements and intellectual property.

This SPV structure means that the franchisee's contractual counterparty is an entity with limited activities and no operating history outside of franchising. The entity's financial health depends entirely on franchise fee and royalty revenues, and its obligations to the Issuer's note holders take priority through the securitization structure.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees:**
- Development Fee: $12,500 per restaurant (waived for Non-Traditional; not applied to franchise fee)
- Franchise Fee: $37,500 (Standard Traditional), $18,750 (Non-Traditional), $0 under Standard Incentive
- Training Fee: $2,100/attendee (expected increase to $4,000 mid-year 2026); waived for 2 managers in first restaurant
- Design Fees: $1,200 for Preliminary Site Layout (PSL), $750–$1,200 for Preliminary Kitchen Layout (PKL)

**Ongoing Fees:**
- Royalty: 4% of Gross Sales (Traditional), 6.2% (Non-Traditional)
- Advertising & Marketing Service Fee (AFA): 3.22%–5.0% of Gross Sales based on tiered DMA structure
- Local Market Advertising + Cooperative Area: Additional amount bringing aggregate to minimum 5.2%
- Learning Management System: $62.99/year per restaurant
- POS Maintenance: $5,000–$10,000/year
- Order Ahead Platform (optional): $50/month + 4% per transaction (35¢ min, $1.25 max); 3% on third-party delivery

**Event-Driven Fees:**
- Renewal: 10% of then-applicable franchise fee
- Transfer: $17,500 (first restaurant); $2,500 (existing franchisee)
- Additional Training: $0–$4,000 (failed inspections)
- Supplier Approval: $2,500–$10,000
- Audits: Expenses + interest if >3% deficiency
- Interest: Up to 18% per year on overdue amounts

**Incentive Programs:**
The Standard Incentive program dramatically reduces early-year costs: royalty ramps from 0.5% (Year 1) through 4.0% (Year 6+), franchise fee is waived, and advertising receives a 75% discount for Years 1-3. The program is available through March 31, 2027 for new Development Agreements. Additional incentives include Relocation ($50,000 royalty credit), VetFran ($10,000 per restaurant up to $100,000), Pioneer ($50,000 per restaurant for first 2 in select New England/DC states), and Early Opening (0% royalty from opening until required opening date, up to 6 months).

### Initial Investment

The total initial investment ranges from $868,550 to $2,456,600 for a free-standing leased location, and $651,550 to $1,379,600 for non-free-standing. The wide range reflects the spectrum from build-to-suit (where landlord incurs most costs) to vacant space conversion (where franchisee bears all costs). Key cost drivers include building costs ($400K–$850K for free-standing), equipment ($225K–$325K), and site costs ($0–$451K).

The current building design is the Inspire image, which is the only permitted design for new restaurants, conversions, and remodels. Typical building sizes range from 1,600 to 3,000 square feet on lots of 30,000 to 40,000 square feet. No financing is offered by the franchisor.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

Approximately 90% of all purchases and leases are subject to franchisor specifications or approved supplier requirements. ARCOP, a non-profit supply chain cooperative funded by upcharges on potato products, roast beef, and frozen breads, negotiates pricing for the entire system. While ARCOP participation is not technically mandatory, currently all domestic franchisees are members.

Franchisees must purchase furniture/fixtures/equipment, food/beverage ingredients, paper goods, uniforms, cleaning services, hygiene programs, auditing services, delivery services, and credit/debit card processing exclusively from approved suppliers. Failure to obtain approval for an unauthorized supplier and purchasing from them constitutes grounds for FA termination. The supplier approval process takes approximately 90 days, and the franchisor may charge the proposed supplier $2,500–$10,000 for evaluation costs.

### Training Program

The Arby's Training Program requires 336 hours (approximately 7 weeks) of entirely on-the-job training at a designated Arby's restaurant. There is no classroom component. The program consists of three phases: Team Member Training (2 weeks, 96 hours covering positional skills), Shift Management Training (3 weeks, 144 hours covering MIC responsibilities), and Critical Thinking & Leadership Training (2 weeks, 96 hours covering management skills). Knowledge checks require a 90% passing score.

For each of the first and second restaurants, franchisees must employ 2 trained managers (4 total). For each additional restaurant, 1 trained manager is required. Training is free for the first 2 managers in the first restaurant; subsequent trainees pay $2,100/attendee (expected to increase to $4,000). The Manual spans 658 pages across 5 sections.

### Technology Stack

The required POS system is the PAR Brink software platform, costing $32,000–$55,000 to purchase with $5,000–$10,000/year in maintenance. The franchisor has unlimited, independent access to POS data (excluding employee personal data). The optional Arby's Order Ahead Platform, provided by affiliate IRB, carries a $50/month fee plus 4% per transaction (35¢ minimum, $1.25 maximum) for order-ahead sales and 3% for third-party delivery orders. A technology steering committee with franchisee representation oversees platform fees.

### Advertising

AFA (AFA Service Corporation) is an independent franchisee-administered organization managing national advertising. Its Board of Directors has decision-making power, and ARG manages day-to-day operations. In FY2025, AFA spent 76.09% on national media, 17.16% on creative/content, 5.58% on merchandising, 0.9% on consumer insights, and 0.27% on G&A. No AFA funds were used for franchise sales solicitation.

Local Cooperative Area (Co-op) advertising is administered by franchisees in each DMA market. Each restaurant has one vote, including company-owned restaurants (which could give the franchisor controlling votes in markets where it has majority presence).

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition

Under the Development Agreement, the Territory is defined by a radius or governmental boundaries. Under the Franchise Agreement, the Protected Area for free-standing restaurants is typically a 1-mile radius from the restaurant location, delineated by radius or boundary streets/highways. Non-Traditional restaurants receive no Protected Area.

### Carve-Outs and Encroachment

Both Territory and Protected Area exclude malls, college/university campuses, hospitals, service plazas, military bases, theme parks, airports, casinos, sports arenas, and sovereign nations. The franchisor reserves the right to operate or franchise non-traditional Arby's restaurants at any of these excluded locations inside the Territory or Protected Area. The franchisor also reserves all alternative distribution channel rights (internet, e-commerce, catalogs, telemarketing, retail) and currently sells consumer packaged goods (frozen fries, Horsey Sauce) at retail stores and online nationwide.

The affiliated brands under Inspire/Roark (Dunkin', Jimmy John's, Sonic, Buffalo Wild Wings, Subway, Dave's Hot Chicken, and many others) may compete in adjacent food service categories. The FDD states the franchisor has no obligation to resolve perceived conflicts between Arby's franchisees and affiliated brands' franchisees regarding territory, customers, or support.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial franchise term is up to 20 years for Traditional Restaurants (may be shorter if the lease is shorter or franchisee is purchasing an existing restaurant). Non-Traditional terms are the shorter of 10 years or the lease term. The successor franchise will be on the then-current standard form as of 12 months before expiration — which **may have materially different terms and conditions**. This "materially different" language is a significant risk factor, as renewal could fundamentally change economic terms.

Renewal requires substantial compliance, no material default in the prior 12 months, a full remodel to then-current image, training compliance, execution of a General Release, application 6–9 months before expiration, and payment of an administrative fee equal to 10% of the then-applicable franchise fee (currently $3,750 for Traditional).

### Transfer

Transfers require franchisor approval, which will not be unreasonably withheld if the transferee meets qualifications. The transfer fee is $17,500 for the first restaurant and $2,500 for existing franchisees. Conditions include good character/reputation, financial qualification, signing current agreements, completing training, correcting deficiencies within 90 days, and executing a General Release.

The franchisor has a right of first refusal on bona fide transfers, except when the buyer qualifies as a "pre-qualified buyer" — meaning they already own and operate at least 5 Arby's restaurants in material compliance, with no defaults, litigation, competing roast beef business, or lease defaults with Arby's.

### Termination

The franchise has no termination right and the franchisor has no without-cause termination right. Curable defaults carry a 30-day cure period (10 days for non-payment, 180 days for casualty). Non-curable defaults resulting in immediate termination include insolvency, bankruptcy, unauthorized transfer, criminal conviction, system duplication, noncompete violation, denial of inspection, and 2+ monetary defaults in 12 months.

### Death and Disability

Under the FA, minority control (<50%) can be transferred to family members or trusts without prior written consent. However, the Development Agreement terminates immediately upon the death of an individual developer, with no provision for disability — a significant continuity gap.

### Noncompete

The noncompete is narrowly scoped to businesses offering roast beef sandwiches for sale within the Protected Area. It applies during the term and for 12 months after termination, covering the franchisee, guarantors, partners, and any 15%+ equity holder.

### Dispute Resolution

There is no arbitration or mediation. All litigation must be filed in federal or state courts closest to the franchisor's principal office (currently Atlanta, Georgia), subject to state-specific overrides. The franchisor may also file where the franchisee resides or does business. Georgia law governs without conflict-of-laws rules, though Georgia's own franchise law applies only if jurisdictional requirements are independently met.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### Population and Methodology

The FDD provides AUVs for 2,182 franchised Traditional Restaurants that operated for the full 2025 fiscal year (December 30, 2024 through December 28, 2025). This represents approximately 93% of all 2,344 franchised restaurants. Exclusions include 21 new restaurants, 6 with extended closures, 115 transferred from company ownership in December 2025, 4 multi-brand locations, 19 Non-Traditional restaurants, and 78 that closed during 2025. All company-owned restaurants are also excluded.

### Overall AUV Performance

The systemwide average AUV was $1,274,787 with a median of $1,201,669. The spread from lowest ($390,125) to highest ($4,008,289) reflects over a 10x range. Only 43% of restaurants exceeded the average, indicating the distribution is skewed upward by high-performing outliers.

### Quartile Analysis

The 1st quartile (546 restaurants) averaged $1,856,734 with a median of $1,762,595. The 2nd quartile averaged $1,345,917. The 3rd quartile averaged $1,086,788. The bottom quartile of 546 restaurants averaged just $809,495, with a median of $821,946 and a floor of $390,125. The gap between 1st and 4th quartile averages is $1,047,239 — meaning the top quartile generates 2.3x the revenue of the bottom quartile.

### Venue Type Analysis

Free-standing locations (1,858 units) averaged $1,267,493. Travel plaza locations (177 units) averaged significantly higher at $1,487,830 — a 17% premium. Other non-free-standing locations (147 units) averaged $1,110,463, the lowest of the three categories.

### Critical Caveats

No cost, expense, or profitability data is provided. The FDD explicitly states these representations "do not reflect the costs of sales, operating expenses or other costs or expenses." Franchisees face widely varying cost structures based on geography, lease terms, labor markets, and operational efficiency. The 2025 Franchised Restaurants average 26 years in operation, meaning they may benefit from favorable lease terms and brand awareness that newer franchisees would not enjoy.

The 78 closed restaurants and 115 recently-refranchised units are excluded from the population, which could bias the results upward. Additionally, some restaurants in the population may be paying reduced royalty rates under various incentive programs while others pay the standard 4%.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### Multi-Year Trajectory

The system has contracted by 150 units over the 2023–2025 period (from 3,415 to 3,265). Company-owned outlets have decreased sharply from 1,110 to 921 — a 17% reduction driven by a massive refranchising initiative in 2025 (115 stores sold to franchisees in December 2025, primarily in Florida, Michigan, North Carolina, and Tennessee).

Franchised units grew modestly from 2,305 to 2,344 (+1.7%) over the same period. The 2025 year saw a dramatic increase in franchised openings (136 vs. 33 in 2024), largely due to the refranchising transactions rather than organic new development.

### Transfer Trends

Transfers totaled 57 in 2025, down from 87 in 2023 but up from 54 in 2024. The 2025 transfers were concentrated in Florida (27), Pennsylvania (14), North Carolina (10), and Virginia (3), suggesting active portfolio reshuffling in key markets.

### Closures and Non-Renewals

In 2025, 70 franchised outlets ceased operations and 8 were not renewed. Texas saw the largest decline (-14), followed by California (-7). The company also closed 44 locations, primarily in Georgia (6), Florida (6), Tennessee (6), North Carolina (7), and Pennsylvania (4).

### Projected Openings

As of December 28, 2025, there are 9 franchise agreements signed but not yet operational, and 11 projected new franchised openings in the current fiscal year. No company-owned openings are projected. The modest pipeline suggests measured growth rather than aggressive expansion.

### Gag Clause

The FDD explicitly states: "During the last 3 fiscal years, we have signed confidentiality agreements with some current and former franchisees. In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with us." This is a material disclosure that prospective franchisees should consider when conducting due diligence through franchisee interviews.

## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Auditor and Opinion

KPMG LLP (Atlanta, GA) issued an unqualified (clean) opinion on both sets of consolidated financial statements. No going-concern language was included. The financial statements were prepared in accordance with U.S. GAAP.

### Arby's Franchisor, LLC — Balance Sheet (December 28, 2025)

Total assets of $302.7 million are dominated by intangible assets ($252.4M), of which $250.0M represents the indefinite-lived Arby's trademark/trade name. This single asset accounts for 83% of total assets. Current assets total $21.2M, including $11.7M in accounts receivable from franchisees and $4.5M from affiliates. Restricted cash of $1.15M is held for securitization reserve requirements.

A notable new addition in 2025 is $28.9M in long-term contract assets (plus $3.4M current), related to a twelve-month incentive program introduced in 2025 to encourage franchisees and affiliates to support strategic initiatives. These represent future payment credits owed to participants.

Total current liabilities are $22.3M, including $15.3M in other current liabilities and $6.1M in affiliate liabilities — both significantly increased from 2024 levels, reflecting the new incentive program payables. Long-term deferred revenue (franchise fees recognized over the term) stands at $9.7M.

Member's equity totals $270.5M, up from $254.4M in 2024, reflecting net income of $153.0M less distributions of $136.8M.

### Arby's Franchisor, LLC — Income Statement (FY2025)

Total revenues of $186.1M comprise franchise fees and royalty revenues of $112.0M (from franchisees), $68.5M (from affiliate/company-owned restaurants), and $5.6M in other revenues (training fees and brand licensing). Revenue has declined for three consecutive years ($204.9M in 2023 → $191.7M in 2024 → $186.1M in 2025), driven by declining affiliate royalty revenues as company-owned stores are sold to franchisees or closed.

Total costs are remarkably low at $33.2M — almost entirely the $31.3M management fee paid to ARG — yielding operating income of $152.9M (82% operating margin). Net income was $152.95M.

The entity is not subject to federal or state income taxes as a single-member LLC; taxes are the responsibility of ARG/Inspire.

### Cash Flows

Operating cash flow of $136.8M was entirely distributed to the member ($136.8M), resulting in zero change in restricted cash. This pattern of distributing substantially all operating cash to the parent is consistent across all three years.

### Securitization Structure

The Arby's Securitization Notes at the Issuer level total $781.7M (2020 Class A-2, anticipated repayment July 2027, legal maturity July 2050). A $300M Variable Funding facility has no drawn balance but $6.9M in outstanding letters of credit. If the notes are not repaid or refinanced by the anticipated repayment date, additional interest accrues. The franchisor entity, along with IP Holder, acts as a guarantor of these notes and has pledged substantially all of its assets.

### Key Financial Observations

- **Leverage**: No direct debt at franchisor level, but the entity guarantees $781.7M in securitization notes at the parent level and has pledged substantially all assets.
- **Distributions**: $136.8M distributed in 2025 (vs. $163.4M in 2024 and $163.3M in 2023) — the entity is a cash distribution vehicle.
- **Related party**: Virtually all costs ($31.3M management fee) are paid to ARG, the parent. Results of operations may not be indicative of standalone performance.
- **Declining revenue**: Revenue decline of 9.2% over 2 years reflects system contraction and refranchising.
- **Trademark asset**: The $250M indefinite-lived trademark represents 83% of total assets. Annual impairment testing showed no impairment in 2023, 2024, or 2025.
- **Contract assets**: $32.3M in new contract assets (2025) related to a strategic incentive program — these represent future payment credits to franchisees/affiliates.

## J. Unresolveds

1. **U1 — Refranchised units excluded from Item 19** (Medium): The 115 company-owned restaurants sold to franchisees in December 2025 are excluded from the Item 19 population. Their performance under franchise operation — potentially with different cost structures, staffing levels, and management quality — is unknown. This is a significant data gap for prospective franchisees interested in markets where refranchising occurred (FL, MI, NC, TN).

2. **U2 — Royalty rate variation in Item 19 cohort** (Medium): The Item 19 population likely includes restaurants paying different royalty rates — standard 4%, various incentive ramp rates (0.5%–3%), and possibly legacy rates under prior Development Agreements. The AUV figures represent top-line Gross Sales, but the actual net economic position of a new franchisee paying standard rates would differ from one paying incentive rates.

3. **U3 — ARG's role vs. contractual status** (Low): ARG provides all management services to the franchise system under a management agreement, but is not a party to the Franchise Agreement or Development Agreement and does not guarantee the franchisor's obligations. If ARG were to cease performing under the management agreement, franchisees' recourse would be limited to the SPV franchisor entity.

4. **U4 — No disability provision in DA** (Medium): The Development Agreement terminates immediately upon the death of an individual developer but contains no provision for disability. This creates a continuity gap — a disabled developer could lose all development rights with no cure period.

5. **U5 — FA clause walk pending** (Low): The Franchise Agreement (Exhibit D, pp. 236–275) has not been directly clause-walked. Key operative terms have been extracted via the Item 17 chart, but unusual clauses, specific cure language, cross-default mechanics, and guaranty scope details require direct reading. Deferred to A2.
