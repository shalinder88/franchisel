## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size:** 6,701 U.S. restaurants as of December 31, 2024 — comprising 5,524 franchised and 1,177 company-owned. The worldwide system totals 19,732 restaurants in 120+ countries. The company-owned count surged in 2024 due to the Carrols Restaurant Group acquisition (1,023 restaurants). RBI is the world's third-largest QSR company by total restaurant count (32,125 across all brands).

2. **Entity structure:** Burger King Company LLC is a Florida LLC formed February 4, 2022, as part of an internal reorganization from Burger King Corporation (the predecessor). BKC is a wholly-owned indirect subsidiary of Restaurant Brands International LP (Ontario), whose general partner is Restaurant Brands International Inc. (Canadian public company). 3G Restaurant Brands Holdings LP holds approximately 26% combined voting power.

3. **Ownership history:** The Burger King brand was founded in 1954 as Burger King of Miami, Inc. It became BK Corporation in 1963. Through multiple ownership changes (Pillsbury, Grand Metropolitan, Diageo, TPG/Bain/Goldman Sachs, 3G Capital), it became part of the RBI family in 2014 when 3G Capital merged it with Tim Hortons. The current LLC assumed all BK Corporation assets in August 2022. BK Corporation was dissolved December 2022. In May 2024, RBI acquired Carrols Restaurant Group (the largest BK franchisee) for approximately $508M, bringing 1,023 restaurants in-house.

4. **Investment range:** $348,400 (Indoor MRS, smallest format) to $4,705,600 (Traditional Freestanding, full format), all excluding real estate purchase costs. A traditional freestanding restaurant, the most common format, ranges from $2,049,200 to $4,705,600. Real property for freestanding may cost an additional $250,000 to $2,000,000 if purchasing.

5. **Fee burden:** The base ongoing fee stack is among the highest in QSR. Royalty at 4.5% of Gross Sales plus advertising contribution at 4.5% (currently at maximum) totals 9.0% before any other charges. Adding the Digital Services Fee ($110/month + 1% of digital sales, capped $4,500/year), Investment Spending (up to 2% of Gross Sales), BK University ($700/year), BK Foundation ($1,000/year), and other technology costs, the all-in recurring fee burden can exceed 11% of Gross Sales before rent. For BKL properties, rent adds an additional 8.5%-10% of Gross Sales plus base rent.

6. **Item 19 highlights:** Traditional restaurant average gross sales of $1,671,613 and median of $1,579,183 for calendar year 2024. Only 43% of traditional restaurants meet or exceed the average. Non-traditional restaurants average $1,321,324 (median $1,155,563). Critical caveat: Item 19 discloses gross sales only — no cost data, EBITDA, or net income is provided. The Fuel the Flame agreement references a 4-Wall EBITDA threshold of $175,000-$230,000 per restaurant, but this is not an Item 19 disclosure.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk:** Among traditional restaurants, 4.8% fall below $700K in annual sales, and 12.9% fall below $1.1M. Among non-traditional restaurants, the picture is more concerning: 29.8% fall below $900K, and 15.9% fall below $700K. Kiosk-format fuel co-branded restaurants average only $973,531.

8. **Financial health:** RBI consolidated net income was $1.445B in 2024. However, this masks significant leverage: total debt of approximately $13.7B against Adjusted Operating Income of $2.4B implies leverage of roughly 5.7x. The BK segment contributed $410M in segment income (17% of RBI total). Cash distributions to shareholders totaled $1.029B in 2024. The Carrols acquisition added $508M in investing outflows and $1,988M in total assets.

9. **Growth trajectory:** The US system is shrinking. Total US restaurants declined from 7,042 (2022) to 6,778 (2023) to 6,701 (2024). While the headline decline of 77 in 2024 appears modest, it masks a dramatic structural shift: 1,061 franchised restaurants were reacquired (primarily Carrols), while only 63 new franchised restaurants opened. Projected new openings for 2025 are 50 total (48 franchised + 2 company). The net franchised count dropped from 6,640 to 5,524 in a single year — a 16.8% decline.

10. **Territory:** No exclusive territory is granted under the Franchise Agreement. Development Agreements provide a geographic territory but it is not exclusive. BKC and its affiliates (Tim Hortons, Popeyes, Firehouse Subs) may operate in the same markets. Alternative distribution channels (grocery, convenience, internet) are fully reserved by BKC.

11. **Renewal:** There is no right of renewal. Instead, franchisees may apply for a successor Franchise Agreement, which may have "materially different terms" than the expiring agreement. A general release, building upgrades, and current franchise fee are required. Big-Box and Delivery restaurant franchisees have no successor option.

12. **Disputes and litigation:** All disputes must be litigated in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida or Miami-Dade County state court, under Florida law. This is flagged as a special risk in the FDD cover page. There are 8 pending material litigation matters, though BKC is a direct party in only 2. The largest pending claim is $500M+ (FIF v. BK Corporation in Ontario, dating to 2013).

13. **Special risks:** (a) Out-of-state dispute resolution in Florida — may force less favorable settlements and higher litigation costs. (b) Mandatory non-binding mediation for development disputes before litigation — may delay court resolution.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Burger King Company LLC offers two forms of franchise ownership: Individual/Owner-Operator and Entity. Under Individual ownership, the Operating Partner must retain 50% equity and devote full time to operations. Under Entity ownership, the Managing Owner must own at least 25% and have authority to bind the entity. A Legacy Entity form allows delegation to a Managing Director. Corporate Addendum permits large institutional operators.

BKC also offers the Crown Your Career Program (employee-to-franchisee pathway), the Carrols Refranchise Program (purchase of BKC/Carrols-owned restaurants), and the Development Agreement for multi-unit development.

### Fee Stack

**Initial fees:**
- Franchise fee: $50,000 standard (20-year term); minimum $15,000; prorated for shorter terms
- Application fee: $250/individual or $5,000/entity
- Training fee: $7,500 per trainee
- TRA deposit: $5,000; MTRA deposit: $10,000 × number of restaurant openings
- Development Agreement prepaid franchise fees: $50,000 per restaurant committed

**Ongoing fees (from Item 6):**
- Royalty: 4.5% of monthly Gross Sales
- Advertising contribution: Up to 4.5% (currently 4.5% as of January 1, 2025)
- Digital Services Fee: $110/month/restaurant + 1% of digital sales (capped $4,500/year)
- Investment Spending (DMA): Up to 2.0% of Gross Sales (may be mandatory if 66.7%+ in DMA participate)
- BK University / Training Material: $700/year/restaurant
- Service Desk Fee: $750-$1,200/year/restaurant
- BK Foundation: $1,000/year/restaurant (purchase or fundraise)
- Gift Card transaction fee: ~1.8% of redeemed sales
- Static Menu Board Kit: $200-$300/month (if no outdoor digital board)
- Network services: $220-$450/month

**Event-driven fees:**
- Transfer: $2,000 first restaurant + $500 each additional (+$175 weekend/holiday surcharge)
- Franchise Extension: $2,500/year
- Audit expenses: Cost of audit if gross sales understated >2%
- Follow-up walk-thru: $1,500
- Background check: $395-$15,000
- One-time cure fee (TRA): $10,000
- Sales Transfer Study: $3,000-$8,000
- Product testing/approval: Up to $5,000
- Deferred Remodel Default: Royalty increases to 6.0% or 7.5%

**Reduced royalty programs available:**
- 2024 Multi-Unit DIP: Royalty starts at 2.5% (Year 1), stepping up to 4.5% (Year 6+)
- Development Agreement (FSS Grade A/B): Reductions of 2.5% (Year 1) tapering to 0% (Year 7+) from Base Fee
- RTF2 Remodel: Negotiated rate or elected between 4.5%-6.0%

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
BKC establishes standards and specifications for most goods and services. Purchases from approved suppliers represent approximately 65-90% of establishment costs and 30-60% of non-occupancy operating costs. BKC is the sole supplier of digital/loyalty technology platforms, and there are sole suppliers for network services, digital menu boards, gift card services, cashless payment processing, quality assurance inspections, and certain food/packaging/equipment.

Restaurant Services Inc. (RSI) is an independent not-for-profit purchasing cooperative that acts as purchasing agent for most supplies. RSI has a 21-member board (18 franchisees from 9 districts, 1 minority association rep, 1 BKC rep, 1 independent). RSI issues patronage dividends to members.

BKC receives payments from suppliers based on franchisee purchases. In 2024, supplier contributions to the national advertising fund totaled approximately 8.4% of consolidated fund revenue. BKC's total revenue from required franchisee purchases and leases was approximately $227 million (16% of BKC's $1.427B total revenue).

The supplier approval process typically takes 90-160 days. BKC officers hold publicly traded shares in General Mills, Johnson & Johnson, Kraft Heinz, and PepsiCo — all approved suppliers.

### Training Program
The Burger King Franchisee Onboarding Training Program requires up to 20 hours of classroom/online training and up to 320 hours of in-restaurant training. Fee: $7,500 per trainee. Location: Miami, FL or designated locations. ServSafe certification required (Illinois requires classroom only, no online). Training staff average 3+ years QSR/hospitality experience. Ongoing training may be required ($7,500 per person).

### Technology Stack
- POS system: $20,000-$25,000 per restaurant (approved vendors only; includes 12-month warranty)
- Network standardization: $220-$450/month (approved vendor required)
- Data reporting systems: $0-$300 setup + $200-$1,200/year
- Digital Services Fee: $110/month + 1% digital sales (cap $4,500/year) — covers BK app, website, third-party ordering, Royal Perks loyalty
- POS software maintenance, menu maintenance, help desk: $2,000-$4,000/year
- Free guest Wi-Fi mandatory
- Royal Perks loyalty program mandatory for all restaurants

### Marketing Fund
Advertising fund administered by BKC. In fiscal 2024, expenditures were: Working Media 79.22%, Production 3.72%, Agency Fees 6.60%, POP Materials 1.04%, Other 9.42%. Contractual minimum of 65% on working media. Annual audit available to Franchisee Association. NFA/FAC has advisory role but no decision-making power.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory
There is no exclusive or protected territory under the Franchise Agreement. The FDD states this explicitly and repeatedly across Items 1, 12, and 17. Development Agreements provide a geographic "Territory" but even this is not exclusive.

### Franchisor Competition Rights
BKC and its affiliates retain the right to:
- Establish restaurants at any location, including near existing franchisees
- Sell through alternative channels (grocery, convenience, internet)
- Grant rights to military installations (Army & Air Force Exchange Service)
- Acquire competing restaurant chains
- Restrict franchisee advertising, internet use, and social media use of BK marks

### Sister Brand Overlap
RBI's portfolio includes Tim Hortons, Popeyes Louisiana Kitchen, and Firehouse Subs. These brands may operate in the same markets as Burger King. The FDD acknowledges menu overlap (sandwiches, fries, desserts, beverages). Conflict resolution between BK franchisees and sister brand franchisees is handled by BKC management, but BKC is "not responsible for resolving conflicts."

### Channel Restrictions on Franchisees
Franchisees may not use BK marks on any website, social media, domain name, or email address without BKC approval. Delivery service through approved aggregators is mandatory. Value Menu items are subject to maximum pricing set by BKC.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
The initial franchise term is 20 years for traditional freestanding restaurants. Non-traditional facilities may have shorter terms based on property control. There is no right of renewal. Franchisees may apply for a successor Franchise Agreement (up to 20 years), but the successor agreement "may have materially different terms." A general release, building upgrades, current franchise fee, and compliance with all requirements are conditions for successor. Big-Box and Delivery Restaurant franchisees have no successor option at all.

### Transfer Provisions
BKC consent required for all transfers. Buyer must be approved, complete training, have all monies current, pay transfer fees ($2,000 + $500/additional), and sign current Franchise Agreement (which may have materially different terms). BKC has right of first refusal on same terms as any third-party offer. Under Crown Your Career, BKC may withhold transfer consent for any reason during the first 5 years. Under Carrols Refranchise, consent may be withheld until remodels are completed.

### Termination and Death/Disability
Cure periods range from 5 days (operational/health/sanitation defaults) to 180 days (failure to meet minimum financial criteria). Non-curable defaults include bankruptcy, criminal conviction, unfair competition, false statements, and unauthorized transfer. In the event of death or disability, the heir must be approved by BKC or sell within 12 months. BKC has the option to operate/manage the restaurant pending resolution.

### Noncompete
During the term: no interest in any other hamburger business. Post-term: 1 year within 2 miles of the restaurant. Item 14 adds an AI-specific restriction: franchisees may not use trade secrets for machine learning, AI model training, or input into generative AI platforms without prior BKC approval.

### Dispute Resolution
Venue: U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida or applicable state court in Miami-Dade County. Governing law: Florida (subject to state-specific overrides in Exhibit P). Mandatory non-binding mediation for development disputes only. No mandatory arbitration. Delivery Restaurant franchisees waive all rights to dispute development of another restaurant.

### Litigation History
There are 8 pending material matters and 6 concluded material matters. BKC is a direct party in only 2 pending matters (Arrington antitrust class action and Chaus enforcement). The largest pending claim is $500M+ (FIF v. BK Corporation, Ontario, 2013). The Arrington case (employee no-solicitation clause challenge) has mediation ordered by April 3, 2026. The Plymouth County v. RBI case (Carrols acquisition fairness) has trial set for February 2027. Zero terminations were reported in all three years 2022-2024.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda exist in Exhibit P for the following states with franchise registration requirements: California, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin.

**Washington addendum (fully read) includes material overrides:**
- Fair and reasonable pricing requirement (RCW 19.100.180)
- Treble damages available (RCW 19.100.190)
- Good faith dealing requirement overrides "reasonable business judgment" standard
- Indemnification limited: no obligation for losses caused by franchisor's negligence/misconduct
- Attorneys' fees: only if franchisor is prevailing party
- Noncompete void unless employee earnings >$100K/year (adjusted for inflation)
- Nonsolicitation restrictions on hiring between franchisees prohibited
- No waiver of franchise law claims via questionnaires or acknowledgments
- No restriction on communicating with regulators

**Michigan notice (included in front matter):** Prohibits unfair provisions including: prohibition on joining franchisee association; requirement to assent to release depriving rights; termination without good cause (30-day cure minimum); refusal to renew without fair compensation; requiring out-of-state arbitration/litigation; refusal to permit transfer without good cause.

**Other state addenda:** Not individually read. Expected to contain similar franchise-protective overrides for termination, renewal, noncompete, venue, and indemnification provisions.
