## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size and maturity**: LXR Hotels & Resorts operates just 4 franchised hotels in the United States as of December 31, 2024, located in Phoenix (Arizona Biltmore), Santa Monica (Oceana), Las Vegas (Crockfords), and Seattle (Hotel 1000). There are zero company-owned hotels and zero projected openings. The brand added one property in 2024 (Arizona Biltmore, likely a brand conversion). This is an ultra-small system with extremely limited US operational track record.

2. **Entity structure and ownership**: The franchisor is Hilton Franchise Holding LLC, a Delaware LLC formed in 2007. It is a wholly owned subsidiary of Hilton Domestic Operating Company Inc., which is ultimately owned by Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. (NYSE: HLT). The same franchisor entity licenses all Hilton brands in the US — the financial statements cover the entire Hilton US franchise portfolio, not just LXR.

3. **Ownership history**: Hilton Franchise Holding LLC has been the LXR franchisor since September 14, 2018. The Hilton corporate lineage dates to 1946 (Hilton Hotels Corporation). The 2017 spinoff of Park Hotels & Resorts divested nearly all company-owned hotels, creating the current franchise-focused model.

4. **Investment range**: $5,364,048 to $845,773,369 for a typical 150-room hotel, excluding real property. The franchisor explicitly acknowledges it does "not yet have actual initial investment information for an LXR hotel." The extraordinary width of this range limits its practical utility for investment planning.

5. **Fee burden**: The core recurring fee load is 5% royalty + 4% program fee (potentially rising to 5%) + 4.3% Hilton Honors = approximately 13.3%+ of Gross Rooms Revenue before accounting for technology fees ($70K–$171K/year in annual maintenance), distribution fees, and quality assurance fees. The GRR definition includes mandatory guest fees (resort fees), making the effective base broader than standard room rate.

6. **Item 19 — No financial performance representations**: The franchisor provides no FPR. With only 4 hotels and no FPR, a prospective franchisee has no franchisor-provided revenue or profitability benchmarks.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk factors**: (a) No renewal right — franchise is entirely non-renewable; (b) all franchisor assets pledged for parent debt; (c) ultra-small system with zero projected growth; (d) gag clause limits franchisee contact; (e) mandatory spa and Forbes Travel Guide costs unique to luxury tier.

8. **Financial health**: The franchisor entity reported $1.502 billion total revenue and $1.488 billion net income in 2024, with 99%+ operating margins. However, this reflects the entire Hilton US franchise portfolio. Cash on hand was just $317K; $1.5 billion was distributed to the parent. All assets are pledged as collateral for Hilton parent debt maturing 2025–2033.

9. **Growth trajectory**: Flat to minimal growth. 3 hotels from 2018–2023, 4 in 2024. Zero projected new openings. LXR appears to be a niche luxury collection within Hilton's broader strategy rather than an actively expanding franchise system.

10. **Territory**: Non-exclusive with no standard protected territory. A "Restricted Area" may be negotiated but is typically limited in duration and riddled with carve-outs (existing hotels, other Hilton brands, acquisitions, gaming, timeshare).

11. **Renewal**: No right to renew. Re-licensing only at franchisor's sole discretion and may require "materially different terms and conditions." This is a critical contract risk.

12. **Disputes and litigation**: Multiple pending antitrust class actions against Hilton Worldwide regarding hotel pricing algorithms (IDeaS/SAS/Amadeus/CoStar). Recent settlements with Texas ($2.1M) and Nebraska ($300K) over mandatory guest fee disclosure practices.

13. **Special risks**: Out-of-state dispute resolution (Virginia; backup New York). New York governing law.

14. **Forbes Travel Guide**: Mandatory participation at $22,400/year including on-site training and quality assessments — a luxury-specific cost not present in other Hilton brands.

Hilton Franchise Holding LLC serves as the single franchisor for all Hilton brands in the US. The entity was formed in September 2007 and began LXR franchising in September 2018. LXR is described as a "collection" brand — each property retains its own name and identity while operating under LXR standards.

The corporate structure is: Hilton Franchise Holding LLC → Hilton Domestic Operating Company Inc. → Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. (NYSE: HLT). Key affiliates include Hilton Reservations Worldwide (reservations), Hilton Supply Management (procurement), Hilton Honors Worldwide (loyalty), and Hilton Systems Solutions (IT).

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition and Carve-Outs
The standard Franchise Agreement provides NO protected territory. The grant is a non-exclusive license at a specified location.

A "Restricted Area Provision" may be negotiated for new developments and conversions (not typically for change of ownership or re-licensing). If granted, the boundaries depend on "the relevant market in the immediate area and competitive circumstances" and vary by hotel — no standard formula.

The Restrictive Period is typically shorter than the franchise term. "Historically, we have extended the Restrictive Period for the full term of the Franchise Agreement; however we do not intend to do so in the future."

**Key carve-outs from any Restricted Area:**
- Existing or approved Brand hotels
- Other Hilton brands (DoubleTree, Hampton, Conrad, Waldorf Astoria, etc.)
- Hotels acquired as part of a 4+ hotel chain transaction
- Gaming-oriented hotels
- Shared ownership/timeshare properties
- Hotels opening after the Restrictive Period

### Channel Restrictions and Franchisor Rights
- Franchisor may engage in any competitive business anywhere, under any brand
- Strategic Partners (SLH, AutoCamp, Evermore, Field Station) may operate in your area
- No relocation permitted
- No right of first refusal for additional franchises
- Must use Reservation Service exclusively for outbound referrals
- Rate parity may be required across distribution channels
- No cross-marketing with non-System hotels without consent

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- **New Construction**: 23 years from Effective Date
- **Conversion**: 10–20 years from Opening Date
- **Change of Ownership**: Remaining term or as approved
- **Renewal**: None. The franchise is non-renewable. Re-licensing is at the franchisor's sole discretion and may require "materially different terms and conditions." There is no mechanism guaranteeing a franchisee can continue operating under the brand after the term expires. This is a material "different terms" risk.

### Transfer Provisions
**Permitted Transfers** (no change of control): Publicly traded equity transfers need no notice. Private transfers <50% with no control change need no notice. Affiliate/family/death transfers require 60 days' notice + consent + $5,500 fee.

**Change of Ownership Transfers**: 60 days' notice, not in default, transferee meets current standards, signs current-form franchise agreement, pays $150,000 application fee, may require PIP. No transfer to Competitors or Sanctioned Persons.

No franchisor right of first refusal. No option to purchase. Franchisee must notify before marketing equity interests.

### Termination and Death/Disability
Franchisee may NOT unilaterally terminate — doing so is a material breach triggering the termination fee.

Franchisor may terminate with cause after cure periods (10 days for payment, 30 days for other defaults). 16 categories of non-curable defaults allow immediate termination, including: pattern of non-compliance within 24 months, 3 defaults in 12 months, insolvency, loss of possession, 5+ days non-operation, fraud, becoming a Competitor.

On death of a natural person owner, franchise may transfer to immediate family within 1 year if they meet then-current requirements.

### Noncompete Restrictions
**During term**: Franchisee may not own or be licensor/franchisor of a competing brand. May be a franchisee of a competitor, manage a competitor's hotel (if not exclusive manager), or hold a minority interest.

**Post-term**: No noncompete covenant. However, must cease all use of Marks, System, and Proprietary Information immediately upon termination/expiration.

**State overrides**: Washington limits noncompete enforceability based on earnings thresholds ($100K employees, $250K independent contractors).

### Dispute Resolution
- **Governing Law**: New York (without choice-of-law principles)
- **Forum**: US District Court for Eastern District of Virginia (Alexandria), or Fairfax County VA state court, or New York NY
- **Franchisor may sue where hotel is located**
- **Arbitration**: None (except Puerto Rico — AAA/JAMS, venue Fairfax VA, seat NY)
- **State overrides**: Multiple states (CA, IL, MD, MN, ND, RI, WA) may override forum selection and/or governing law

### Litigation History
5 pending actions: All are class-action antitrust cases alleging hotel pricing algorithm conspiracies (IDeaS, SAS, Amadeus, CoStar). Hilton Worldwide is a defendant.

6 concluded actions including: Texas AG ($2.1M, mandatory guest fee disclosure); Nebraska AG ($300K, fee disclosure); Portland Hotel Ownership (Curio dispute, $30M counterclaim, settled with mutual releases); San Pedro Inn (Hampton termination, settled); Soule (Hawaii resort fee class action, $178K + vouchers); US v. HHC (1970 antitrust, consent decree).

### Key Financial Observations

**Leverage and parent dependency**: The entity retains virtually no cash ($317K at year-end 2024). It distributes $1.4–1.55 billion annually to its parent. All assets ($1.27 billion) are pledged as collateral for Hilton parent debt maturing 2025–2033. The entity operates as a pass-through for Hilton's franchise royalty stream.

**Distributions vs. retention**: Distributions to Hilton affiliates exceeded net income in 2022 ($1.55B distributed on $1.23B net income), were approximately equal in 2023 ($1.4B on $1.37B), and slightly exceeded in 2024 ($1.5B on $1.49B). Retained earnings declined from $541M (2023) to $529M (2024).

**Related party concentration**: $703.9M (55% of total assets) is receivable from Hilton affiliates through the centralized cash management system, over which the entity has no control.

**Asset composition**: Franchise contract intangible assets grew from $238.5M (2023) to $392.7M (2024), primarily due to the $85M acquisition of Graduate brand franchise contracts in May 2024.

**Revenue growth**: Total revenue grew 22% from 2022 to 2024, driven by franchise royalty fee growth (21% over same period), reflecting system-wide RevPAR recovery and net hotel additions across all Hilton brands.

**No US income taxes**: Entity is treated as a disregarded entity for US tax purposes; all taxable income flows to Hilton Worldwide.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda exist in both the Franchise Agreement (Exhibit D-1) and Disclosure Document (Exhibit J-1) for the following jurisdictions:

### States with Addenda
California, Hawaii, Illinois, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Puerto Rico (FA only), Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington

### Material Overrides by State

**California**: Liquidated damages clauses may be unenforceable per CC §1671. NY governing law may be unenforceable. VA venue may be unenforceable. General release excludes CA franchise law claims.

**Hawaii**: General release does not relieve franchisor from liability under Hawaii franchise law.

**Illinois**: IL Franchise Disclosure Act governs for IL licensees. May not waive rights under Act through arbitration or otherwise (except settlements).

**Maryland**: MD law may supersede FA on termination/renewal. Franchise must be sued within 3 years of grant. General release does not relieve liability under MD law. No questionnaire or acknowledgment can waive claims.

**Michigan**: Prohibits: no-association clauses, pre-contract releases, termination without good cause (30-day cure), refusal to renew without FMV compensation (if term <5 years), out-of-state litigation/arbitration, refusal of transfer without good cause.

**Minnesota**: 90 days termination notice (60 to cure), 180 days non-renewal notice. General release does not relieve MN franchise law liability. Liquidated damages prohibited. Out-of-state litigation, jury waiver, and liquidated damages provisions are prohibited.

**New York**: Franchisee may terminate on any grounds available by law. NY franchise law governs NY causes of action. General release may not be enforceable. Injunctive relief requires "proper proofs."

**North Dakota**: All liquidated damages deleted. ND law governs (supersedes NY choice of law). Out-of-state venue deleted. Jury waiver deleted. General release does not relieve ND franchise law liability.

**Puerto Rico**: Arbitration required (AAA/JAMS), venue Fairfax VA, seat NY. Franchisor may seek temporary/injunctive relief in court.

**Rhode Island**: General release excludes RI franchise law claims. Out-of-state venue and non-RI governing law void for RI Act claims.

**Virginia**: Termination without "reasonable cause" is unlawful under VA Retail Franchising Act.

**Washington**: WA franchise law (RCW 19.100.180) may supersede FA on termination/renewal. General release excludes WA Act claims (except negotiated settlements with independent counsel). Noncompete void if employee earns <$100K or independent contractor earns <$250K. No-poach provisions are void.
