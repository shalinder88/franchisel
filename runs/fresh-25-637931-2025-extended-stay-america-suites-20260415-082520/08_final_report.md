# Final Diligence Report — Extended Stay America Suites

**Filing**: 637931-2025 (Wisconsin DFI)
**Franchisor**: ESH Strategies Franchise LLC
**Issuance Date**: March 27, 2025
**Extraction Date**: April 15, 2026

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System overview**: Extended Stay America Suites is a mid-scale extended-stay hotel franchise system owned equally by Starwood Capital Group and Blackstone through a multi-layered holding structure. The franchisor entity (ESH Strategies Franchise LLC) was formed in Delaware in 2010, began offering franchises in 2017, and has never owned or operated a hotel. All hotel operations are conducted by affiliate ESA Management LLC. The system targets self-sufficient, value-conscious extended-stay and transient guests with fully furnished rooms including in-room kitchens.

2. **System size and trajectory**: As of December 31, 2024, the system comprised 446 ESA Suites hotels (107 franchised, 339 company-owned). The system has been shrinking rapidly — from 643 hotels at end-2022 to 446 at end-2024 — driven by the strategic rebranding of company-owned hotels to the lower-tier ESA Select Suites brand (123 hotels rebranded in 2023-2024) and sales to franchisees and third parties. Franchised hotels have grown modestly from 93 to 107. Only 1 projected new franchised opening. This represents a system in active restructuring, not growth.

3. **Ownership and entity structure**: The franchisor sits within a deep private equity ownership chain: ESH Strategies Franchise LLC → ESH Hospitality Strategies LLC → Eagle Strategies Holdings LLC → equally owned by SCG (Starwood Capital Group) entities and Blackstone Real Estate Partners IX entities. Related hotel brands include ESA Premier Suites, ESA Select Suites, Yotel (SCG), and Shimao (SCG/China). The ownership structure introduces risks typical of PE-owned franchise systems including potential for leveraged transactions and ownership changes.

4. **Investment range**: New construction of a 124-room prototype hotel requires $9.17M–$14.12M excluding land (and land costs can be substantial given the ~2-acre requirement). Conversion of an existing 100-room extended-stay property ranges from $205K to $3.95M, a vast range reflecting the condition variability of conversion candidates.

5. **Fee burden**: The ongoing fee load is substantial at 10.0% of Gross Room Revenue (5.5% royalty + 4.5% system services fee), plus booking commissions, a 5% digital marketing commission on paid digital channel revenue, and monthly PMS/hosting/support fees of $3.87/room. GRR is broadly defined to include loyalty redemptions, group booking rebates, business interruption insurance proceeds, and mandatory guest fees.

6. **Item 19 — Franchised hotel economics**: Franchised hotels achieved average RevPAR of $46.85 and ADR of $59.89 in 2024, significantly below owned-hotel averages of $62.35 and $80.87 respectively. For a 124-room hotel at franchised average RevPAR, annual room revenue would be approximately $2.12M. At 10% ongoing fees, the franchisee would pay approximately $212K/year in royalties and system services fees alone, before booking commissions and technology costs. The ADR gap of ~$21/night between owned and franchised hotels is material and unexplained in the FDD.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk**: The FDD does not provide expense data, EBITDA, or profit margin information. Without cost structure disclosure, franchisees cannot assess profitability from the FDD alone. At franchised average ADR of ~$60/night for an extended-stay property, margins may be tight after the 10% fee burden, property-level operating costs, and debt service.

8. **Financial health of franchisor**: The franchisor entity reported $25.8M revenue and $1.5M net income in 2024, improving from a $2.2M loss in 2023. The improvement was driven by franchise system growth (25 hotels added in 2024 across all ESA brands) and $2.1M in contract termination fees. The entity has $9.2M cash and positive member's capital of $8.2M. Clean audit opinion with emphasis on related party transactions. Not subject to federal income tax as an LLC.

9. **Growth trajectory**: Minimal — 1 projected new franchised opening. The franchise program appears to be a secondary strategy alongside the owned portfolio, though the owned portfolio is shrinking.

10. **Territory risk**: Non-exclusive; territory protections (if granted) cover only ESA Suites brand, not ESA Premier Suites, ESA Select Suites, or other related brands. Franchisor reserves extensive rights including operating competing brands in territory.

11. **Contract term**: 20 years, no renewal right — unusual for hotel franchises and a significant long-term risk given the capital investment required.

12. **Dispute resolution**: Arbitration in Charlotte, NC under North Carolina law with jury trial waiver. State addenda modify this for 11 states.

13. **Litigation**: Antitrust class action pending regarding IDeaS pricing algorithm. Prior guest-practices class action settled for $100K.

14. **Special risk**: Out-of-state dispute resolution (North Carolina) — identified by states requiring risk disclosure.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure
ESH Strategies Franchise LLC is the franchisor entity, a shell-like LLC that does not operate hotels and relies entirely on affiliated entities for all operational functions. The Manager (ESA Management LLC) provides overhead services ($6.6M in 2024) and system services ($3.8M in 2024) to the franchisor under a services agreement. Trademark rights are licensed from ESH Strategies Branding LLC at 5% of franchise royalties ($429K in 2024). This structure concentrates operational risk in entities outside the franchisee's contractual relationship.

The FDD offers two programs: (1) a single-unit franchise and (2) a multi-unit development program requiring at least 2 hotels. The Franchise Agreement term is 20 years for new-build; conversion terms are set by franchisor based on hotel age/condition/location. There is no right to renew or extend.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees**:
- Initial Franchise Fee: $50,000 (non-refundable; ranged $40K-$50K in prior year)
- Development Fee: $50,000 × number of hotels (min 2), credited against IFFs
- Pre-Opening PIP Fee: $2,000 (credited toward IFF)
- GM Certification: $1,900/person
- New Owner Orientation: $1,900/person
- Pre-Opening On-Site Training: $2,500 (waivable for experienced operators)
- Project Management Fee: $5,000 + third-party expenses
- Construction Site Visits: $6,000 (3 visits)
- IT Site Visits: $2,000/visit (up to 2)

**Ongoing Fees**:
- Royalty: 5.5% of GRR
- System Services Fee: 4.5% of GRR
- Digital Marketing Commission: 5% of GRR via paid digital channels
- Agoda Marketing: 0.6% of GRR on Agoda bookings

- PMS Hosting: $1.00/room/month

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
The franchisor estimates 80-90% of establishment purchases and 65-75% of operating purchases must be from approved or specified sources. Sole suppliers exist for PMS, RMS, CRS, credit card terminals/processing, and digital credit card authorization. The franchisor is the sole supplier for project management (PMS/reservation system installation) and IT infrastructure certification. For sole-supplier items, the franchisor does not intend to approve alternatives.

Alternative supplier process: submit for approval with 60-day response time. No fee except out-of-pocket expenses. However, for items where the franchisor is the sole supplier, alternatives will not be approved.

### Training Program
Four required initial training components totaling approximately 37-39 hours:
1. New Owner Orientation: 12 hours/2 days at Charlotte, NC ($1,900/person)
2. GM Certification: 12-14 hours/2 days, online or Charlotte ($1,900/person)
3. Pre-Opening On-Site: 12 hours/2 days at hotel ($2,500)
4. Service Culture: 1 hour online (included)

Training director: Diane Marques, VP of Training (25+ years hospitality experience, since April 2023). GM must be certified before opening (new) or within 60 days (conversion/transfer). New Owner Orientation within 6 months (new) or 3 months (conversion).

Remedial training ($4,000-$6,500) required if QA failure. Annual human trafficking awareness training required (franchisee's choice of program).

### Technology Stack
- Network infrastructure: $125K-$175K installation + $4-$7/room/month service
- Telecommunications: $11.5K-$16.5K installation + $300-$500/month service
- PMS: $3,500 hardware + $750 setup + $3.87/room/month ongoing
- RMS/CRS: $500 setup
- Credit card: $1K-$3K terminals + $200-$300 install + $25-$35/month + $0.03/transaction
- Digital credit card auth: $200-$250 implementation + $500/year
- PC: $1K-$1.5K + $0-$85/device/year maintenance
- $500/month non-compliance penalty for tech standards failure
- No contractual limit on upgrade frequency or cost

The franchisor has independent access to all franchisee databases, including financial performance and guest data.

### Marketing Fund Spending
System Services Fee (4.5% of GRR) is not a separate advertising fund but a general services fee. In 2024, $5,326,228 was spent on marketing (2.4% of franchisee GRR):
- 72% digital marketing and internet advertising
- 17% administrative fees
- 11% other marketing tools including SEO

SSF pools contributions across all ESA Network brands. No periodic accounting provided. No franchisee advertising council. Franchisor-owned hotels do not pay SSF. Franchisor may use SSF in any market and is not required to spend proportionally.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Territory Definition
The franchisor MAY grant a Territory at its discretion, based on the franchisee's financial and operational capability. Whether a territory is granted and its size are discretionary. If granted, during the term (and while franchisee is not in default), the franchisor will not open a new Brand Hotel in the Territory.

**Critical limitation**: Territory protection covers only the ESA Suites brand. It does NOT prevent the franchisor from opening ESA Premier Suites, ESA Select Suites, Yotel, or other related brand hotels in the Territory. Given the system's active rebranding strategy (converting Suites to Select), this is a material encroachment risk.

### Reserved Rights
The franchisor reserves the right to:
- Operate Brand Hotels outside Territory
- Operate Network Hotels (any brand) inside or outside Territory
- Own/acquire/construct Brand Hotels inside Territory (not open during term)
- Market and advertise inside Territory
- Acquire or merge with other hotel chains
- Continue operating existing Brand Hotels in Territory at FA effective date
- Begin operations in Territory 6 months before FA expiration
- Use all distribution channels inside Territory without compensation

### Channel Restrictions
Franchisees may not use internet or social media for marketing without consent. All digital/email marketing is conducted by the franchisor and funded through SSF; franchisees are currently prohibited from independent digital marketing.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- **Term**: 20 years for new-build; conversion term set by franchisor
- **Renewal**: No right to renew or extend — this is a significant risk given the capital investment and the 20-year depreciation cycle of hotel improvements
- **No "materially different" renewal risk**: Because there is no renewal, the franchisee simply loses the right to operate under the brand at term expiration

### Transfer Provisions
Transfer requires franchisor approval. Conditions include:
- Satisfy all monetary obligations
- Pay then-current PIP fee
- Transferee agrees to update hotel to then-current Standards
- Franchisee and owners sign general release
- Transferee submits new application and pays then-current IFF as transfer fee
- Transferee enters then-current form of FA (which may have materially different terms)
- Right of First Offer: only if Exhibit F is signed, for markets franchisor deems strategic

### Termination and Death/Disability
- **Franchisee termination**: Only upon fire/casualty exceeding coverage limits or state law grounds
- **Franchisor without cause**: Fire/casualty exceeding coverage (FA); HTA terminable on 10 days notice
- **Curable defaults**: 30 days (10 for monetary)
- **Non-curable defaults**: Bankruptcy, cease operations, contest marks, safety threats, felony conviction, false records, 3 defaults in 12 months, 2 similar defaults in 6 months — immediate termination
- **Death/disability**: Must transfer to third party (including heirs) meeting all transfer conditions within required timeframe

### Noncompete
- **During term**: None
- **Post-term**: None
This is unusually favorable and allows franchisees to operate competing hotels during and after the franchise term.

### Dispute Resolution
- **Method**: Arbitration (except for indemnification claims, equitable relief, and enforcement of arbitral awards)
- **Venue**: Charlotte, NC (franchisor HQ)
- **Governing law**: North Carolina
- **Jury trial waiver**: Yes
- State addenda modify venue/law for 11 states (see Section I below)

### Litigation History
- **Pending**: In re Extended Stay Hotel Antitrust Litigation — consolidated Sherman Act class action regarding IDeaS pricing algorithm use. Two original cases (Au v. IDeaS, July 2024; Gonzalez v. IDeaS, September 2024) allege coordinated pricing through revenue management software. Case transferred to N.D. California, motion to dismiss stayed.
- **Resolved**: Brittian v. ESA Management — guest practices class action settled for $100,000 in July 2024.

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

The franchisor provides a comprehensive FPR with 12 tables covering the 2024 calendar year, segmented into three hotel populations: Owned (337), Franchised (93), and Combined Brand Hotels (430).

### Operating Metrics

| Metric | Owned (337) Avg/Med | Franchised (93) Avg/Med | Gap |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|-----|
| Occupancy | 77.1% / 78.0% | 78.2% / 80.9% | +1.1% |
| ADR | $80.87 / $77.14 | $59.89 / $59.94 | -$20.98 |
| RevPAR | $62.35 / $60.68 | $46.85 / $45.98 | -$15.50 |
| EsOcc | 60.5% / 61.3% | 62.9% / 64.9% | +2.4% |

**Critical observation**: Franchised hotels achieve similar or slightly better occupancy but dramatically lower ADR ($21 less) and RevPAR ($15.50 less) than owned hotels. This suggests franchised hotels are in lower-rate markets, are lower quality, or lack the revenue management sophistication of company-managed properties. A prospective franchisee should not use owned-hotel metrics for pro forma projections.

### Reservation Channel Mix
- Franchised hotels rely more on Property Direct (33.2% vs 19.3%) — indicating less system-driven demand
- Owned hotels generate more through Call Center (31.4% vs 22.2%) — suggesting better integration with central reservation systems

### Length of Stay
30+ night stays represent 59.4% of consumed room nights system-wide, confirming the heavily extended-stay nature of the business. This creates revenue stability but also concentration risk — losing a 30+ night guest impacts revenue more than a transient guest.

### B2B Revenue
91.2% of B2B revenue comes from 7+ night stays, indicating strong corporate/project-based business.

- No EBITDA, net income, or profit margins
- No occupancy or RevPAR trends over time
- No per-hotel revenue distribution (only average/median/at-or-above)
- No breakdown by market type, hotel age, or renovation status

## I. State Addenda Summary

### States with Addenda
California, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, Virginia, Washington

### Material Overrides by State

**California**: Interest rate capped at 10%/year. Liquidated damages clauses may be unenforceable under Civil Code Section 1671. NC choice of law/venue may not apply. General release excludes California Franchise Investment Law and Franchise Relations Act claims. Anti-waiver protections.

**Hawaii**: Standard registration disclosures. Registered in CA, HI, IL, IN, MD, MI, MN, NY, ND, RI, SD, VA, WA, WI.

**Illinois**: Illinois law governs. Venue outside Illinois void (arbitration outside IL permitted). Anti-waiver protections. Franchisee termination/non-renewal rights per Illinois Franchise Disclosure Act.

**Indiana**: General release excludes Indiana Deceptive Franchise Practices Law claims. NC venue may not be enforceable. Indiana franchise laws govern. 10-day pre-sale disclosure requirement.

**Maryland**: General release excludes Maryland Franchise Registration and Disclosure Law claims. May sue in Maryland for MD law claims. 3-year statute of limitations for franchise claims. SSF accounting available upon written request.

**Minnesota**: 90 days notice of termination required (60 days to cure), except for immediate-effect defaults. 180 days non-renewal notice required. Release excludes Minnesota Franchise Act claims. Cannot require out-of-state litigation, jury waiver, or liquidated damages/termination penalties. NSF fee capped at $30. Franchisor must protect and indemnify for trademark use. Anti-waiver protections.

**New York**: Release subject to GBL Article 33 protections. Franchisee may terminate on any legal grounds. Franchisor assignment only to willing/financially able assignee. Choice of law subject to GBL Article 33.

**North Dakota**: Release excludes ND Franchise Investment Law claims. Liquidated damages and termination penalties held "unfair, unjust and inequitable." Mediation at mutually agreed site. May bring action in ND. No jury waiver. No punitive damages waiver. Anti-waiver protections.

**Rhode Island**: Venue/choice of law outside RI void for RI Franchise Investment Act claims.

**Virginia**: Reasonable cause required for franchise termination per Virginia Retail Franchising Act. Anti-waiver protections.

**Washington**: Washington Franchise Investment Protection Act prevails in conflicts. Arbitration/mediation must be in Washington or mutually agreed location. Release excludes WA franchise law rights. Noncompete void unless employee earns >$100K/year (or contractor >$250K/year). No-poach provisions void. Transfer fees limited to reasonable estimated/actual costs. Anti-waiver protections.
