## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System Size:** 2,502 total Panda Express restaurants at end of FY2024 — 173 franchised and 2,329 company-owned (operated by affiliate PEI). This is one of the largest Chinese/Asian QSR systems in the world.
2. **Entity Structure:** Citadel Panda Express, Inc. is a California corporation, wholly-owned by Panda Express, Inc. (PEI), which is wholly-owned by Panda Restaurant Group, Inc. (PRG). The Cherng family (Andrew and Peggy) co-founded the system in 1983 and remain Co-Chairmen and Co-CEOs. PRG owns all trademarks.
3. **Ownership History:** First Panda Express opened 1983 in Glendale, CA. Franchising began 1995; current License Agreement form since 2004. The system has grown steadily — company-owned outlets increased from 2,153 (2022) to 2,329 (2024), a net gain of 176 in three years.
4. **Investment Range:** $514,500 to $3,275,500 for a single captive-venue restaurant. A pilot drive-thru program adds up to $1,500,000. The wide range reflects the diversity of venue types — airport locations command significantly higher lease costs.
5. **Fee Burden:** 8% royalty on Gross Volume (or $4,000 minimum per four-week Royalty Period, whichever is greater). No advertising fund contribution required. However, a 10% surcharge on Proprietary Products adds a material invisible cost on food purchases. All fees are CPI-adjustable annually.
6. **Item 19 Highlights:** 156 captive-venue restaurants reported for FY2024. Total gross sales of $247.3 million; average $1,585,064; median $1,186,865. Airport locations are the standout performers averaging $3.9M. Only 42% of units exceed the system average, indicating significant right-tail skew. No cost, expense, or profit data is provided.
7. **Bottom-Quartile Risk:** The minimum gross sales reported is $246,547 (a university location). University locations — which represent the largest venue category at 72 units — have the lowest average at $889,636. A franchisee operating in a lower-performing university venue faces significant underperformance risk against the mandatory minimum royalties.
8. **Financial Health:** The franchisor entity is in strong financial health — $70M in total assets, $97M in retained earnings, $24.8M net income on $28.3M revenue. Expenses are minimal ($2.9M) because the entity is essentially a licensing vehicle. The auditor (CliftonLarsonAllen LLP) issued an unqualified opinion.
9. **Growth Trajectory:** Company-owned growth is accelerating — 87 openings in 2024 vs. 57 in 2022. Franchised growth is steady at 8–11 net additions per year. 123 company-owned openings projected for 2025 vs. only 14 franchised. The company-owned system is the dominant growth engine.
10. **Territory:** NO exclusive territory. No protected area. The franchisor and affiliates can open competing outlets anywhere, including adjacent to your restaurant. This is one of the most extreme no-territory positions in franchising.
11. **Renewal:** 10-year Operating Term with a single 5-year successor term. Renewal may require signing a "materially different" agreement — a significant risk factor. $10,000 successor fee plus potential remodel to current standards.
12. **Disputes:** Arbitration in Los Angeles County (California); jury waiver; class action waiver. Subject to state-specific overrides (Kansas requires in-state proceedings).
13. **Special Risks:** Mandatory minimum royalties regardless of sales; out-of-state dispute resolution; training completion cancellation at sole discretion with no refund; significant unopened franchise agreements.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Citadel Panda Express, Inc. was incorporated in California on October 25, 1990, specifically as the franchising vehicle for the Panda Express brand. It is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Panda Express, Inc. (PEI), which is itself wholly-owned by Panda Restaurant Group, Inc. (PRG). The principal address is 1683 Walnut Grove Avenue, Rosemead, California 91770. The entity also has a wholly-owned subsidiary, Citadel Aviation Investments, LLC, which holds airplane assets primarily used by PEI and PRG.

The franchise model is centered on "captive venues" — locations within airports, military facilities, universities, hospitals, casinos, and travel plazas. Licensees typically operate in spaces ranging from 500 to 1,500 square feet. A pilot program for free-standing drive-thru locations (approximately 2,600 sq ft) exists but is limited.

PEI operates 2,329 company-owned Panda Express restaurants as of December 28, 2024. PRG operates additional restaurants internationally in Canada, Japan, and Korea. PRG is also a minority owner in 59 joint venture licensed restaurants in captive venues within the United States.

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees:**
The standard initial license fee is $25,000, fully non-refundable, payable within 5 days of the franchisor's written authorization for a restaurant. In FY2024, actual initial fees ranged from $12,500 to $25,000, with the lower amounts reflecting negotiated terms for multi-restaurant licensees under existing agreements.

**Ongoing Fees — Royalties:**
The primary ongoing obligation is an 8% royalty on Gross Volume, calculated per four-week Royalty Period, due by the 15th of each month. However, if the percentage amount falls below $4,000, the franchisee must pay the $4,000 minimum instead. This minimum is CPI-adjustable annually. The minimum royalty does not apply during force majeure closures, but it is prorated for any period where the restaurant is closed for holidays or breaks approved by the venue owner (such as university summer breaks). In practice, a restaurant generating less than $200,000 per period ($2.6M annualized) would be paying above the 8% rate due to the minimum floor.

Royalties are calculated on "Gross Volume," which is defined very broadly to include all sales whether cash or credit, proceeds from business interruption insurance (less deductible), third-party delivery orders, mail/fax/phone orders, and deposits not refunded. The only deductions permitted are sales tax actually paid and customer refunds. Notably, credit card fees, delivery service charges, and employee discounts are not deducted.

**Ongoing Fees — Proprietary Product Surcharge:**
In addition to royalties, franchisees pay a 10% surcharge on purchases of Proprietary Products (food items made from franchisor recipes, branded items). In FY2024, this generated $2,378,044 in revenue for the franchisor (10.65% of domestic revenue). Since 85-90% of all purchases must be from designated suppliers, this surcharge represents a significant ongoing cost that does not appear in the "royalty" line.

**Operational Fees:**
- Food safety inspections: $177.24 quarterly (Ecosure) with 4% annual increases; $177.24 refused entry fee
- Customer satisfaction surveys: $20-22/store/month (SMG)
- Mystery shopper: $19.75-55 per service plus $11-12 meal reimbursement (Ipsos)
- Promotional materials: $300-1,000/year (mandatory purchase from marketing department)

**Event-Driven Fees:**
- Transfer fee: $10,000 per restaurant (max $30,000 per transaction) plus out-of-pocket costs and attorney's fees — due even if transfer is not completed
- Successor fee: $10,000 per restaurant (CPI-adjustable)
- Early termination fee: Anticipated royalties for remaining 8-month notice period
- Audit costs: Full cost of audit plus interest if understatement exceeds 3%
- Management fees: 7% of Gross Volume plus costs if franchisor takes over management due to uncured default
- Interest on late payments: 10% per annum or maximum legal rate
- Remedial training: $500/day plus all travel/lodging/food expenses

### Initial Investment

The estimated initial investment ranges from $514,500 to $3,275,500 for a single captive-venue restaurant, covering pre-opening costs and the first three months of operations. The largest cost drivers are leasehold improvements ($100,000-$1,500,000), FF&E ($120,000-$650,000), and insurance ($94,500-$157,500 annual premium).

Insurance requirements are notably demanding: $10 million commercial general liability, $3 million auto liability, $10 million cyber insurance (worldwide), property all-risk at replacement cost with business interruption coverage, and workers' compensation per state law. Self-insurance is only permitted with $150 million minimum net worth and annual audited financial statements.

The typical captive-venue Panda Express occupies 500 to 1,500 square feet, though some locations extend to approximately 3,000 square feet. Airport rent can be significantly higher than other venues, accounting for the high end of the lease estimate. Food court CAM charges typically run 35-50% of minimum rent, and percentage rent (typically 10%) applies above threshold sales volumes.

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
The franchisor estimates that 85-90% of all expenditures for products and services must be for goods meeting franchisor specifications, obtained from designated suppliers. Proprietary Products — food items made according to the franchisor's recipes and formulas — must be purchased exclusively from designated distributors. A 10% surcharge on Proprietary Products generates significant revenue for the franchisor.

Two required suppliers are partially owned by franchisor officers: Stampede Holdings, LLC (beef supplier) and I/O Controls Corporation (equipment — warmers, LED strips). The franchisor and affiliates retain all rebates, discounts, and allowances from supplier sales. In FY2024, PRG received a $612,286 soda beverage rebate but passed 100% through to the licensees that made the purchases.

Supplier approval takes approximately 8 weeks. The franchisor evaluates product safety, availability, quality, and brand equity. Approval can be conditioned or revoked. Franchisees must sign a consent form authorizing suppliers to release purchase information to the franchisor.

### Training Program
The training program is one of the most extensive in the QSR franchise space:

- **Head of Business/Operations:** 11 weeks total — 148 hours classroom + 290 hours on-the-job training
- **Store Manager:** 8 weeks plus pre-work online study — 24 hours classroom + 328 hours OJT
- **Assistant Manager/Head Chef/Cook:** 6 weeks plus pre-work — 32 hours classroom + 243 hours OJT
- **Kitchen Help:** 2 weeks — 8 hours classroom + 78 hours OJT
- **Shift Lead:** 2 weeks — 13 hours classroom + 67 hours OJT
- **Counter Help:** 1 week — 5 hours classroom + 35 hours OJT
- **Operations Training Leader:** 4 weeks — recommended when 5+ locations

All training is conducted at company-owned Panda Express restaurants, licensee-certified training units, or other designated locations. Trainers have minimum 15 months' field experience as Panda Store Managers. Failure to complete training or pass required testing can result in termination of the License Agreement without refund — one of the four special risks highlighted on the cover page.

Minimum staffing: 1 certified SM/AM on-site during all operating hours, at least 6 certified chefs/cooks (scaling +1 per $8,000 weekly sales above $7,000), and at least 3 certified service associates (same scaling). At least 6 head chefs/cooks must be re-certified annually.

### Technology Stack
- POS system with non-resettable grand total required
- Approved: Xenial/Xpient POS on NCR RealPOS CX7 or Posiflex XT8315
- Hardware cost: $14,000-$21,000; software subscription: $2,000-$4,000/year; maintenance: $500-$2,000/year
- iPad and temperature probe required
- PCI DSS compliance mandatory; stateful inspection or Next-Gen firewall required
- Wireless access point must be on separate firewall segment from POS
- Full-cloud POS subscription option available (higher recurring cost, lower initial cost)

### Marketing
There is no formal advertising program for the franchise system and no franchisee advertising council. Franchisees are not required to contribute to any marketing fund. Instead, they benefit from advertising and promotional programs implemented by PEI/PRG on behalf of all Panda Express restaurants. Franchisees must purchase quarterly promotional materials from the franchisor's marketing department ($300-$1,000/year) and display them as campaigns are introduced. All advertising must be either developed by the franchisor or approved in writing before use. Internet and social media use of the Marks requires prior written consent.

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory
There is no exclusive or protected territory of any kind. The franchisor is explicit: "You will not be awarded any 'protected' or 'reserved' territorial or other rights. You will not receive an exclusive territory." This is one of the most unrestricted territory positions in franchising.

The franchisor and its affiliates can locate other Panda Express restaurants (or any other brand) anywhere, using any distribution channel (Internet, catalog, telemarketing, direct marketing), and develop or become associated with other concepts, including dual branding. They can also acquire, merge with, or affiliate with businesses that compete directly with your restaurants — and require brand conversions at your expense.

### Channel Restrictions
Franchisees cannot market or sell through the Internet or any channel other than their physical restaurant. There are no restrictions on the location of customers the franchisee may solicit, but neither the franchisor nor other franchisees are restricted from soliciting customers anywhere.

### Encroachment Risk
Given that the franchise model operates in captive venues (airports, military bases, universities), the practical encroachment risk is somewhat mitigated by venue-level exclusivity arrangements with landlords. However, the franchisor has no contractual obligation to protect against encroachment, and the affiliates operate 2,329 company-owned locations that could theoretically be placed in any venue.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
The License Agreement has an unlimited overall term, subject to termination rights. However, each specific restaurant has a 10-year Operating Term from the date it opens to the public. If no restaurant location has been authorized within 2 years of the agreement's effective date, the agreement expires.

Upon expiration of a restaurant's Operating Term, the franchisee may be eligible for a single 5-year successor term — after which there is no further renewal. Successor conditions include: 6-12 months advance written notice, Good Standing, compliance with then-current new-unit standards (which can require a complete remodel), payment of $10,000 successor fee, execution of a general release, and — critically — the franchisee **may be required to sign a materially different agreement**. This materially-different-terms risk means the franchise relationship is effectively renegotiated at year 10.

### Termination Triggers
The termination provisions are extensive. The franchisor can terminate with cause for both curable and non-curable defaults. Curable defaults with a 15-day cure period include: confidentiality/trademark violations, inaccurate Gross Volume reporting, failure to pay, refusal of audit/inspection, health/safety violations, and failure to respond to guest complaints within 10 days of written notice. Other curable defaults receive 30 days (60 days if the default cannot reasonably be cured in 30).

Non-curable defaults triggering immediate termination include: material misrepresentation in the application, abandonment, bankruptcy/insolvency, criminal misconduct, unauthorized transfer, Gross Volume understatement exceeding 5%, cross-default under other agreements, failure to complete training, lease termination for cause, unauthorized catering/delivery, and — notably — 3 defaults within any 12-month period regardless of whether each was individually cured.

The California addendum modifies cure periods: 5-day cure for payment defaults, 60-day cure for other defaults, and a 75-day maximum when cure cannot be completed in 60 days.

### Transfer Provisions
All transfers require franchisor consent and payment of a $10,000 per-restaurant transfer fee (maximum $30,000 per transaction) plus out-of-pocket costs and attorney's fees. The fee is due even if the transfer is not completed. The franchisor has a 30-day Right of First Refusal and may substitute cash for other forms of payment in the proposed transaction.

The conditions for transfer approval are extensive: transferee must qualify and sign the then-current License Agreement, complete training, meet current restaurant standards (potentially requiring renovation), assume all obligations, clear all debts, sign a general release, and not be engaged in a Similar Business. The transferor remains liable for the business without an express release.

### Noncompete Restrictions
During the term, franchisees and their affiliates cannot offer, sell, or provide competing Asian-style food items (Chinese, Japanese, Thai, Korean) at the facility where the restaurant is located, nor authorize others to do so. Post-term, the noncompete obligations continue per Section 17.4 — though the practical scope is limited since the restriction is facility-specific and the license for that facility will have ended. Confidentiality obligations survive for 5 years post-term per Section 8.1(F).

### Dispute Resolution
All disputes go through mediation, then binding arbitration under AAA rules, at a neutral location in Los Angeles County (or wherever the franchisor's headquarters is located). The agreement includes a jury waiver and a class action waiver. California law governs, with Federal Arbitration Act preemption. Noncompete provisions are governed by the law of the state where the breach occurs. The Kansas addendum overrides venue to require in-state proceedings.

### Litigation History
The franchisor has 4 pending cases and 6 concluded cases. The most significant pending matter is a conditional global settlement of data breach class action lawsuits arising from a March 2024 data security incident. Concluded cases include multiple wage/hour class actions (settled for $2.86M, $4.1M, and $2.75M) and a delivery fee class action ($900K + $500K vouchers). The NovaDine arbitration over online ordering vendor resulted in a net win for PRG ($5.3M award minus $1.7M counterclaim).

## F. Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations

### FPR Data
The FDD provides unaudited gross sales data for FY2024 (December 31, 2023 to December 28, 2024) for 156 captive-venue licensed Panda Express restaurants operating at least 52 weeks. Of these, 59 are joint ventures with a PRG affiliate as minority owner. The data excludes 15 restaurants open less than one year, 7 that closed in 2024, and 2 sole-venue-type restaurants (theme park and corporate campus) excluded for privacy.

Total gross sales across all 156 venues: $247,270,032. System average gross sales: $1,585,064. Median: $1,186,865. The 30% gap between average and median indicates significant upward skew from high-performing airport locations.

**By venue type:**
- **Airport** (22 units): Average $3,903,433 — the highest-performing venue type by far. Median $3,580,050. Range: $767K to $9.2M. Only 36% exceed the average, suggesting a few very high-volume airports pull the average up.
- **Military** (51 units): Average $1,489,026. Median $1,370,147. The most balanced venue type — 45% exceed the average.
- **University** (72 units): Average $889,636. Median $824,825. The largest category but lowest average. Subject to seasonal closures (summer breaks, etc.).
- **Hospital** (3 units): Average $608,731. Too small a sample for meaningful conclusions.
- **Casino** (4 units): Average $1,884,571. Small sample.
- **Travel Plaza** (4 units): Average $3,008,974. Small sample.

42% of all units exceeded the system average. The average age of reported units is 6.91 years.

### Critical Caveats
The FPR provides gross sales only — no cost, expense, or profit data whatsoever. A prospective franchisee has no basis from this FDD alone to estimate EBITDA or owner cash flow. The data covers only captive-venue locations in 38 states. Restaurants in geographic areas with stronger Panda Express brand recognition tend to outperform. University locations are subject to seasonal closures that affect comparability.

## G. Item 20 — Outlet Data

### System Trajectory
The overall Panda Express system has grown steadily across the three reporting years (2022-2024). Total outlets increased from 2,299 to 2,502, a net gain of 203 units (8.8%). Company-owned growth dominates — 81 net additions in 2024 alone (87 opened, 6 closed). Franchised growth is positive but modest at 8 net additions in 2024.

### Franchised Activity
Over the three-year period, the franchised system opened 35 restaurants (15 in 2022, 9 in 2023, 11 in 2024). There were zero terminations in 2022-2023 and only 2 in 2024 (both in Ohio). Non-renewals have been zero across all three years, suggesting a system that retains its franchisees effectively. One restaurant was reacquired by the franchisor in 2024 (Georgia — transitioning to company-owned). Total ceased operations were minimal: 4 in 2022, 1 in 2023, and 0 in 2024.

### Transfer Activity
Transfers have been modest: 5 per year in 2022-2023, declining to 3 in 2024. States with transfer activity include Arkansas, Florida, Idaho, Illinois, Maryland, Minnesota, and Texas.

### Projected Openings (2025)
15 franchise agreements are signed but outlets not yet opened as of December 28, 2024. The system projects 14 new franchised outlets and 123 new company-owned outlets in 2025. The 9:1 ratio of projected company-owned to franchised openings underscores that the franchisor's growth strategy is heavily weighted toward company-owned expansion.

### Gag Clause
The FDD explicitly discloses: "In some instances, current and former franchisees sign provisions restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience with the Panda Express franchise system." This is a material gag clause disclosure that prospective franchisees should consider when conducting due diligence through conversations with existing operators.

## H. Item 21 — Financial Statements

### Auditor and Opinion
The financial statements were audited by CliftonLarsonAllen LLP (Pasadena, California). The audit opinion, dated April 7, 2025, is unqualified (clean opinion). The statements are prepared in accordance with U.S. GAAP. The entity is a 52/53-week fiscal year operation.

### Balance Sheet (December 28, 2024)
Total assets were $69,999,787, down from $80,274,033 in the prior year. The decline is primarily due to the reclassification of $35.3 million "Due from Affiliate" from a current asset to a reduction of shareholder's equity.

Current assets total $8.5 million, including $3.7M cash, $4.8M accounts receivable, and $11K prepaid taxes. The dominant asset is Property and Equipment (net) at $61.5M, of which $61.2M (gross) represents airplanes. These airplanes are primarily used by PEI and PRG, not in franchise operations. The entity also holds $5.9M in land and $4.4M in buildings and improvements.

Total liabilities are remarkably low at $4.2M, consisting almost entirely of deferred revenue (initial franchise fees recognized ratably over the franchise term). There is no debt on the balance sheet. Shareholder's equity is $65.8M, comprising $160K common stock (16,000 shares), $4.2M additional paid-in capital, $96.7M retained earnings, offset by -$35.3M Due from Affiliate.

### Income Statement (FY2024)
Total net revenues were $28,262,859, a 9.9% increase from $25,718,800 in FY2023. Revenue is dominated by royalty income at $24.9M (88.2% of total), followed by license fees (proprietary product surcharges) at $2.4M, initial fees at $817K, and rental income at $146K.

Total expenses were remarkably low at $2,945,950 — reflecting the asset-light licensing model. Depreciation ($2.2M) is the largest expense, followed by property tax ($231K), professional services ($339K), salaries ($143K), and miscellaneous ($65K). Net income was $24,842,691, yielding an extraordinary 87.9% net margin. As an S Corporation, there is no federal income tax at the entity level — only foreign withholding taxes of $474K.

### Cash Flow (FY2024)
Operating cash flow was $2.7M, heavily impacted by a $23.3M increase in "Due from Affiliate" (advances to PRG). Without this intercompany movement, operating cash would have been approximately $26M. Investing activity was minimal ($26K in property purchases).

### Key Financial Observations
The entity functions as a licensing vehicle with minimal operating costs. The 87.9% net margin demonstrates the profitability of the franchisor's revenue streams (royalties + proprietary product surcharges). The $61.2M airplane balance is unusual for a franchise entity and raises questions about asset allocation and intercompany relationships. The $35.3M Due from Affiliate (non-interest-bearing advances to PRG) was reclassified from a current asset to an equity reduction in FY2024, suggesting a shift in how the company views the recoverability or nature of this balance. Revenue concentration is notable — 25% of revenue from 2 customers, and 54% of AR from 4 customers — reflecting the captive-venue model where large multi-unit operators dominate the franchisee base.

## I. State Addenda Summary

The FDD includes state addenda for 14 states: California, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Kansas, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, and Wisconsin.

**California:** Most extensive modifications. Cure periods adjusted (5-day for payments, 60-day for other defaults, 75-day maximum). Market withdrawal rights for the franchisor (no liability for termination if completely withdrawing from state/area, but cannot prevent franchisee from retaining control of business). Post-termination repurchase of items at price paid minus depreciation. CFRA fair market value appraisal process if termination found unlawful. General release requirements limited by Corp Code Section 31512.

**Kansas:** All dispute resolution must be conducted in Kansas under Kansas law. Indemnification provisions cannot cover the promisee's own negligence or intentional acts.

**Maryland:** Section 22 (Acknowledgments) entirely rewritten. Franchise exemption criteria detailed for fractional, large franchisee, insider, and large investment exemptions.

**Michigan:** Front-matter disclosure (pp.5-6) covers Michigan Franchise Investment Law protections — good cause required for termination, fair compensation on non-renewal, no out-of-state arbitration, no unreasonable transfer restrictions.

**New York:** Additional risk factors required on cover page highlighting out-of-state arbitration risk. Franchisor cannot use negotiation to impose less favorable terms than those in the FDD.

**Virginia:** Cannot cancel franchise without "reasonable cause" per Section 13.1-564 of the Virginia Retail Franchising Act.

All registration states include the NASAA required statement: No waiver of claims under applicable state franchise law, and no disclaiming reliance on franchisor statements.

## J. Unresolveds

1. **Death/disability provisions (MEDIUM):** The License Agreement does not contain specific death or disability provisions for the franchisee. This is unusual for a franchise system. The agreement binds "executors, administrators, heirs, assigns, and successors in interest," suggesting the franchise interest passes through the estate, but there is no specific succession mechanism, buy-back obligation, or timeline for estate transfer.

2. **Post-term noncompete scope (LOW — resolved via retry):** The noncompete is facility-specific (no competing Asian food at the same venue) and continues post-term per Section 17.4A. Practical enforceability is limited since the license for that facility will have ended. The confidentiality obligation survives 5 years per Section 8.1(F).

3. **Airplane assets on balance sheet (LOW):** $61.2M in airplanes primarily used by PEI/PRG sitting on the franchise entity's balance sheet. Depreciation expense ($2.2M/year) reduces reported net income. This appears to be an intercompany arrangement rather than a franchise-operations asset.

4. **Due from Affiliate trajectory (MEDIUM):** $35.3M owed by PRG to the franchise entity, up from $12M in 2023. Now reclassified as an equity reduction rather than a current asset. This is a significant non-interest-bearing intercompany balance that represents capital flowing from the franchise entity to the parent group.

## K. Contradictions

   - Item 20 reports 173 domestic franchised outlets at end of FY2024
