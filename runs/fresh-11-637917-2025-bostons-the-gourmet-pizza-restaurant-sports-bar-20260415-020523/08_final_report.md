## Filing ID: 637917-2025 | Issuance Date: March 25, 2025
## Franchisor: Boston Pizza Restaurants, LP

## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **Brand concept**: Full-service casual dining restaurant + sports bar concept featuring gourmet pizza, pasta, entrees, and alcohol service in 5,000–7,000 sq ft locations. Canadian-origin brand (Boston Pizza) operating in the US under "Boston's The Gourmet Pizza Restaurant & Sports Bar" mark.

2. **System size**: 24 franchised restaurants across 14 US states, 0 company-owned (as of December 31, 2024). The US system is a fraction of the broader brand: 371 franchised units in Canada (BPCLP) and 23 in Mexico. The sole US company-owned unit (Florida) was closed in 2024.

3. **Entity structure**: Delaware limited partnership formed December 21, 2001. Owned by Treliving Private Investments Ltd. (British Columbia) and BPR GP, Inc. (Delaware). Ultimate control: Treliving Family Holdings, Inc. / James Treliving. Intellectual property owned by TPI and licensed exclusively to BPR.

4. **Ownership history**: BPR USA offered US franchises from October 1995; current LP entity took over December 2001. Canadian operation has been franchising since 1983.

5. **Investment range**: $1,030,500 – $3,306,375 for a single unit (excluding real property). Wide range driven primarily by construction costs ($165K–$1.375M) and FF&E ($385K–$963K).

6. **Fee burden**: 9% of Gross Sales ongoing (5% royalty + 3% brand fund + 1% local advertising), plus $50,000 initial franchise fee, $15,000 on-site opening assistance, $20,000 pre-opening marketing = $85,000 to franchisor at opening.

7. **Item 19 highlights**: Top-third average gross sales $3,430,111 (median $3,564,265); middle-third average $2,163,950; bottom-third average $1,312,549. Bottom restaurant at $975,231. No cost or profit data disclosed. 4.1× spread between top and bottom performers.

8. **Bottom-quartile risk**: Bottom 5 restaurants averaged $1,312,549 in gross sales. At 9% total fee burden, that's ~$118K in fees on ~$1.3M revenue. With a full-service restaurant cost structure (food ~30%, labor ~30%, occupancy ~10%, other ~15%), a bottom-quartile unit would likely operate at or below breakeven. The $800,184 lowest-grossing 12-month restaurant is almost certainly cash-negative.

9. **Financial health — CRITICAL**: Going concern opinion from Moss Adams (February 28, 2025). Partners' capital deficit of ($458,010). Net operating loss of ($164,028) in FY 2024. Partners extracted $800,000 in distributions despite the loss. Cash reserves declined from ~$1.08M to ~$485K (unrestricted + restricted). Five states (IL, MD, ND, SD, WA) imposed fee deferral requirements due to financial condition.

10. **Growth trajectory**: Net +2 units in 2024 (5 opened, 2 terminated), rebounding from net -4 in 2023. 4 franchise agreements signed but not yet opened. 3 projected new franchised outlets next fiscal year. Modest growth but fragile given financial condition.

11. **Territory**: 1-mile radius protected territory, but explicitly not exclusive. Franchisor retains alternate channel rights, delivery cross-territory rights, and ability to acquire competitors within territory.

12. **Renewal risk**: 10-year initial term + 2 × 5-year successors. $25,000 renewal fee. Successor agreement "may have materially different terms and conditions (including, e.g., higher royalty and advertising contributions)."

13. **Dispute resolution**: Mediation/arbitration in Dallas, Texas; Texas law governs. Overridden by multiple state addenda.

14. **Special risks flagged**: Out-of-state dispute resolution, spousal liability guarantee, and franchisor financial condition concern — all three called out on the cover page.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Boston Pizza Restaurants, LP is a Delaware limited partnership controlled by the Treliving family through a chain of Canadian holding companies. The intellectual property (trademarks, system) is owned by TPI in British Columbia and licensed to the US entity. This creates a dependency: the US operation is a licensee of a Canadian IP holder.

The franchisor has been operating since 2001 (predecessor since 1995). It is a relatively small US system (24 units) backed by a large Canadian system (371 units). The US entity is asset-light: only $49,490 in net property and equipment.

### Fee Stack

**Initial fees (to franchisor):**
- Initial Franchise Fee: $50,000
- On-Site Opening Assistance Fee: $15,000
- Pre-Opening Marketing Assistance: $20,000
- **Total to franchisor at opening: $85,000**

**Ongoing fees:**
- Royalty: 5% of weekly Gross Sales
- Brand Fund Contribution: 3% of weekly Gross Sales
- Local Advertising: 1% of monthly Gross Sales (minimum)
- **Total ongoing: 9% of Gross Sales**

**Event-driven fees:**
- Renewal: $25,000
- Transfer: $25,000 ($1,000 non-refundable deposit)
- Management Fee: $500/day + costs (if franchisor must manage)
- Liquidated damages: Formula-based, minimum $30,000
- Technology: $1,500/year
- Conference: $1,500/year
- Late payment: $100 + 18%/year
- Audit: Actual cost if Gross Sales understated >3%
- De-identification: Actual costs on termination
- Broker fees: Actual costs on transfer
- Professional fees/indemnification: Variable

All fees non-refundable and uniformly imposed. Fixed fees subject to CPI adjustment (>5% threshold). Payment via ACH/EFT required.

**Fee deferral**: Illinois, Maryland, North Dakota, South Dakota, and Washington have imposed financial assurance requirements deferring initial fees until franchisor meets pre-opening obligations and franchisee opens for business. This is a direct consequence of the franchisor's financial condition.

### Initial Investment (Single Unit)

Total: $1,030,500 – $3,306,375 (excluding real property)

**Largest components:**
- Construction/leasehold improvements: $165,000 – $1,375,000 (widest range; retrofit vs. shell build-out)
- FF&E: $385,000 – $963,000
- Audio/visual systems: $75,000 – $235,000 (sports bar concept requires significant AV)
- Signage/graphics/artwork: $52,000 – $131,000
- POS/computer: $35,000 – $52,500

No financing offered by franchisor. Restaurant size: 5,000–7,000 sq ft. Based on open shop bidding (no union premium).

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions
- ~85% of opening purchases from designated/approved suppliers
- 50–60% of ongoing purchases from designated/approved suppliers
- Proprietary food products must come from franchisor or licensed third parties
- Designated architect and project management firm required
- Designated POS vendor (Posi-Touch) required
- Designated kitchen equipment vendor required

### Supplier Rebates
The franchisor received $979,786 in supplier rebates in FY 2024, representing 26.3% of total franchisor revenue ($3,721,290). Rebates range from 2–5% of systemwide purchase costs. The franchisor has no obligation to pass these through to franchisees.

This is a significant revenue concentration risk. If supplier relationships change or are renegotiated, the franchisor's already-fragile financial position would deteriorate further.

### Training Program
- 3 weeks in Dallas, Texas
- 50 hours classroom + 100 hours on-the-job = 150 total hours
- Week 1: Culture, guest service, hiring, product knowledge
- Week 2: Management, floor supervision, cost controls, bar operations
- Week 3: One-on-one with support center, kitchen/FOH operations
- No charge for first 2 restaurants (up to 7 people)
- Additional training: $1,000/trainer/5-day period + $350/day per diem
- Successor/remedial training at current rates

Jeffrey Melnick oversees the training program (currently on leave; Sergio Carvallo overseeing during leave). All training personnel have at least 1 year of experience in subjects taught.

### Technology Stack
- POS: Posi-Touch system (designated vendor)
- Computer system: $35,000–$52,500 initial; ~$4,500/year maintenance
- Technology fee: $1,500/year (may increase with vendor price changes)
- Required high-speed internet
- Required acceptance of all franchisor-designated credit/debit cards and gift cards
- Franchisor has independent remote access to all electronic data

Additional required technology solutions (at franchisee's expense): phone systems, security systems, scheduling software, employee shift/task management, music subscription, inventory solutions, digital ordering. Franchisor reserves right to add new systems.

### Marketing Fund
- Brand Fund Contribution: 3% of Gross Sales (not audited)
- FY 2024 spending: 25% production, 50% media, 25% administration
- Annual accounting available on written request
- Franchisor has complete discretion on use
- No guarantee of benefit to individual franchisee
- May be used for R&D, technology, quality control, training tools, staff salaries
- No advertising cooperatives currently established

### Local Advertising
- 1% of Gross Sales per month minimum
- Rolling 3-month compliance measurement
- Monthly report with receipts due by 10th of following month
- Shortfall paid to Brand Fund
- All advertising must be pre-approved
- $500 fee per occurrence for unauthorized advertising

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory
The franchise territory is typically a 1-mile radius around the approved location. The territory does not depend on sales volume or other contingency. However:

**The territory is NOT exclusive.** The FDD explicitly states: "You will not receive an exclusive territory. You may face competition from other franchisees, from outlets that we own, or from other channels of distribution or competitive brands that we control."

### Franchisor Rights Within Territory
The franchisor retains the right to:
- Use Marks to sell products/services through alternate channels (wholesale, internet, non-traditional locations) within the territory
- Allow delivery and catering services from other Boston's locations into the territory without compensating the franchisee
- Establish non-exclusive delivery systems (call-ahead, internet ordering, mobile app)
- Acquire competitive businesses within the territory
- Implement multi-area marketing programs that solicit customers anywhere
- Use different trademarks to sell similar products within the territory

### Channel Restrictions on Franchisee
- No wholesale, internet, or mail order sales
- No independent internet marketing or e-commerce without written permission
- Must participate in franchisor-mandated delivery platforms
- May not contract with unapproved delivery platforms
- Must honor systemwide promotions and gift cards

### Territory on Renewal
"If you renew your Franchise, your Franchise Territory may be modified depending on the then-current demographics of the Franchise Territory, and on our then-current standards for territories."

### Development Territory (Area Dev)
Non-exclusive during compliance. Size varies by demographics. Exclusivity lost on first failure to meet development schedule. Second failure is material default (may terminate, reduce territory, or extend schedule).

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal
- **Initial term**: 10 years
- **Successor terms**: 2 × 5 years (max potential 20 years)
- **Renewal fee**: $25,000
- **Materially different terms risk**: "You must sign our then-current Franchise Agreement... which may have materially different terms and conditions (including, e.g., higher royalty and advertising contributions) from the Franchise Agreement that covered your original term."
- Must be in good standing; must sign general release (subject to state law)

### Transfer
- All transfers require franchisor approval
- Transfer fee: $25,000 ($1,000 non-refundable deposit)
- Right of first refusal: 30 days to match offer
- Option to purchase at fair market value on termination
- Transfer to controlled entity: actual costs only

### Death/Disability
- Must transfer within 180 days
- Estate must apply within 120 days
- Franchisor may terminate if not transferred in time

### Termination and Defaults
- **By franchisee**: Only if franchisor is in material breach and fails to cure within 30 days
- **By franchisor without cause**: Not applicable
- **By franchisor with cause**: Numerous triggers including cross-default on any other agreement
- **Curable defaults**: 30-day cure period (Section 24.3)
- **Non-curable defaults**: Sections 24.1 and 24.2 (not enumerated in FDD body; in franchise agreement)

### Noncompete
- **During term**: No competing business, no >5% interest in competitor, no services for competitor — anywhere (global scope during term)
- **Post-term**: 2 years; 10-mile radius from franchisee's location AND all other Boston's locations operating or under construction at time of termination
- **Fallback**: If court finds primary scope too broad, reduces to 10-mile from franchisee's location only
- **Tolling**: Restricted period extended by duration of any violation
- Subject to state-specific limitations (see State Addenda)

### Dispute Resolution
- All disputes must be mediated, then arbitrated, in Dallas, Texas
- Litigation (if applicable) in Dallas, Texas
- Texas law governs
- Jury trial waiver likely in franchise agreement
- Subject to applicable state law

### Liquidated Damages
Formula: Average monthly (Royalty + Brand Fund Contributions) × lesser of (36 or remaining months in term). Minimum $30,000.

At system-average gross sales (~$2.3M), monthly royalty+brand fund = ~$15,300. For 36 months: ~$551K. This is a substantial penalty.

### Litigation History
Three disclosed actions, all resolved:
1. **Patel v. BPR** (2019): Franchisee alleged breach of oral agreements, fraud, DTPA violations ($200K–$1M sought). Settled for $5,000 to franchisee. Notably low settlement amount.
2. **BPR v. Kaushal** (2020): Franchisor sought $205,000 liquidated damages. Dismissed without prejudice.
3. **BPR v. BOSTCG/Patel** (2023): Franchisor sought $271,947 liquidated damages. Defendants counterclaimed breach and "string-along fraud." Settled for $85,000 to franchisor; fraud counterclaims abandoned.

Pattern: Franchisor pursues liquidated damages aggressively against departing franchisees. Two of three cases involve liquidated damage claims.

## I. State Addenda Summary

State addenda exist for 16 states. Material overrides are summarized below.

### States with Addenda
California, Hawaii, Illinois, Iowa, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Ohio, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin

### Key Overrides by Category

**Forum Selection Voided or Limited:**
- Illinois: litigation must be in IL federal or state courts
- Michigan: arbitration/litigation outside MI is void
- Minnesota: litigation cannot be required outside MN
- North Dakota: dispute resolution outside ND may not be enforceable
- Rhode Island: venue/jurisdiction outside RI is void for claims under RI Act
- Washington: arbitration/mediation must be in WA or mutually agreed location

**Choice of Law Overridden:**
- Illinois: IL law applies to IL Franchise Disclosure Act claims
- Indiana: IN law supersedes conflicting provisions
- Minnesota: MN law prevails over conflicting provisions
- North Dakota: choice of law may not be enforceable under ND law
- Washington: WA Franchise Investment Protection Act prevails
- Wisconsin: WI Fair Dealership Law supersedes conflicting provisions

**Post-term Noncompete Limited:**
- California: covenant may not be enforceable under CA law
- Indiana: modified to comply with IC 23-2-2.7-1(9); limited to franchise territory for 2 years
- Minnesota: liquidated damages/termination penalties may not be enforceable (MN Rule 2860.4400J)
- North Dakota: covenants contrary to ND Century Code §9-08-06 may be unenforceable
- Washington: noncompete void unless employee earns >$100K/year (adjusted for inflation); void for independent contractors <$250K; no-hire provisions void

**General Release Restrictions:**
- California: release may not waive rights under CA Franchise Investment Law
- Indiana: prospective general release prohibited under IN Deceptive Franchise Practices Act
- Maryland: release may not apply to MD Franchise Registration and Disclosure Law
- Minnesota: general release prohibited (MN Rule 2860.4400D)
- New York: rights under Article 33 of NY General Business Law preserved
- North Dakota: release may not cover ND Franchise Investment Law claims
- Washington: release void unless negotiated settlement with independent counsel

**Fee Deferral (Financial Condition):**
- Illinois: initial fees deferred until pre-opening obligations met and franchisee opens (imposed by IL AG due to financial condition)
- Maryland: all initial fees deferred until pre-opening obligations met and outlet opens (imposed by MD Securities Commissioner due to financial condition)
- North Dakota: initial franchise fee deferred until pre-opening obligations met and franchisee opens
- South Dakota: initial fees and all pre-opening payments deferred until pre-opening obligations met and franchisee opens
- Washington: initial fees deferred until pre-opening obligations met and restaurant opens; fees released proportionally per restaurant

**Termination Protections:**
- Illinois: termination/non-renewal provisions may not be enforceable under Sections 19/20 of IL Act
- Indiana: unilateral termination without good cause or in bad faith is unlawful (IC 23-2-2.7-1(7))
- Michigan: termination only for good cause; 30-day cure opportunity
- Minnesota: 90 days' notice of termination (60 to cure); 180 days' notice of non-renewal
- Virginia: cancellation without reasonable cause is unlawful (Section 13.1-564)
- Washington: RCW 19.100.180 may supersede termination provisions

**Liquidated Damages:**
- California: certain liquidated damages clauses unenforceable under CC Section 1671
- Minnesota: liquidated damages/termination penalties may not be enforceable
- North Dakota: consent to liquidated damages may not be enforceable
- Washington: liquidated damages amended to exclude Brand Fund Contributions from calculation for WA franchisees

**Other Notable Overrides:**
- Indiana: rebates from suppliers must be properly accounted for and submitted to franchisee (IC 23-2-2.7-1(4))
- Washington: transfer fees limited to franchisor's reasonable estimated/actual costs; indemnification limited (no obligation for losses caused solely by indemnified party's negligence/willful misconduct)
