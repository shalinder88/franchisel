## A. Executive Snapshot

1. **System size:** 59 total restaurants at year-end 2024 — 30 franchised, 29 company-owned. The system has grown from 29 units in early 2022, but growth has decelerated sharply: net +16 (2022), +9 (2023), +5 (2024).

2. **Entity structure:** Cupbop Franchise, LLC is a Utah LLC formed in November 2014, wholly owned by Cupbop Co. (a Utah corporation) since January 2021. Two additional parent entities — Gold Light Holdings, LLC (UT, managed by Kevin Santiago) and MAK Holdings LLC (NV). The franchisor does NOT operate restaurants; it exists solely to offer and manage franchise rights. Cupbop Co. is the operating entity that runs company-owned locations and supplies proprietary products to franchisees.

3. **Ownership history:** Founded by Junghun Song as food trucks in May 2013 under Cupbop Co. First storefront opened May 2016. Franchise entity originally incorporated as Cupbop Franchising, Inc. (UT corporation, Nov 2014), converted to LLC in December 2016. Became wholly owned subsidiary of Cupbop Co. via corporate restructuring in January 2021. Gold Light Holdings and MAK Holdings became parents in January 2021.

4. **Investment range:** $296,400 to $664,400 for a single restaurant. The dominant cost driver is leasehold improvements and FF&E ($180,000-$475,000), reflecting wide variability in build-out costs. Initial franchise fee is $40,000 ($30,000 for additional units).

5. **Fee burden:** Ongoing fees total at least 8.5% of Gross Sales in practice (6% royalty + 2% ad fund + 0.5% local advertising), with a theoretical maximum of 13.5% (6% royalty + 4.5% ad fund + 1% local + 2% cooperative). The total cumulative advertising burden is capped at 6.5% of Gross Sales.

6. **Item 19 highlights:** Revenue-only FPR disclosed. Franchise restaurant average Gross Revenue was $658,208 (median $669,260) across 24 qualifying units. Company-owned averaged $794,003 (median $731,325) across 26 units — a 17% premium over franchise units. Bottom-quartile franchise units averaged just $401K, which raises unit-level viability concerns given the fee burden.

7. **Bottom-quartile risk:** At the bottom-quartile franchise average of $400,962, royalty alone would consume approximately $24K. Add advertising (2%) of $8K, local advertising (0.5%) of $2K, and the franchisee has roughly $367K left before any operating expenses. With no cost data disclosed, the profitability of bottom-quartile units is unknown but concerning.

8. **Financial health of franchisor:** The franchise entity generated $2.03M in revenue and $1.03M in net income in 2024. However, distributions of $1.9M dramatically exceeded net income, leaving member's equity at just $21,352 — a razor-thin capitalization for an entity with 30 franchise obligations.

9. **Growth trajectory:** Growth decelerated from +16 (2022) to +9 (2023) to +5 (2024). The system projects 5 new franchise and 9 new company-owned openings in the next fiscal year, meaning the franchisor is expanding its own footprint more aggressively than the franchise side.

10. **Territory:** Protected territory granted but no minimum size guaranteed. Significant encroachment carve-outs for Non-Traditional Venues (stadiums, airports, hospitals, universities, etc.) and online delivery orders inside the protected territory. Minimum sales performance requirement of 50% of national trailing average starts in Year 2.

11. **Renewal risk:** One 10-year successor term available, but the renewal agreement may be "materially different" from the current agreement. Renewal fee is $10,000.

12. **Disputes and litigation:** No litigation or bankruptcy disclosed. Dispute resolution is in Salt Lake County, Utah via mediation then arbitration, subject to state-specific addenda overrides.

13. **Special risks (state-required disclosure):** (1) Out-of-state dispute resolution in Utah, (2) mandatory minimum sales performance levels, (3) supplier control — must purchase nearly all inventory from franchisor/affiliates at their set prices.

## B. Fee Stack, Entry Structure, Initial Investment

### Front Matter and Entity Structure

Cupbop Franchise, LLC exists solely as a franchise-granting entity. It does not operate restaurants. The intellectual property (trademarks, system, recipes) is owned by Cupbop Co. and licensed to the franchise entity via a License Agreement dated December 15, 2020. This creates a dependency: the franchise entity's ability to sublicense to franchisees depends on the continued viability of this license agreement with its parent.

The Korean BBQ concept originated with food trucks in Salt Lake City (2013), expanded to storefronts (2016), and began franchising in March 2017. The system is geographically concentrated, with company-owned operations primarily in Utah (26 of 29) and franchise operations spread across Arizona (10), Idaho (9), Utah (4), Oklahoma (3), Nevada (2), and Texas (2).

### Fee Stack

**Initial Fees:**
- Standard initial franchise fee: $40,000 (lump sum, non-refundable)
- Second/additional unit: $30,000
- Area Development: $40,000 first + $30,000 each additional (5-20 units typical)

**Ongoing Fees (as percentage of Gross Sales):**
- Royalty: 6% (monthly, auto-debited)
- Advertising Fund: currently 2% (can increase to 4.5%)
- Local Advertising: minimum 0.5% (can increase to 1%)
- Cooperative Advertising: up to 2% (not currently assessed)
- Cap: total advertising-related fees cannot exceed 6.5%

**Event-Driven Fees:**
- Transfer fee: $10,000
- Successor franchise fee: $10,000
- Relocation fee: $10,000
- Non-compliance: $500 per occurrence + $500/day continuing
- Unauthorized advertising: $1,000 per occurrence
- Late fee: 10% of past-due amount
- Interest: lesser of 18% annual or legal max
- Liquidated damages on termination: sum of prior 24 months' royalties
- Management fee (death/disability/default): $500/person/day + expenses
- Audit costs: if understatement >3% or failure to provide records

**Notable fee mechanics:**
- Auto-debit required for all fees before opening
- If you fail to report Gross Sales, franchisor can debit 120% of last payment + late fee + interest
- Tax reimbursement with gross-up provision — you reimburse the franchisor for any state taxes on your payments, including an amount to make them whole after-tax
- Gross Sales definition is broad: includes all revenue, catering charges, gift cards, business interruption insurance proceeds

### Initial Investment

The total estimated initial investment for a single Cupbop restaurant ranges from $296,400 to $664,400. The largest variable is leasehold improvements and FF&E ($180,000-$475,000), which depends heavily on the condition of the chosen space (previously a restaurant vs. raw shell) and landlord contributions. The franchise fee ($40,000) and grand opening advertising ($10,000) are fixed. The working capital estimate ($27,000-$63,000) covers three months of operations including payroll, rent, utilities, and inventory.

Key notes:
- Space requirement: 1,200-2,000 square feet
- Insurance estimate ($400-$600) in the table appears to reflect only initial premium deposits, not the full annual cost of the required $2M general liability + business interruption + workers' comp coverage
- Professional fees ($2,000-$5,000) cover attorney review of franchise documents and entity formation

## C. Supplier Control, Operating Control, Technology Burden, Reporting Burden

### Supplier Restrictions

The supplier control framework is one of the three state-required special risk disclosures. Franchisees must purchase:
- **From Cupbop Co. only:** Proprietary sauces, marinades, proteins, branded paper products (bowls, cups)
- **From franchisor, affiliates, or approved suppliers:** Other food and non-food products branded/packaged exclusively for Cupbop
- **Per specifications:** Soft goods, small wares, utensils, cleaning supplies — from any supplier meeting franchisor specifications

Estimated portion of purchases subject to restrictions: 22-40% of establishment costs, 22-33% of ongoing operating costs.

**Supplier approval process:** Franchisee may propose alternative suppliers by written request with financial information, references, etc. Franchisor may inspect facilities, require testing (at franchisee's expense), and must respond within 30 days. Approval may be revoked on 30 days' notice.

**Revenue disclosure:** For 2024, the franchisor entity had $0 in product sales to franchisees. Cupbop Co. (parent) had $36,920 in product sales to franchisees — surprisingly low for a system of 30 franchise units. No rebates currently, though the right is reserved.

### Training Program

The 14-day initial training program consists of 20.5 classroom hours and 95 on-the-job hours (115.5 total) at the Draper, Utah office and/or operating Cupbop store locations. Topics cover orientation and culture, administrative procedures (permits, payroll, royalty payments), marketing, POS systems, food safety and recipes, and opening/closing procedures.

Two people are trained at no additional cost; additional attendees cost $1,500 each ($2,000 for expelled trainees re-attending). Franchisees bear all travel and living expenses.

Lead trainer Gina Silveira has only 3 months of Cupbop experience (though 13 years of restaurant industry experience). JongBin Lim has 4 years with Cupbop and 21 years in the industry.

Failure to complete training is a noncurable default under the Franchise Agreement.

### Technology Stack and Costs

- **POS system:** Required, franchisor-specified. Purchase cost: $2,500-$5,000. Annual maintenance: $7,200-$12,000.
- **Accounting software:** QuickBooks Online (franchisor-designated), $200/year current.
- **Internet:** High-speed required at all times. Franchisor has unlimited access to POS data.
- **Security codes:** All must be shared with franchisor.
- **Computer system support fee:** Not currently assessed but reserved for future.
- **Upgrade obligation:** Must upgrade/update to meet changing franchisor specifications at your own cost.

### Marketing Fund Spending and Audit Status

For 2024, the advertising fund spending breakdown was:
- 31% marketing production
- 29% marketing placement
- **40% administrative expenses**

The 40% administrative allocation is notable. The advertising fund is not independently audited. Franchisees may request an annual unaudited statement in writing. The franchisor may use fund contributions to pay salaries of personnel managing the fund, overhead, travel expenses, and other costs "reasonably related to" fund administration.

Company-owned restaurants contribute at the same rate as franchisees. The fund is not used to solicit new franchise sales (though the franchise system website may reference franchise availability).

## D. Territory, Competition, Channels, Encroachment

### Protected Territory Definition

Each restaurant receives a protected territory around its authorized location. The size and scope vary based on population density, demographics, and other local characteristics. **No minimum territory size is guaranteed.** The franchisor determines the territory on a case-by-case basis.

Within the protected territory, the franchisor will not establish or license another Cupbop restaurant during the franchise term. Catering services are permitted within the protected territory and approved adjacent areas.

### Encroachment and Carve-Outs (HIGH RISK)

The protected territory has substantial carve-outs:

1. **Non-Traditional Venues:** The franchisor expressly reserves the right to operate or franchise Cupbop locations at stadiums, arenas, airports, hospitals, universities, military bases, department stores, corporate campuses, convenience stores, and other locations "where the owner or manager sets financial or experiential standards for operators for which you do not qualify" — BOTH INSIDE AND OUTSIDE your protected territory, without compensation. Cupbop Co. already operates concessions at Vivint Smart Home Arena, Rice-Eccles Stadium, and LaVell Edwards Stadium.

2. **Online delivery:** Orders placed by customers within your protected territory may be fulfilled by the franchisor, affiliates, or another franchisee, as determined by the approved third-party delivery service.

3. **Alternative channels:** The franchisor reserves all rights to internet, catalog, wholesale, supermarket, telemarketing, mail order, direct sales, and similar channels without compensation.

4. **Future brands:** Neither the franchisor nor affiliates currently plan to operate under another trademark offering similar services, but this right is explicitly reserved.

### Minimum Sales Performance

Beginning in Year 2 of operation, franchisees must maintain at least 50% of the trailing twelve-month sales average of all national Cupbop restaurants. Failure to meet this minimum over a 2-year period constitutes a default, giving the franchisor the option to terminate. This is one of the three state-required special risk disclosures.

### Channel Restrictions

Franchisees may ONLY offer Cupbop products and services at their authorized location. No other channels of distribution are available without prior written approval.

## E. Contract Burden and Legal Mechanics

### Term and Renewal

The initial franchise term is 10 years from the date the restaurant opens for business. One 10-year successor term is available, subject to conditions:
- Written notice 180-220 days before expiration
- Maintain possession of premises
- Remodel to then-current standards
- Pay $10,000 successor franchise fee
- Sign a new franchise agreement **that may be materially different from the current agreement**

The "materially different" successor agreement is a significant risk factor. At renewal, the franchisor could impose higher royalty rates, different territory terms, or other changed provisions.

### Transfer Provisions

Transfers require prior written consent (not to be unreasonably withheld) and payment of a $10,000 transfer fee. The transferee must: assume all obligations, sign the then-current franchise agreement, complete required training, and acknowledge receipt of the FDD. The franchisee and all owners must execute a general release.

The franchisor has a right of first refusal to match any third-party offer for the franchise business or ownership interest.

No transfer fee applies if the transferee is an entity the franchisee controls.

### Termination and Death/Disability

**Termination by franchisee:** Only if the franchisor breaches and fails to cure, or as allowed by state law.

**Termination by franchisor with cause — cure periods:**
- 3 days: health, safety, or sanitation violations
- 10 days: monetary defaults, insurance lapses
- 30 days: monetary defaults to third parties, curable operational defaults
- 90 days: relocation if loss of premises

**Noncurable defaults (immediate termination):** Include failure to find a site within 120 days, failure to open within 16 months, opening without approval, training failure, abandonment, unapproved transfers, material misrepresentations, felony conviction, understating Gross Sales, repeated defaults, bankruptcy, and cross-defaults with other franchisor/affiliate agreements.

**Death/disability:** Franchise may continue if a qualified representative is appointed within 180 days, or the franchise may be transferred per the transfer provisions.

### Noncompete Restrictions

**During term:** Cannot own an interest in, perform services for, or divert business to any competing business. Cannot interfere with franchisor's or other franchisees' employees.

**Post-term:** For 2 years after termination or expiration, cannot have direct or indirect ownership in or perform services for a competitive business at the premises or within 20 miles of ANY Cupbop restaurant (existing, operating, or under construction at the time of termination). This is a broad geographic restriction that expands as the system grows.

**State overrides:** Multiple states (Minnesota, North Dakota, Washington, etc.) limit or void noncompete provisions. Washington specifically voids noncompetes for employees earning under $100K and contractors under $250K.

### Dispute Resolution

All disputes are governed by Utah law and must be mediated in Salt Lake County, Utah before a single mediator. If mediation fails, binding arbitration in Salt Lake County (for ADA disputes) or litigation in Salt Lake County courts. This is one of the three state-required special risk disclosures.

State addenda override venue and choice-of-law provisions for 13 states. Illinois requires IL courts and IL law. Minnesota prohibits requiring litigation outside the state or waiver of jury trial. Washington requires arbitration in WA or by mutual agreement.

State-specific addenda exist for 13 states: California, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin.

### Material Overrides by State

**Illinois:** Litigation in IL courts, IL law applies. Waivers of IL Franchise Disclosure Act rights are void.

**Michigan:** Prohibitions on: restricting franchisee associations, requiring waivers of rights, termination without good cause, refusal to renew without fair compensation, requiring out-of-state arbitration/litigation, refusal to permit transfer without good cause.

Indemnification limited (no obligation to indemnify for franchisor's negligence/fraud). Transfer fees limited to actual costs. Inter-franchisee hiring restrictions void. Treble damages available.

## J. Unresolveds

1. **Nevada franchise closures (HIGH):** 3 of 5 franchised units in Nevada ceased operations in 2024. No explanation in the FDD. Was this a market failure, site issue, franchisee financial distress, or other cause? This should be investigated.

2. **Franchisor capitalization (MEDIUM):** Member's equity of $21,352 after $1.9M in distributions. If the franchisor faces unexpected costs, lawsuits, or needs to fund system-level obligations, the entity has minimal financial cushion. The franchisor relies entirely on ongoing cash flow.

3. **Trainer experience (LOW):** Lead trainer Gina Silveira has only 3 months of Cupbop experience. While she has 13 years in the restaurant industry, the brevity of brand-specific experience is unusual for a franchise system's lead training role.

4. **Confidentiality clause scope (MEDIUM):** The FDD states franchisees signed agreements "restricting their ability to speak openly about their experience." The exact scope and enforceability of these restrictions is unclear. A prospective franchisee should attempt to contact current and former franchisees and assess whether they are willing to speak despite these agreements.

5. **Related-party product pricing (LOW):** The franchisor and Cupbop Co. acknowledge they "may profit from the sale and distribution of items" to franchisees. The current revenue from product sales to franchisees was only $36,920 in 2024, but this could increase as the system grows or as product requirements expand.

6. **Item 20 off-by-one (LOW):** 2023 system total start (44) does not match 2022 end (45). Immaterial but unexplained.

## K. Contradictions

1. **Insurance estimate vs. requirements:** Item 7 estimates insurance at $400-$600, while Item 8 requires $2M general liability, $2M aggregate, business interruption (12 months), workers' comp, and employer's liability at $1M. The $400-$600 estimate appears to cover only initial premium deposits, not annual cost. This could significantly understate the true initial investment for insurance.

2. **Revenue from franchisee purchases:** Item 8 states "We and our affiliates will make a profit on such products or services" and "We reserve the right to require that you purchase from us," but revenue data shows only $36,920 from Cupbop Co. product sales and $0 from the franchisor in 2024. The actual profit from required purchases may be embedded in product pricing rather than separately disclosed.

3. **SG&A figure:** The OCR-rendered income statement showed SG&A as $902,734, but arithmetic cross-validation confirmed the actual figure is $992,734. This was an OCR artifact, not a document contradiction. Resolution: corrected via Retry R1.
